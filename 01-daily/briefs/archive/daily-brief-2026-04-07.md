## High Impact News

### Automotive Tariff Costs Hit $35.4B — Iran Conflict Compounds Supply Chain Pain
**Relevance:** As an EA at an auto glass company, input cost volatility (aluminium, glass) and vehicle production slowdowns directly affect both your suppliers and the volume of vehicles needing windscreen services.

U.S. auto tariffs have now accumulated $35.4B in costs for automakers since implementation. Toyota alone faces $9.1B in tariff-related costs for FY ending March 2026. Simultaneously, the Iran conflict has driven Brent crude up 63% in March (largest monthly gain since 1988), reduced Strait of Hormuz transit 95%, tightened helium supplies (key for semiconductor fabs), and pushed aluminium prices ~10% higher. GlobalData revised its 2026 global vehicle forecast down 500,000 units to 92.4 million. Q1 U.S. new vehicle sales are expected to fall 7% YoY — partly a hangover from consumers pulling forward purchases ahead of tariffs.

**Impact Assessment:**
- **Projects Affected:** Pricing and volume planning for any glass replacement demand forecasting
- **Potential Effects:** Aluminium cost pressures could flow into vehicle repair costs; fewer new vehicles entering the fleet may modestly extend the age of the existing fleet (which actually increases repair demand over time)
- **Action Suggested:** Check whether your procurement or operations team are tracking aluminium spot pricing and what exposure your glass supply chain has

### GM Lays Off 1,300 at Factory Zero EV Plant
**Relevance:** EV production pullbacks affect the composition of the vehicle fleet, which matters for ADAS-equipped windscreen calibration demand.

GM has temporarily laid off 1,300 workers at its Factory Zero EV plant in Detroit-Hamtramck due to slowing EV demand. This continues a broader industry pattern of automakers moderating EV ramp-up pace in response to consumer hesitancy and cost pressures. The move signals that the ICE-to-EV transition will be slower and lumpier than forecast, meaning ADAS-heavy EVs (which require specialist windscreen calibration) will enter the fleet more gradually.

**Impact Assessment:**
- **Potential Effects:** Windscreen calibration volumes for EVs will grow more slowly than optimistic projections suggested; plan workforce upskilling timelines accordingly

## Strategic Developments

### Yale Economist: Most Jobs Won't Be Automated — They're Not Worth the Trouble
**Relevance:** Directly relevant to AI workforce strategy and how you frame AI adoption internally.

A Yale economist published a contrarian argument (Fortune, April 4) that AGI is unlikely to automate most jobs — not because AI can't do them, but because compute will rationally flow toward high-leverage bottleneck work (fusion energy, asteroid defence, existential risk reduction) rather than replacing the mundane majority of human tasks. This sits in productive tension with the AI transformation narrative and is worth having in your back pocket when discussing AI literacy ROI with leadership.

**Strategic Implications:**
- The case for AI investment shifts from "automate headcount" to "amplify high-leverage roles" — a stronger EA narrative
- Workforce anxiety about AI may be overblown for most operational roles; redirect energy to AI literacy rather than AI replacement planning
- Focus automation investment on genuine bottlenecks in your business, not broad displacement

### Pitt Study Maps Who Is Actually Losing Jobs to AI Right Now
**Relevance:** Grounding for real AI workforce impact vs. projection — useful when building AI literacy programmes.

A University of Pittsburgh study (published April 2, 2026) examined actual labour market data on AI-driven job displacement rather than projections. Cashiers (88% automation risk, 3.1M employed) and pattern-based production roles face the highest near-term exposure. Knowledge worker displacement is measurably slower than forecast — supporting the case that AI is more augmentation than replacement in most enterprise contexts today.

**Strategic Implications:**
- Your windscreen technicians and calibration specialists are low automation risk (high manual dexterity + judgment required)
- Enterprise architects are in the "augmentation" cohort — AI tools enhance capability rather than replace
- AI literacy investment should be framed as competitive advantage, not defensive retraining

**Sources:**
