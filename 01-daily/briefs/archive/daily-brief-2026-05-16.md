### EU AI Act AI Omnibus — Formal Political Agreement Reached
**Update:** _first covered 14 May 2026_

**Relevance:** This is the formal confirmation you need for the EU AI Act compliance workstream. The provisional agreement now has Council and Parliament sign-off — formal adoption is on track for July 2026, ahead of the August 2 high-risk AI system deadline.

On May 7, the EU Council and Parliament formally agreed on the AI Omnibus amendments. The key changes confirmed in the final text:

- **High-risk AI system (HRAIS) deadline extended** — formal extension confirmed; the August 2, 2026 standalone obligation deadline is now formally being superseded. The new deadline is 2 December 2027 for most HRAIS requirements.
- **AI system database registration re-introduced** — providers must register AI systems in the EU high-risk systems database. This was previously removed from drafts; it's back in the final text.
- **Transparency watermarking** — AI-generated content transparency solutions now due by 2 December 2026 (reduced from the previous six-month delay to three months).
- **AI-generated intimate content** — new prohibitions on "nudifier" AI systems effective 2 December 2026.
- **Machinery regulation overlap** clarified — removes a compliance ambiguity affecting industrial AI.

Formal legal-linguistic revision and official adoption in the Official Journal is expected July 2026.

**Impact Assessment:**
- **Projects Affected:** EU AI Act compliance workstream; Informatica AI Governance Catalog decision
- **Potential Effects:** The extended HRAIS deadline (to December 2027) reduces the urgency of the compliance sprint but does not remove it — if anything, it gives time to do it properly. The database registration requirement is important: the AI inventory you need to build is now a formal legal obligation, not just good governance practice. This directly strengthens the case for using Informatica's AI Governance Catalog (which already handles model registry, lineage, and policy gates) rather than building separately.
- **Action Suggested:** Update the EU AI Act compliance workstream timeline to reflect December 2027 HRAIS deadline. Confirm that the AI system database registration obligation is on the scope list for the Informatica CDGC assessment.

### Musk v. Altman — Jury Deliberating, Verdict Expected This Week

**Relevance:** An adverse verdict against OpenAI could delay or restructure its IPO plans and slow the for-profit transition. The outcome affects the competitive dynamics of every AI vendor Belron evaluates.

Closing arguments concluded Thursday May 14. The jury — which serves in an advisory capacity — began deliberations Friday and is expected to reach a verdict Monday May 18. Judge Gonzalez Rogers has indicated she will very likely follow the jury's recommendation.

The central question: did OpenAI breach fiduciary duties to its original donor base when it restructured to create a for-profit subsidiary? Musk claims he was misled about OpenAI's direction; OpenAI and Altman claim the restructuring was necessary to raise capital for the computing costs of frontier AI.

MIT Technology Review analysis from May 15 assesses the most likely outcome: the judge finds OpenAI breached fiduciary duties on at least one count while declining to order full unwinding of the public benefit corporation structure.

**Strategic Implications:**
- A ruling that OpenAI breached its mission creates governance precedent for how AI labs can restructure. It does not shut OpenAI down but adds regulatory and reputational drag on its IPO path.
- Musk's admission (week 1 testimony) that xAI distills OpenAI's models is strategically interesting — it confirms the competitive interdependence that makes this more than just a personal dispute.
- For Belron's AI vendor assessment: if OpenAI's IPO path is disrupted, its enterprise sales and product investment stability are worth monitoring.

### Mintlify Kills Seat-Based Pricing — "Agents Are the Primary User Now"

**Relevance:** The first enterprise SaaS vendor of significance to eliminate seat-based pricing in favour of outcome-based or usage-based billing. A leading indicator of how AI changes enterprise software economics.

Mintlify (developer documentation platform) announced it is eliminating seat-based pricing entirely. CEO: "Seat-based pricing has no future when agents are the primary user of software, not humans." The implication: as AI agents consume software at scale, per-seat models break — you can't charge a seat licence for every agent invocation.

This isn't just about documentation tooling. It's a signal about how enterprise software pricing is going to restructure as agentic AI becomes standard. Salesforce, ServiceNow, and SAP will all face this question.

**Strategic Implications:**
- For Belron's AI tool procurement: expect vendors to start repackaging pricing as agent adoption grows. Lock-in at favourable per-seat terms now may pay off in 2-3 years when agents drive higher consumption.
- For CCOTF platform selection: contact centre platforms (Genesys, Five9, Salesforce Agentforce) will increasingly price on outcomes (calls resolved, CSAT score) or AI usage rather than agent seats. Model the economics both ways in vendor evaluation.
- For MCP Governance: if agents become primary users of enterprise software, the API and MCP governance layer (managing what agents can call and at what rate) becomes a cost control mechanism, not just a security control.
