### Anthropic's Own Data Shows 94% of Computer/Math Tasks Are Theoretically Automatable — And Hiring Is Already Reacting
**Relevance:** This is not a think-tank forecast — it is Anthropic's analysis of millions of real Claude conversations, matched against 800 occupations. The gap between theoretical and actual automation is the most useful number here: 33% actual vs 94% theoretical means the next three years are where the impact lands, not some distant future.

Anthropic economists Maxim Massenkoff and Peter McCrory published two pieces this week (Fortune, April 7 and 9) drawing on the Anthropic Economic Index:

- **94%** of tasks in computer and math occupations are theoretically automatable by AI
- Current actual AI handling of those tasks: **~33%** — the "exposure gap" is where disruption will arrive as adoption deepens
- **Most exposed roles:** Financial and investment analysts, computer programmers (75% task coverage), customer service reps, medical record specialists, and data entry workers
- **Productivity finding:** Tasks requiring a college degree (16 years of education) were sped up by a factor of **12x** by Claude — gains are concentrated in high-skill work, not just repetitive tasks
- **Early employment signal:** Hiring of workers aged **22–25** has slowed in the most AI-exposed occupations — the first labour market echo of AI adoption, showing up in entry-level and early-career roles first
- **Augmentation vs automation split:** 52% of Claude conversations are classified as augmentation (human + AI), 45% as full automation — so the dominant current pattern is still "AI as tool," but the automation share is growing

McCrory is explicit about the tail risk: a scenario of a "Great Recession for white-collar workers" is plausible if the automation share doubles from 3% to 6% in the most exposed occupations.

**Impact Assessment:**
- **EA relevance:** The productivity multiplier data (12x on complex tasks) is the strongest available argument for why AI adoption is not optional — it's a competitive disadvantage not to. Useful for any board or leadership brief.
- **Workforce planning:** The 22–25 age group signal matters for Belron's graduate intake and early-career hiring planning. If this trend continues, it will reshape how the organisation onboards new knowledge workers.
- **Skills premium:** Workers with AI skills command **up to 56% higher wages** — relevant to internal upskilling strategy.
- **Action Suggested:** Consider whether this data warrants a short internal note to HR/People leadership — the hiring signal specifically is actionable now, not in two years.

### Meta Launches Muse Spark — First Closed-Source Frontier Model, and the End of Meta's Open-Weight-Only Strategy
**Relevance:** Meta was on your watchlist specifically as the open-source option for data-privacy-sensitive workloads (like customer photos in damage assessment). Muse Spark changes that calculus — Meta is now a closed-model competitor, and the LLaMA open-source commitment has a visible asterisk.

On 8 April 2026, Meta launched Muse Spark, the first model from Meta Superintelligence Labs under chief AI officer Alexandr Wang (hired from Scale AI in a $14.3B deal). Key facts:

- **Model type:** Multimodal (text + image input, text output), closed source — no weights download
- **Performance:** Competitive with OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google across most benchmarks, but not clearly superior to any of them
- **Modes:** Quick answers mode, deep analysis mode, and a rolling-out "Contemplating" mode for complex multi-step tasks
- **Strategic shift:** Breaks with Llama's open-weight playbook; Meta says it "hopes to open-source future versions" — notable downgrade of the commitment from "will" to "hopes"
- **Why the change:** Meta spent $72B on AI infrastructure in 2025, rising to $115–135B guided for 2026, with LLaMA 4 underperforming and Chinese open-weight models overtaking it. Zuckerberg needed a competitive frontier model fast.

**Strategic Implications:**
- Meta is no longer the reliable open-source backstop in your foundation model landscape. For any use case where data residency and no-egress matter (like customer photos), LLaMA 3.x remains the open-weight option — but the next generation may be closed or capability-limited in the open version.
- Mistral AI now becomes a more important European open-weight option if Meta retreats further from open source. Worth watching.
- Muse Spark as a damage assessment tool: multimodal, strong reasoning, benchmark-competitive — it's now a viable option alongside GPT-5.4, Claude, and Gemini. If future open-source versions retain the multimodal capability, the privacy-safe deployment path survives.
- **Watchlist update:** Meta AI entry should be updated — the open-source strategy is no longer guaranteed.

### Forrester Formalises the EA-as-Orchestrator Thesis — Four New Architect Roles Defined
**Relevance:** Forrester has now codified what the EA role looks like in the agentic AI era. This is useful directly — both as a frame for your own role evolution and as external validation to use in internal conversations about what EA should own.

Forrester's updated research on the future of the enterprise architect's job identifies a structural shift: EA moves from gatekeeper at review gates to real-time orchestrator of intelligent systems. Four specific new roles are now defined:

1. **Customer/employee-centric value mapper** — Architects connect architectural decisions to measurable business outcomes through knowledge graphs and value streams (not just IT capability maps)
2. **Digital twin strategist** — Using AI-powered simulation to rehearse architectural options and expose trade-offs before commitment
3. **Enterprise knowledge curator** — Governing semantic layers; ensuring AI outputs are grounded in trusted, governed context (RAG, GraphRAG); the person who decides what the agents can "know"
4. **Agentic governance champion** — Managing farms of AI agents: establishing guardrails, feedback loops, accountability frameworks, and human-in-the-loop thresholds

The shift Forrester describes: "EA must produce machine-readable context — policies, constraints, dependencies, and data lineage — that agents can understand and respect in real time." Static architecture documentation becomes a liability; live, queryable architectural knowledge becomes the asset.

**EA Implications:**
- Role 4 (Agentic governance champion) is the most urgent — and the most directly connected to the agent sprawl findings from last week's briefs
- Role 3 (Enterprise knowledge curator) is the most undervalued today but may be the most strategic — whoever governs what the agents know governs the quality of every agent decision
- The "digital twin" role (2) overlaps interestingly with LeanIX's trajectory as a SAP-integrated platform — worth watching whether SAP LeanIX positions itself as the digital twin substrate
- **Practical use:** These four roles are a useful framing device for a conversation with your CIO or CTO about expanding the EA mandate into agentic AI governance
