# Drip-Feed Irrigation — Back Garden Research

**Task:** [[PROJECT-OVERVIEW#Irrigation|Investigate a drip-feed irrigation system for the back garden]]
**Your starting point:** outside tap with a **dual outlet** and a **timer on each outlet**. That's the hard part already done: you have the water source and the scheduling. Everything below is what connects *downstream* of those two timers.

---

## The headline

You're well set up. Each timer outlet can drive its own independent drip **zone**, so you can split the back garden into two watering circuits with different schedules (e.g. beds on one, pots on the other). The only non-obvious "must do" is fitting a **pressure reducer** after each timer — see the warning below.

---

## ⚠️ The one thing you must not skip: pressure reduction

Mains pressure is far too high for micro-drip fittings. Running drippers straight off the tap will pop the 4mm connectors off and, importantly, **can stop the timer's own diaphragm valve opening/closing properly**. A pressure regulator is described as essential, sitting **on the outflow (downstream) side of the timer**, typically reducing to around 1.5 bar. ([Holman](https://support.holmanindustries.com.au/hc/en-au/articles/14126918332303-Pressure-Reducers-Explained), [Drip Depot](https://help.dripdepot.com/support/solutions/articles/11000044625-drip-irrigation-pressure-regulators-faq))

Order of components on each outlet: **timer → (filter) → pressure reducer → 13mm supply pipe → 4mm micro-tube + drippers.** A small inline filter before the reducer is worth adding: drippers clog easily and it's the most common failure. Some starter kits include a reducer in the tap connector; some don't. **Gardena's system requires its "Master Unit"** at the start, which is a combined filter + pressure reducer, so that box does both jobs.

---

## The three system types (mix them)

| Type | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Adjustable drippers on 4mm micro-tube** | Pots, containers, individual specimen plants | Each dripper dialled to the plant's need. Ideal for the patio pots / step-ladder display. Check as plants grow. |
| **Inline dripline** (13mm pipe with emitters pre-set every ~30cm) | Beds, borders, rows, hedges | Even coverage, lay it along the bed and forget it. The tidiest option for renovated borders. Mulch over it. |
| **Porous soaker hose** | Cheap-and-cheerful borders | Least precise, more pressure-sensitive, clogs over time. Fine as a budget option but inferior to dripline. |

A typical back garden ends up as: **inline dripline for the beds/borders (Zone A)** + **adjustable drippers for the pots (Zone B)** — which maps neatly onto your two timer outlets.

---

## Design rules of thumb (so a run actually works)

- **4mm micro-tube runs stay short.** Rule of thumb is roughly a 9m / "30ft" cap per micro-tube run and a limited number of drippers on it, or pressure drops off toward the end. ([Drip Depot micro-tubing](https://help.dripdepot.com/support/solutions/articles/11000103729-using-drip-irrigation-micro-tubing))
- **13mm pipe is the backbone.** You can run a long 13mm supply line (well over the sizes a domestic garden needs) and branch short 4mm tubes off it to each plant/pot. ([Irrigation Tutorials](https://irrigationtutorials.com/drip-irrigation-design-guidelines-basics-of-measurements-parts-and-more/))
- **Dripper flow rates** are typically 2 or 4 litres/hour (adjustable drippers vary). Drip is deliberately slow, so schedule your timers for longer, less frequent sessions (e.g. 30–60 min, a few mornings a week) rather than short bursts.
- **Efficiency payoff:** drip is cited as up to ~70% less water than a hose and far less than a sprinkler, because it delivers to the root zone with little evaporation. ([Garden Watering](https://www.gardenwatering.co.uk/blogs/drip-irrigation-systems-kits-and-accessories/best-garden-drip-irrigation-kits-uk-2026-the-specialist-roundup))

## Brands / where to buy (UK)

- **Hozelock** — the de-facto UK standard, cheapest to source (B&Q, Homebase, Amazon). Kits like the Universal/Automatic watering kit (13mm pipe, covers ~10m²) and pot kits (15/20 drippers + 4mm pipe). *Buy the kit without the timer since you already have timers.* ([Hozelock kit example](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Hozelock-Classic-Irrigation-Borders-Controller/dp/B010DT6AII))
- **Gardena Micro-Drip** — premium, modular, expandable, best build quality. Needs the **Master Unit** (filter + pressure reducer) at the head. Dearer but the nicest long-term system for a proper renovation.
- **Claber** — Italian, good value middle ground (Aqua-Flow / Oasis / Terrazzo kits for patios and beds).
- **Specialist retailers for build-your-own components** (better value than boxed kits for a whole garden): [Easy Garden Irrigation](https://www.easygardenirrigation.co.uk/collections/drip-irrigation-systems), [Garden Watering](https://www.gardenwatering.co.uk/collections/garden-irrigation-kits).
- **Budget generic kits** (MIXC / Landrip on Amazon) — cheap, lots of fittings, quality variable. ([Garden Tool Box roundup](https://www.gardentoolbox.co.uk/garden-watering-spraying/automatic-garden-watering-system/))

**Compatibility note:** stick to one brand's connector standard (or one component supplier) so the 13mm/4mm fittings all mate. Hozelock and Gardena do not always interchange.

## Practical gotchas

- **Clogging is the #1 issue** — fit the filter, and flush the line ends occasionally.
- **Winterising** — drain and disconnect before the first frost; bring the timers indoors (you likely already do this).
- **Check pot drippers periodically** — plants' needs change through the season and as they grow.
- **Mulch over dripline** in beds to cut evaporation further and hide it.

---

## Recommended approach for your setup

1. **Two zones, one per timer outlet:**
   - **Zone A — beds/borders:** inline dripline laid along the planted areas.
   - **Zone B — pots/patio containers:** 13mm supply with 4mm adjustable drippers to each pot.
2. **On each outlet:** add a filter + **pressure reducer** (~1.5 bar) before the 13mm pipe.
3. **Brand:** Hozelock if you want cheap and available now; Gardena Micro-Drip if you want the better, expandable long-term system to match a proper renovation. Claber is a fair middle.
4. **Before buying:** measure the beds/borders (total run length) and count the pots — that determines dripline length and dripper count, and which kit size to get.
