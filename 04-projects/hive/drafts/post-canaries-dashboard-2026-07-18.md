# Hive Post Draft: Stanford's Canaries Dashboard

**Community:** AI Community

## Post body

We spend a lot of time predicting how AI will reshape work. Stanford's Digital Economy Lab has started measuring it instead.

Their new **Canaries Dashboard** tracks employment across occupations by how exposed they are to generative AI, using ADP payroll data covering around **4.6 million workers** since ChatGPT launched in late 2022. It is designed as an early-warning system: the canaries in the coal mine for where AI is starting to bite.

Three things stand out:

- **It is not broad displacement (yet).** Across the whole workforce, the differences are still modest. The story is concentrated, not general.
- **Early-career workers feel it first.** Workers aged 22 to 25 in high-exposure roles like software development and customer service are already seeing noticeable employment declines, while less-exposed roles keep growing. The effect deepens for the youngest group.
- **Automation vs augmentation is the real dividing line.** Where AI *automates* the task, employment falls or flattens. Where AI *augments* the worker, the employment effect is weak to negligible. Exposure alone does not predict impact; how the AI is used does.

That last point is the one worth sitting with. The same technology can subtract a role or amplify a person, and the design choice is largely ours.

For a business like ours, that reframes the question. It is less "which jobs does AI replace?" and more "are we designing our AI to automate people out, or to make our people better at the job?" The tools we are exploring, from computer-vision damage assessment to contact-centre assistance, land very differently depending on which side of that line we build them on.

**Worth a look:** the dashboard updates monthly, so it is a live source rather than a one-off study. [Stanford Canaries Dashboard](https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/project/indicators/canaries-dashboard/)

**For discussion:** Where in your part of Belron do you see AI showing up as automation vs augmentation? And are we deliberately choosing which one we build, or letting the tool decide for us?

## Short variant (augmentation-forward, ~150 words)

**Suggested title:** The choice that decides how AI and our jobs get on

Stanford's new Canaries Dashboard is measuring AI's real effect on work, using payroll data from 4.6 million workers. The finding that matters most for us is an encouraging one: the outcome depends far less on the technology than on how we choose to use it.

Where AI is set up simply to automate a task, employment softens. But where AI is used to *augment* people, helping them do their jobs better, that effect all but disappears. Same tool, very different results, and the choice is ours to make.

That is the opportunity. The AI we are exploring at Belron can be aimed squarely at the augmentation side: giving our technicians and contact-centre teams better information, faster, so they can focus on the work only people can do.

**For discussion:** what would "AI that makes your job better" look like in your team?
