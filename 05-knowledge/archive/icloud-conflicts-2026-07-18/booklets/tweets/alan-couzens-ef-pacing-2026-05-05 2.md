# Thread: Athletes & Pace/Heart Rate — Economy of Form (E.F.)

## Summary
Alan Couzens (endurance coach) on the single most important running metric: pace at a given heart rate — what he calls Economy of Form (E.F.). The argument: 90% of aerobic progress is achieved by raising your speed at the same heart rate, not by running harder. The practical implication is to never accept a run with a poor E.F. — use a treadmill, run early in the day, or use cooling strategies to ensure quality aerobic stimulus.

## Key Points
- **Higher pace at a given heart rate = 90% of the game** — this is the metric to track and optimise over time
- **Never accept a shitty E.F.:** conditions that degrade it (heat, fatigue) mean a run is doing less aerobic work than it appears. Solutions: run early, use cooling, use a treadmill
- **The treadmill is explicitly endorsed** as a tool for controlling E.F. — directly relevant to treadmill-based training
- **"Rack up as many full, juicy heartbeats as you can"** — the goal is maximising aerobic cardiac stimulus, not miles or pace per se

## Why It Matters
Directly relevant to the running plan started today (zero to 10K on a treadmill). The E.F. framework explains why conversational pace is the right protocol for Phase 1–3: it maximises aerobic adaptation at a given heart rate. If a heart rate monitor is available, tracking pace-at-HR over weeks is a concrete measure of fitness improvement before distance becomes the metric.
