# Communities of Practice Framework

## Framework Overview
A design and diagnostic model for building sustainable Communities of Practice (CoPs) — particularly relevant to the EA practice at Belron and the architecture community. Synthesised from the CoP guidebook via NotebookLM, applied to Belron's context.

## Why This Matters for Belron

The EA function at Belron operates across ~35 countries with practitioners distributed across opcos and the central team. This is precisely the operating context where CoPs outperform formal structures:
- **Tacit knowledge** about how to apply EA in a global windscreen business is not in any process document — it lives in practitioners' heads
- **Cross-boundary problems** (CCOTF ↔ AI Damage Assessment ↔ MCP Governance) are where CoPs unlock insights that project structures miss
- **Pre-IPO context:** A functioning EA CoP that operates across Belron's estate is a governance maturity signal — it demonstrates the architecture community is self-organising and strategically connected

## Core Principles

### Principle 1: A CoP Is Defined by Domain, Not Task — This Distinction Is Everything

**Statement:** Teams are defined by tasks and disband when done. Projects have start and end dates. CoPs are defined by a knowledge domain — they exist and evolve as long as the domain matters. Confusing CoPs with teams or project groups is the most common design error and the fastest route to failure.

**The three-element diagnostic:**

| Element | Question | Weak signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Domain** | Do members share a genuine passion for the topic? | Low attendance, surface-level conversations |
| **Community** | Do members trust and learn from each other? | No informal side-conversations, high churn |
| **Practice** | Do interactions produce actionable tools, stories, methods? | Activity without output, no knowledge artefacts |

**Application for Belron EA CoP:**
- Domain ✓ — Enterprise Architecture in a complex global windscreen business is a genuine specialist domain
- Community ? — Depends on whether distributed EA practitioners already have informal relationships to build on
- Practice ? — Are there existing shared artefacts (architecture principles, reference models, standards) that the community owns and evolves?

**Confidence:** High — framework validated across many enterprise CoP implementations

---

### Principle 2: CoPs Fail When Treated as a Service to the Organisation

**Statement:** The moment a CoP is burdened with administrative tasks, reporting requirements, or delivery obligations it didn't choose, energy drains and the best contributors disengage. The community must serve its members first — strategic value to the organisation is a by-product of a community that serves its members well, not a design requirement imposed on members.

**The most common enterprise failure modes:**

| Failure Mode | Description | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Fad** | Everything gets labelled a CoP; concept diluted | Require all three elements (Domain + Community + Practice) |
| **Neglect** | Organisation doesn't engage with CoP outputs | Sponsor accountability; Value-Creation Story reporting |
| **Groupthink** | Members stop questioning each other | Deliberately invite external voices; rotate facilitators |
| **Silo** | Community becomes inward-looking | Build cross-boundary interactions into the design |
| **Burden** | CoP loaded with admin/delivery obligations | Keep CoP activity separate from project deliverables |

**Evidence:**
- [[braindump-2026-05-31-communities-of-practice-notebooklm]] — "The 'fad' risk is the most common enterprise failure mode. When leadership labels every working group a 'community of practice,' the concept loses meaning"

**Confidence:** High

### Principle 3: "News from the Future" Is the Highest-Value CoP Output — Lead with This Argument

**Statement:** At maturity, a CoP identifies emerging trends and technologies before they hit the formal strategy radar. This is the argument for sustained investment that resonates with leadership — not learning efficiency, not HR development, but strategic intelligence that arrives months earlier than formal scanning processes.

**Value chain to communicate to sponsors:**
```
Immediate insight → Applied practice change → Realised business result
```
(Not: meeting attendance → member satisfaction → "learning culture")

**Belron application:** An EA CoP that operates across 35 countries will surface:
- What's working in opco X that opco Y hasn't tried
- Emerging technology patterns (AI, MCP, agentic workflows) interpreted through the Belron lens before they hit formal strategy cycles
- Inconsistencies in the IT estate that represent IPO due-diligence risk — surfaced by practitioners, not by auditors

**Evidence:**
- [[braindump-2026-05-31-communities-of-practice-notebooklm]] — "At full maturity, a CoP identifies emerging trends and technologies before they hit the formal strategy radar. This is the argument for sustained investment — not learning efficiency, but strategic intelligence."

**Confidence:** High on the argument; Medium on timeline to reach this maturity at Belron

### Principle 4: Sponsorship Legitimises Without Controlling

**Statement:** The right sponsor provides resources, ensures the community's voice reaches strategic conversations, and then gets out of the way. Micromanaging sponsors kill CoPs faster than neglect. Three levels of sponsorship are needed.

| Sponsor Level | Role |
|---|---|
| **Executive** | Legitimises the domain in the organisation's strategic vocabulary; opens doors to resources |
| **Domain** | Connects CoP outputs to relevant business capability or programme decisions |
| **Local** | Enables participation — protects practitioners' time and signals that CoP activity is valued work |

**For the Belron EA CoP:**
- Executive sponsor: CTO or CIO (signals EA CoP is strategic, not just a training programme)
- Domain sponsor: Lead EA (natural fit — already has cross-cutting authority)
- Local sponsors: Functional EAs and opco architecture leads (protect practitioner time)

---

### Principle 5: Launch Strategy Matters — the "Non-Launch" Is Often Better

**Statement:** Formal launch events with fanfare generate attendance but not community. A better starting approach is the "non-launch" — start with a specific, real problem that a group of practitioners want to solve together. Community forms around the problem; structure follows from value.

**Non-launch sequence for EA CoP at Belron:**
1. Identify one specific, live challenge where distributed EA expertise would help (e.g. "How should we apply BCM to a new opco?")
2. Convene 4–6 practitioners across Belron for a case clinic or problem-solving session
3. Capture the output as a reusable artefact (a BCM template, a reference architecture, a decision tree)
4. Use the artefact as evidence of value when pitching the CoP concept formally

## The Community Lifecycle

| Phase | Description | Right intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Potential** | Discovery — practitioners recognising shared interest | Facilitate introductions, surface the domain |
| **Coalescing** | Finding value — first interactions, testing the water | Low-stakes early wins, case clinics |
| **Establishing** | Organising — rhythm, roles, charter emerging | Create structure without killing informality |
| **Committing** | Building identity — members own the community | Formalise sponsor relationship, expand scope |
| **Evolving** | Stewardship — community shapes the domain itself | Cross-boundary engagement, strategic reporting |

**Belron EA CoP likely position:** Potential/Coalescing — practitioners exist but no formal community yet. The architecture community proposal (EA/SA/TA operating model) is a catalyst that could accelerate movement to Coalescing.

## Boundaries & Limitations

**This framework works when:**
- Practitioners genuinely share a knowledge domain they care about (not assembled for organisational convenience)
- Participation is voluntary or semi-voluntary (not mandated attendance)
- There is at least one active convener/coordinator with time to run it

**Does NOT work when:**
- CoP is created to fulfil a reporting requirement rather than a practitioner need
- Leadership insists on deliverables and timelines that turn the CoP into a project
- The domain is so narrow that fewer than 6–8 people globally could participate meaningfully
