# Pattern: The EA Artefact as Instrument

## Pattern Description
Every significant EA artefact produced during the week of 6–9 April 2026 was framed — either explicitly or implicitly — as serving two purposes simultaneously: solving a thinking problem AND creating a reason for stakeholder engagement. This dual-purpose design is the distinguishing feature of high-impact EA work vs. work that is technically correct but never adopted.

**Frequency:** Present in 6 out of 7 substantive professional braindumps this week
**Domains:** Professional (EA effectiveness)
**Significance:** This pattern, once consciously applied, changes how artefacts are designed — the audience question ("who would find this valuable and why?") becomes as important as the content question ("what does this show?")

## Analysis

**What Triggers This Pattern:**
- Any time an EA artefact is being designed or planned
- When facing the tension between "doing the work" and "being visible"
- When a stakeholder engagement feels contrived or forced — the artefact removes the awkwardness

**What Follows This Pattern:**
- Artefacts get designed with a clearer audience in mind from the start
- The production process itself generates stakeholder conversations (filling gaps = asking people)
- Stakeholder engagement feels purposeful rather than performative — there is always something concrete to share or validate

**Underlying Mechanism:**
EA practitioners who struggle with visibility often treat work and visibility as separate activities — you do the work, then separately figure out how to communicate it. The Artefact as Instrument pattern collapses these into one: the artefact IS the communication vehicle, designed from the outset to open conversations.

---

## How to Apply

When designing any EA artefact, answer three questions before starting:
1. **What thinking problem does this solve?** (The content rationale)
2. **Who would find this valuable to see?** (The audience)
3. **What format makes it most shareable and discussable?** (Not: most technically complete)

The third question is often neglected. A one-page diagram that sparks a 30-minute conversation with a senior stakeholder is worth more than a 40-page document that no one reads.
