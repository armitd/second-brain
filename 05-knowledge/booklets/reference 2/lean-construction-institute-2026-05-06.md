# Lean Construction Institute (LCI)

## Quick Summary

The Lean Construction Institute promotes lean principles applied to the built environment — architecture, engineering, and construction. Its core mission: eliminate waste and rework in design and construction projects. The flagship methodology is the **Last Planner System (LPS)**, developed by Glenn Ballard and Greg Howell in the late 1990s, which restructures how construction schedules are planned and committed to by the people actually doing the work.

## Key Insights

- **Last Planner System is the core framework:** LPS inverts traditional top-down scheduling. Instead of a project manager issuing plans that field teams follow, the "last planners" — those closest to the work (trade foremen, discipline leads) — design work sequences and make reliable commitments. Planning happens in greater detail the closer it is to execution, not months in advance.

- **Three pillars of LPS:** (1) Regular coordination meetings with the people executing the work; (2) explicit commitments from last planners on specific tasks and deadlines; (3) visible performance metrics tracking whether commitments are kept (Percent Plan Complete, PPC). The metric drives accountability and surfaces systemic obstacles early.

- **Lean Construction vs. Lean Startup:** Both draw from Toyota Production System / lean manufacturing principles, but Lean Construction focuses on *flow* and *commitment reliability* in project delivery — not Build-Measure-Learn cycles. The key shared concept is waste elimination, but Lean Construction's "waste" is physical site waste, rework, and waiting time; Lean Startup's "waste" is building features that don't generate validated learning.

- **Integrated Project Delivery (IPD):** LCI also champions IPD — a contract structure where owner, designer, and contractor share risk and reward, incentivising collaboration rather than adversarial relationships. More radical than lean scheduling alone.

## Why It Matters

The lean manufacturing → Lean Construction lineage is the same source river as Lean Startup (both trace back to Toyota). Understanding the Lean Construction branch is useful context for evaluating how lean principles have been applied in capital-intensive, long-cycle industries — which is closer to Belron's physical operations (vehicle glass repair/replace, workshop scheduling, technician dispatch) than software product development.

The Last Planner System's commitment-reliability model could be a useful reference when thinking about how Belron manages field service scheduling or AI-assisted job planning — the concept of "only commit to what you can reliably deliver" is directly applicable to service dispatch and capacity planning.
