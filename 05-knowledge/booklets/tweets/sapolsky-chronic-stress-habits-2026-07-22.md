# Thread: Sapolsky — 10 habits that keep you in chronic stress

## Summary
A thread summarising Stanford neuroscientist Robert Sapolsky (on Chris Williamson's podcast) on how "normal" daily habits keep the nervous system stuck in survival mode. The core idea: these aren't 10 separate problems but symptoms of one root — a stress response evolved for 30-second predator threats now running for years. (Note: the original thread is heavily wrapped in workshop-signup CTAs, stripped out here.)

## The 10 habits
1. **Replaying conversations** — the brain can't tell it isn't happening now; same cortisol surge. Notice, name, redirect.
2. **Worrying about scenarios that haven't happened** — the zebra reacts to the lion for 30 seconds; humans worry for 30 years. Ask "is this happening right now?"
3. **Doomscrolling news** — activates 150-million-year-old stress circuitry. One news window per day.
4. **Bingeing tragic/violent content** — the nervous system can't tell fiction from reality. Audit what you consume after 7pm.
5. **Trying to control the uncontrollable** — both lack of control *and* trying to control uncontrollable things spike cortisol. Surrender one thing per day.
6. **Skipping social connection when "too busy"** — weak social support is a key stress amplifier (baboons with strong bonds lived longer). Schedule connection like a meeting.
7. **Saving stress relief for the weekend** — it has to be daily; 20 min daily beats 3 hours on Saturday.
8. **Forcing a stress practice you hate** — if meditation stresses you, it's the wrong tool. Try walks, music, hobbies.
9. **Chasing the next dopamine hit** — dopamine is about anticipation, not reward; yesterday's high feels insufficient today. Notice when chasing; pause.
10. **Staying in draining environments** — low-rank baboons in unstable troops had higher cortisol and shorter telomeres even when safe. Audit who/what drains you.
