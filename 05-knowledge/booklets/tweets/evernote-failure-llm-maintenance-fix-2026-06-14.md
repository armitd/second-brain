# Thread: The PKM Industry Solved the Wrong Problem

**Author:** Aakash Gupta (@aakashgupta)

## The Core Argument

> "Evernote had 250 million users in 2018. They sold for $185 million in 2023. That's 74 cents per user."

Notion, Roam, Obsidian, Tiago Forte — "every single one of these solved the wrong problem. They all made it easier to *capture* information."

The real failure: maintenance burden compounds faster than value. Clip 200 articles, tag meticulously for three months, life gets busy — the whole thing rots into a guilt archive you never open again.

## Karpathy's One-Sentence Fix

> "Humans abandon wikis because the maintenance burden grows faster than the value. LLMs don't get bored."

That's the entire product insight the note-taking industry missed for 15 years.

- The problem was never capture
- The problem was never organisation
- The problem was never search
- **The problem was that a human had to maintain the system**

## Why the LLM Solves This

The LLM does exactly the work humans refuse to do:
- Update cross-references when new information arrives
- Flag contradictions between sources
- Maintain an index across 100+ pages
- Keep doing it on day 200 with the same quality as day 1

> "Evernote spent a decade adding features. The answer was removing the human from maintenance entirely."

## Why This Matters

**For COG positioning:** This is the cleanest articulation of *why COG works where Evernote didn't*. The process-readwise skill, the weekly check-in, the braindump-to-booklet pipeline — all of these are LLM-maintained workflows that would have collapsed under human maintenance load within months.

**For Belron AI advocacy:** The Evernote example is concrete and boardroom-legible. Belron's knowledge management problem — operational knowledge rotting in SharePoint, process docs never updated, tribal knowledge lost when people leave — is the same pattern at enterprise scale. The argument: "Evernote failed because humans can't maintain systems long-term. LLMs can. That's not a feature difference — it's the thing that makes AI-assisted knowledge management finally viable."
