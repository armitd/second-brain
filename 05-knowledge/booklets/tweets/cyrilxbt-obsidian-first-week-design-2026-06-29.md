# Thread: Obsidian System Design for Survival Past Week One

## Summary
CyrilXBT's structural analysis of why most Obsidian setups die in week two — not due to lack of discipline but due to design failures — and a 5-step build sequence that solves all four failure patterns. The central argument: a system that asks the least of you exactly when you have the least to give is the one that survives.

## The Four Failure Patterns
1. **Too many decisions at capture time** — friction compounds when you're busiest, which is exactly when you most need it to work
2. **No immediate payoff** — without a visible near-term return in week one, there's no reason to keep showing up
3. **Maintenance debt that compounds silently** — nobody notices the decline until the vault feels unusable
4. **Structure copied from someone else** — their workflow, their job; forces your thinking into the wrong shape

## The Five Design Principles
1. **Zero-friction capture:** Single inbox, no tagging decisions, no folder choice at capture. One global hotkey to today's inbox. The only decision is whether to write something down.
2. **5-minute evening processing pass:** One decision per capture — task, permanent note, reference, or delete. The deletion option matters: "a system that treats every capture as sacred accumulates noise fast."
3. **Build payoff inside week one:** Friday recap Dataview query to see the week's captures in one place. Name the first time the vault saves something you'd have forgotten — write it down. Make real value visible before novelty wears off.
4. **Sustainable maintenance budget:** 5–10 minutes daily, 20 minutes weekly. If a workflow regularly exceeds budget, simplify the workflow, not find more time. One-tag-system: max 5 tags, add a sixth only after genuinely needing it 3+ times.
5. **Structure built from real behavior:** Do a 3-day behavior audit — capture everything in one file, no organizing. Read back and notice what you actually captured. Build folders around those real patterns, not aspirational ones.

## Best Quote
*"The system that gets used after week one isn't the most impressive one. It's the one that asked the least of you exactly when you had the least to give."*
