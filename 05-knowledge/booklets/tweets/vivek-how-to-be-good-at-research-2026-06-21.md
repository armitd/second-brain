# Thread: How to Be Good at Research

## Summary

A long-form thread on the actual craft of research — not the visible outputs (papers, threads, announcements) but the underlying skills. Framed around ML research but generalises broadly. Heavy Hamming, Feynman, Karpathy references.

## Key Points

- **Pick your own problems:** Most researchers absorb problems from advisors or trending topics, not choose them. Schulman's advice: set an outcome you genuinely want to exist, reason backwards to experiments. Taste (judging what matters) is a muscle — practice by predicting experimental results before running them.

- **Upgrade your inputs:** Shared reading lists produce shared ideas. Old material is criminally underpriced (mixture of experts: 1991; LSTM: 1997; Bitter Lesson: 2019). Read the paper itself, not the thread summarising it. "The appendix is where the bodies are buried."

- **Write everything down:** An idea feels fully formed until you try to write it. Darwin wrote down any fact that cut against his theory on the spot — because memory deletes inconvenient evidence. Keep a log: hypothesis, setup, expectation, result, updated belief. Then put some of it in public. "A body of public writing is an unfakeable sample of how you think."

- **Tighten the loop:** Alec Radford = volume. More runs per day, more wrong ideas discarded per week. Research speed is mostly the speed at which you discover you're wrong. Karpathy's rule: overfit a single batch before training at scale. "Thirty seconds, half your bugs, gone."

- **Stare at the outputs:** A descending loss curve is reassurance, not analysis. Pull 100 failures, read them all, sort into piles, attack the biggest pile. "One transcript of genuinely strange behaviour will teach you more than the next decimal of accuracy ever will."

- **Wander on purpose:** Your first subfield is an accident of timing. Spend real time in multiple areas before deciding where you live. Ablate until you know which component carries the result — it's usually one, and usually not the one in the title.

- **Long game:** "Knowledge and productivity compound like interest. The daily edges look trivial in isolation. What you read, what you record, how fast your loop runs, who you argue with. Give them a few years and they produce careers that look like luck from the outside."

## Why It Matters

Useful reference for any analytical/research practice — EA work, PoC design, architecture review. The Karpathy "overfit a single batch" principle translates directly to the AIDA PoC: test on a tiny dataset first, get the inference pipeline right, then scale to the full benchmark set. The "stare at the outputs" principle (read 100 failures before changing the model) applies to any evaluation methodology.
