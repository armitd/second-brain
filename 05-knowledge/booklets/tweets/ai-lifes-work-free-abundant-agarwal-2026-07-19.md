# Essay: When Your Life's Work Becomes Free and Abundant

## Summary
A personal essay by Aditya Agarwal (ex-Dropbox CTO, early Facebook engineer) on watching coding — his life's craft — become "free and abundant" via AI, and what that reveals about knowledge work. The core claim: the trait that now matters most is not intelligence, credentials, or experience but someone's *relationship with change* — whether they run toward it.

## Key Points
- After a weekend coding with Claude: "We will never write code by hand again." In the five days after, he produced more (and more ambitious) code than in the previous five years — constraints he'd accepted his whole career simply dissolved.
- Holds "wonder and grief in the same hand" — rejects both the doomer ("all replaced") and booster ("everything's fine") camps as missing what the shift actually feels like.
- **Hiring signal from ~20 work trials: zero correlation between years of experience and AI-tool adaptability.** What predicted success was a "builder's disposition" (side projects, love of making); FAANG résumé + brand university predicted "almost nothing."
- One firm set coding tasks intentionally too long to do by hand — a clean filter for who actually uses AI daily. The output gap wasn't 10%, it was ~10x.
- **The dividing line is dispositional, not generational:** 15-year veterans crushing it, some recent grads treating AI as an abstraction to debate. "Résumé vs restlessness — bet on restlessness."

## Why It Matters
A credible, first-person complement to the [[stanford-canaries-dashboard-2026-07-19|Stanford Canaries Dashboard]] on future of work: the Canaries data shows *where* AI bites; this essay argues *who* thrives (adaptability over credentials). Strong material for internal AI-literacy and change-management messaging — the "adaptability is the new currency, and unlike a degree it's available to everyone" framing is both reassuring and mobilising for a workforce. **Candidate Hive post** for the AI Community (future of work / how to thrive with AI).
