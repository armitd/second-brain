# Thread: AI Absorbed the Process Layer — David Byttow

## Summary
Byttow argues that agents are collapsing the coordination layer of organisations. For decades, engineering leadership was built around synchronisation — tickets, standups, dependency graphs, sprint planning. Agents now decompose, sequence, and execute. The scaffolding that existed to keep humans aligned is being made redundant, and what remains is judgment.

## Key Points
- **Coordination was the bottleneck, not execution.** The real cost of software delivery was keeping humans synchronised — status syncs, handoff docs, meetings about alignment. Agents carry zero coordination cost
- **Process people are now redundant.** If your value was assigning tickets, facilitating standups, or tracking burndown charts, that work is already being absorbed
- **The new core skill is goal clarity.** AI output quality correlates almost perfectly with the clarity of the goal, not the implementation steps. "If you cannot articulate the goal clearly enough for an AI agent to act on it, you could not articulate it clearly enough for your team either. AI just makes the gap visible."
- **Leverage inverts.** One sharp operator orchestrating a fleet of agents can cover surface area that previously required 5-10 people. Power moves from span of control to strength of judgment
- **Bad judgment scales faster.** The natural checkpoints where bad ideas died (engineer pushback in sprint planning, PM asking "are we sure?") were features of the coordination layer. Remove them and mediocre judgment causes damage at a rate previously impossible
- **"AI does not eliminate organisations. It removes excuses."** The buffer between the quality of your judgment and its consequences is gone

## Why It Matters
Directly relevant to Belron's EA agenda and the Contact Centre of the Future project. The question "do we have someone who actually owns this and knows what good looks like?" is the right filter for any agentic AI initiative. Also a useful frame for internal conversations about AI governance and org design.
