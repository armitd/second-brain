# Thread: Is the UK's EV Target About to Fall to Pieces?

## Summary
A transport-policy thread arguing the UK's EV transition has plateaued not on price or range but on **charging access** — specifically the gap between home charging (for those with driveways) and expensive public charging (for those without). Makes the case that "generation 2" EV policy must solve kerbside charging, with London as the test bed.

## Key Points
- EV sales boomed to ~25% market share, then plateaued; the motor industry is laying off workers and closing plants. Carmakers are fined £18,000 per off-target vehicle but the public isn't obliged to buy.
- **The real blocker is charging economics:** home charging ~7p/kWh (~2p/mile) vs ultra-rapid public ~75p/kWh (pricier than petrol) — a 6-10x fuel-cost gap. You only get the cheap rate with a driveway.
- Across **58 polling subgroups, 57 gave the same answer: it's about charging**, the thing with least policy attention.
- In inner London, **4 in 5 homes have no off-street parking** (1.8m households). Public charge-point locations don't match where transport workers live. Even Uber's £20k+ EV incentives aren't converting drivers.
- **Gen 1 policy** (cut purchase price, tackle range anxiety, help home charging) got EVs from <1% to 25%. **Gen 2 policy** must tackle the harder "average car / no driveway" case — kerbside charging, urban access.

## Why It Matters
Directly relevant to the automotive-industry watch: EV adoption dynamics shape the vehicle parc Belron services (windscreen repair/replace on EVs, ADAS calibration on newer vehicles). The charging-access ceiling and the industry slowdown are macro signals worth tracking. Also a candidate Hive item for the General Innovation community (automotive industry shift).
