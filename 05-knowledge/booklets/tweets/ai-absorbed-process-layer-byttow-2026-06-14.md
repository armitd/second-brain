# Thread: AI Absorbed the Process Layer

**Author:** David Byttow (@davidbyttow)

## Summary
Agents are dissolving the coordination layer of organisations. Sprint planning, dependency graphs, ticket grooming, status syncs — all of this was load-bearing when coordination was expensive. Now coordination cost is approaching zero for anyone using agents seriously. What remains is judgment. The essay is a warning and a reframe: AI doesn't eliminate organisations — it removes excuses and the buffer between judgment quality and its consequences.

## Core Argument

### The coordination layer was always the overhead, not the value
> "The bottleneck was coordination overhead: ticket grooming, status syncs, handoff docs, meetings about alignment, dashboards tracking dashboards. The real cost was keeping humans synchronized."

Organisations optimised around process because execution cost was low and misalignment cost was high. Now execution cost is collapsing and coordination cost is approaching zero.

### What's left is judgment
> "AI will not replace managers. It will expose the ones whose contribution was procedural rather than directional."

If your value was assigning tickets, facilitating standups, tracking burndown charts, or managing dependency spreadsheets — that work is being absorbed. If you equated control with contribution, the leverage just disappeared.

### The core skill shifts to goal clarity
> "The quality of AI output correlates almost perfectly with the clarity of the goal, not the implementation steps, not the task breakdown."

> "**If you cannot articulate the goal clearly enough for an AI agent to act on it, you could not articulate it clearly enough for your team either. AI just makes the gap visible.**"

The unit of leverage shifts from team size to individual clarity. Span of control → strength of judgment.

### The darker side: bad judgment scales faster
> "When execution becomes cheap, a founder with a wrong mental model of their customer ships the wrong product in a week instead of a quarter. The feedback loops that used to exist — the engineer pushing back during sprint planning, the PM asking 'are we sure about this?' in a grooming session — those were features of the coordination layer, not bugs."

> "**A brilliant operator with sharp instincts becomes terrifyingly effective. A mediocre one causes damage at a rate that was previously impossible.**"

The failure mode of the agentic era: "we built it perfectly and it didn't matter."

### The conclusion
> "AI does not eliminate organizations. It removes excuses. And it removes the buffer that once existed between the quality of your judgment and the consequences of acting on it."

## Why It Matters for Belron EA
This framing is directly relevant to two things:
1. **Belron AI advocacy:** The pitch is not "AI saves time" — it's "AI makes the gap between good judgment and bad judgment visible at speed." The EA role is inherently judgment-heavy. This is the argument for investing in people who can define goals clearly, not people who manage process.
2. **MCP Governance:** If agents are handling coordination, the governance question shifts from "who approves this task?" to "who owns the goal?" The MCP Governance framework may need to account for goal specification as the critical governance layer, not task approval.
