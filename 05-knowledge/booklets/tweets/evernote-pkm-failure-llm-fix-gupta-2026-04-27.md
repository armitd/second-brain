# Thread: Why PKM Tools Failed — LLMs Remove the Maintenance Burden

## Summary
Aakash Gupta uses Evernote's collapse (250M users in 2018, sold for $185M) to diagnose why all PKM tools ultimately fail. They solved the wrong problem: capture and organisation. The real problem is maintenance burden — keeping a knowledge base current requires ongoing human effort that people eventually abandon. Karpathy's key insight: "LLMs don't get bored." LLMs can handle maintenance entirely, removing the human from the loop for the most tedious and failure-prone part of PKM.

## Key Points
- **Evernote's failure wasn't features:** it was that the maintenance burden of a large knowledge base eventually overwhelms users
- **All PKM tools solve the wrong problem:** they optimise capture and organisation, but maintenance is the failure mode
- **Karpathy's framing:** the LLM wiki pattern works because LLMs don't experience the boredom and friction that cause human PKM systems to rot
- **LLMs as maintenance layer:** the right architecture delegates maintenance (summarising, linking, pruning, retrieving) to LLMs, not humans
- **Design implication:** a well-structured LLM-managed knowledge base outlasts any human-managed one

## Why It Matters
The theoretical foundation behind COG and the Karpathy LLM wiki pattern. Useful context when explaining why COG is structured the way it is — and a reminder not to over-engineer human-facing organisation at the expense of LLM-readable structure.
