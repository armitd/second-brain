# Enshittification — Cory Doctorow

## What It's About
Doctorow's analysis of how digital platforms systematically decay: first they serve users, then they extract value from users to serve business customers, then they extract from everyone to serve shareholders. Combines economics (switching costs, collective action problems) with IP law critique to explain why companies can enshittify and survive.

## Key Highlights
- **The platform = middleman trap**: "Intermediaries are part of the solution to connecting people — but they become part of the problem when they grow so powerful that they can act as gatekeepers who can usurp the relationship between the two sides of their markets."
- **Amazon search is broken by design**: Top Amazon results are on average 29% more expensive than the best match. The best match is 17 places down. $38B/year in paid placement corrupts the index.
- **Too big to jail → too big to care**: "Once a company is too big to fail, it becomes too big to jail, and then it becomes too big to care. Antitrust law was designed to fight that apathy."
- **Switching costs as the trap mechanism**: Everything you give up when you leave a platform — data, network, integrations — is the mechanism that allows enshittification to proceed unchallenged.
- **IP law weaponised against users**: DMCA + DRM + smart devices = manufacturers can make it *illegal* to use your own device in ways that benefit you over them. Applies from insulin pumps to tractors.
- **Criti-hype**: Credulous amplification of tech companies' claimed mind-control capabilities (Skinnerian behaviour modification) that helps them sell ads by lending legitimacy to the fear. Even sceptical coverage can be criti-hype.
- **The real enshittification formula**: "Combine the banality of evil with an internet-connected device and a federal law that criminalises doing anything with that device that the manufacturer dislikes."

## Why It Matters
Directly relevant to any vendor selection decision at Belron — especially for AI platforms, SaaS contracts, and proprietary tools with deep integration. Switching costs are the mechanism by which today's good vendor becomes tomorrow's extractive one. The MCP Governance project's vendor-agnostic architecture stance is a direct counterplay to this.

Also useful as a framework for explaining to Belron leadership why open standards (MCP, open-weight models) matter strategically: they reduce the switching costs that enable enshittification.
