# A Guide to Open Innovation and Crowdsourcing — Paul Sloane

## What It's About
A practitioner guide to open innovation and crowdsourcing: how organisations tap external and internal crowds to generate, refine, and fund ideas. Directly relevant to the Hive innovation platform as a conceptual backbone for how to run community-sourced innovation well.

## Key Highlights
- **Jeff Howe's crowdsourcing taxonomy — four categories:**
  1. **Collective intelligence / crowd wisdom** — gather a crowd and create conditions to share knowledge (employee suggestion boxes, IBM's global "idea jams", InnoCentive's expert problem-solving, von Hippel's lead-user innovation). *The category Hive most naturally sits in.*
  2. **Crowd creation** — the crowd co-creates the product/service; work broken into small pieces done in "spare cycles" (open-source model, Mechanical Turk, Threadless).
  3. **Crowd voting** — the crowd's judgement organises/filters large volumes of submissions (prediction markets, HP internal sales prediction; Google search as popularity-based crowd voting).
  4. **Crowdfunding** — micro-contributions pooled to fund efforts.
- The design choices — *how you gather the crowd, what you ask them to do, and how you ask* — determine what you get out of it.

## Why It Matters
The taxonomy is a ready-made framing for the [[04-projects/hive/PROJECT-OVERVIEW|Hive]] curation channel: Hive is primarily a *collective-intelligence / crowd-wisdom* play (surfacing ideas and external signals across Belron's communities), with *crowd voting* as the natural next layer (letting communities upvote candidates). Useful vocabulary when positioning what Hive is for and how to structure engagement.
