# Sharing Knowledge in Communities of Practice — Robert Sheldon

## Summary
Overview of Wenger's communities of practice (CoP) framework. A CoP requires three elements: Domain (shared interest), Community (members who interact and learn from each other), and Practice (shared repertoire of tools, stories, and approaches). All three must develop in parallel — a community lacking any one element is not a true CoP.

## Key Highlights
- **Domain:** Shared interest that members are committed to — defines the community's identity
- **Community:** Active relationships and joint learning — not just shared job titles or affiliation
- **Practice:** Shared repertoire of tools, stories, and ways of addressing recurring problems — develops through sustained interaction
- "It is the combination of these three elements that constitutes a community of practice"

## Why It Matters
EA at Belron could benefit from a formal CoP structure — particularly during the transition period. The CoP framework gives language and structure to what EA needs to build: a domain (architecture principles), a community (EA practitioners + engaged stakeholders), and a practice (shared tools, patterns, decision frameworks).
