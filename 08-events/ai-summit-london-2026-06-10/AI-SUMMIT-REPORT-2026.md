## Executive Summary

The AI Summit London 2026 was dominated by two interlocking themes: **governance** and **the gap between AI ambition and real production impact**. The consistent message across sessions was that the hard part of enterprise AI is not the model — it's the data, the operating model, and the governance framework. AI pilots are failing to scale not because the AI doesn't work, but because the organisations aren't ready.

For Belron, three things landed across the two days:
1. The **PoC-to-production problem** is universal — and the organisations solving it have clean data, clear ownership, and governance embedded from day one
2. **AI governance is lagging deployment** — agentic systems are being deployed faster than the controls to manage them, which is exactly the MCP Governance problem
3. **Data quality is the #1 AI failure mode** — not model performance; this directly validates the training data strategy concern for the Damage Assessment PoC

## Key Themes

### 1. Why AI Pilots Fail to Reach Production

The most consistent finding across the summit: pilots fail at the organisational level, not the technical level.

**What the summit said:**
- Successful scaling requires clean, well-structured data, unified infrastructure, and integrated workflows designed for AI from the start
- Pilots fail due to: fragmented infrastructure, misaligned priorities, unclear ownership, siloed processes, and lack of operational planning
- Organisations focusing solely on technical accuracy without ensuring business alignment undermine scalability
- Recommendations: start with well-defined use cases, adopt agile methodologies, rapidly evaluate pilot viability, embed governance-ready systems from the start

**Belron implication:** The AI Damage Assessment PoC is at the exact inflection point this theme describes. The technical question (can AI assess windscreen damage from a photo?) is largely answered — 95%+ accuracy is achievable off the shelf. The harder question is: does Belron have the data governance, ownership, and operating model to scale it? The summit says that's the question that kills pilots, not the AI.

---

### 2. AI Governance Is Not Keeping Pace With Deployment

**What the summit said:**
- Traditional governance — static, reactive — is inadequate for generative AI and agentic systems
- Shadow AI, overprivileged access, and vendor lock-in are the recurring failure modes
- Without dynamic, real-time governance mechanisms, risks related to bias, compliance failures, and security vulnerabilities persist
- Effective practices: embedding legal, risk, and compliance teams into development processes; modular architectures; runtime guardrails; agent management systems
- Governance must be tiered: low-risk applications → streamlined oversight; high-risk → stringent controls, continuous monitoring, human intervention

**Belron implication:** This is the exact problem the MCP Governance framework is architecting. The summit's consensus validates the approach: tiered governance, tool-level controls, real-time enforcement. The Noma / Linx / Microsoft Agent 365 commercial products exist precisely because this gap is now enterprise-scale. The question for Belron is whether to buy or build the control layer.

---

### 3. AI Agents Breaking Organisations — What Actually Fails

**What the summit said:**
- When AI agents make real decisions, the failure modes are: misaligned workflows, undefined ownership, absence of contextual parameters, overprivileged agents, credential sprawl, adversarial input vulnerabilities
- Cultural resistance and erosion of trust are as much a barrier as technical failure
- Employees resist systems they perceive as opaque — particularly when AI disrupts established processes
- Solutions: modular, role-specific agents with tightly defined guardrails; human-in-the-loop mechanisms; observability; upskilling

**Belron implication:** The "what breaks when agents make real decisions" analysis is a direct input to the MCP Governance framework. The failure modes described — credential sprawl, overprivileged agents, opaque decisions — are exactly what MCP governance is designed to prevent. The "governance by architecture" framing from the summit is particularly useful: design the agent's constraints into its architecture, don't rely on post-hoc policy enforcement.

---

### 4. Enterprise AI Failure Is Primarily a Data Problem

**What the summit said:**
- Enterprise AI underperformance is predominantly attributed to data challenges, not model deficiencies
- Recurring issues: poor data quality, fragmentation, integration gaps, data drift, incomplete lineage, training-serving skew
- Smaller, purpose-built models often outperform general models when data is well-curated
- Danger of over-reliance on perfect data as a prerequisite — paralysis through perfectionism is also a failure mode
- Recommendations: semantic layers, unified data infrastructures, proactive monitoring, knowledge graphs

**Belron implication:** The Damage Assessment PoC training data strategy is more important than model selection. The summit consensus — data problems kill AI, not model limitations — validates the emphasis on Encord/Scale AI for annotation quality. The "purpose-built smaller model" point is also relevant: a fine-tuned windscreen damage classifier may outperform GPT-4o zero-shot for the specific repair/replace decision.

---

### 5. Human-AI Dynamic — Augment, Don't Replace

**What the summit said:**
- General consensus: AI should augment rather than replace human capabilities
- Human oversight remains essential in high-stakes, accountability-driven tasks
- Training employees and fostering AI literacy emerged as critical enablers
- Panellists cautioned against "cognitive surrender" — over-reliance on AI for decisions that require human judgement
- Entry-level role disruption is a real concern; organisations must design inclusive adoption pathways

**Belron implication:** The contact centre framing for CCOTF — human agents assisted by AI, not AI replacing agents — is validated by the summit consensus. The "cognitive surrender" risk is relevant to any AI-assisted damage assessment: the technician reviewing an AI recommendation must genuinely engage with it, not rubber-stamp it.

## Strategic Takeaways for Belron

### Immediate applications

1. **The PoC-to-production checklist** from the "Why Pilots Fail" analysis should be applied to the Damage Assessment PoC right now — before the pilot completes, not after. The blockers (data governance, ownership clarity, operating model) take longer to solve than the model.

2. **"Governance by architecture"** — the summit's recommended pattern for agentic systems — should become a design principle in the MCP Governance framework. Don't enforce governance through policy after the fact; build it into how agents are structured.

3. **The Andrade vendor co-responsibility principle** — any vendor delivering the Damage Assessment PoC model must accept co-responsibility for production outcomes, not just model accuracy. Build this into the vendor evaluation criteria.

4. **Data > model** — if the summit consensus is right (and it is), the training data strategy for damage assessment is more important than which foundation model to use. Prioritise data quality, annotation, and pipeline over model benchmarking.

### Questions the summit raised for Belron
- Does Belron have a defined "AI governance owner" — or is AI risk treated as someone else's technical problem (the Gallagher failure mode)?
- Which Belron AI initiatives are at risk of the "cognitive surrender" pattern — where human oversight exists on paper but not in practice?
- Does Belron have a unified data infrastructure that would support production AI, or is the data architecture still fragmented by opco?
