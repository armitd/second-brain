# Braindump: Garden Planting — Shrubs in Front of Photinia

## Raw Thoughts
Think about planting for the garden. Specifically some green shrubs to go in front of the photinia.

---

## Context

**Photinia** (likely Photinia x fraseri 'Red Robin') is a popular evergreen shrub with distinctive red new growth that matures to glossy dark green. It typically grows 2–4m tall and works well as a backdrop or hedge. The challenge: what to plant in front of it that complements rather than competes?

**Design brief (inferred):**
- Evergreen green shrubs — foliage-focused, not competing with photinia's red new growth
- Front-of-border position — so lower/medium height, ideally 0.5–1.5m
- Should provide year-round structure and texture interest

---

## Shrub Options to Consider

### Strong Candidates

**1. Pittosporum tenuifolium (varieties)**
- Height: 0.5–2m depending on variety; many compact cultivars
- Foliage: Wavy-edged, glossy dark green or variegated; small and textural
- Why it works: Evergreen, low maintenance, contrasts beautifully with photinia's bold red/green; compact varieties like 'Tom Thumb' (purple-black) or 'Golf Ball' (bright green, rounded) stay tidy without pruning
- Sun/shade: Full sun to partial shade ✓

**2. Choisya ternata (Mexican Orange Blossom)**
- Height: 1–1.5m
- Foliage: Glossy, aromatic, bright green — dense and rounded
- Why it works: Structural rounded shape contrasts with photinia's upright habit; fragrant white flowers in spring and often again in autumn; evergreen ✓
- Variety to consider: 'Sundance' (yellow-green) or straight species for pure green
- Sun/shade: Full sun to partial shade ✓

**3. Sarcococca (Sweet Box)**
- Height: 0.5–1m
- Foliage: Small, glossy, dark green — very neat and dense
- Why it works: Slow-growing and low maintenance; intensely fragrant white flowers in winter (a gap when photinia is less interesting); tolerates shade well
- Good for: Underplanting or edging in front of larger shrubs ✓

**4. Viburnum davidii**
- Height: 0.8–1.2m
- Foliage: Large, deeply veined, dark green — bold and architectural
- Why it works: Strong textural contrast to photinia's finer leaves; produces turquoise-blue berries (if male and female planted together); evergreen ✓
- Sun/shade: Full sun to partial shade ✓

**5. Osmanthus x burkwoodii**
- Height: 1–2m (slow-growing, can be kept compact)
- Foliage: Small, dark green, slightly toothed — similar feel to box but more disease-resistant
- Why it works: Dense, formal structure; fragrant white flowers in spring; a good box substitute ✓
- Sun/shade: Full sun to partial shade ✓

**6. Loropetalum chinense (Chinese Fringe Flower)**
- Height: 1–2m
- Foliage: Dark green to burgundy — interesting texture; fringe-like pink flowers
- Why it works: Related to photinia; the burgundy-leaved varieties add depth without competing
- Note: Less commonly available; check local garden centre

### Worth Considering if More Ground Cover Needed

**Euonymus fortunei (varieties)**
- Low spreading habit, 0.3–0.5m
- Varieties like 'Emerald Gaiety' (green/white) or 'Emerald 'n' Gold' (green/yellow) for edging
- Very low maintenance, evergreen ✓

**Skimmia japonica**
- 0.5–1m, glossy green, red berries in winter — good seasonal interest when photinia is quiet

---

## Design Notes

- **Height graduation:** Photinia at the back (tall) → medium shrubs (Choisya, Viburnum davidii) in the middle → lower edging (Sarcococca, Euonymus) at the front
- **Texture contrast:** Photinia has bold, glossy oval leaves — best offset by smaller-leaved shrubs (Pittosporum, Osmanthus, Sarcococca) or strongly veined/architectural ones (Viburnum davidii)
- **Avoid:** Other red-tipped shrubs (e.g. Pieris) — too similar to photinia and competing rather than complementing
- **Year-round interest:** Combine a winter-fragrant plant (Sarcococca) with a spring-flowering one (Choisya, Osmanthus) for staggered interest
