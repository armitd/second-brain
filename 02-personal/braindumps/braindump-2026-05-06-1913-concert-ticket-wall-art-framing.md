# Braindump: Concert Ticket Wall Art — Framing & UV Protection Research

## Raw Thoughts
Creating a piece of wall art from old concert tickets from the 80s and 90s.

Research captured on IKEA frame UV protection:

Neither the IKEA RIBBA nor SANNAHED frames are marketed by IKEA as having dedicated UV-filtering or conservation-grade glazing.

The current SANNAHED range uses standard "front protection in plastic", specifically polystyrene plastic. IKEA does not state any UV-blocking percentage or archival/conservation properties.

Historically, newer RIBBA frames also moved from glass to plastic/acrylic-style fronts in many sizes, but again without UV specifications. Photography/framing discussions note that IKEA's standard glazing is not considered conservation-grade UV protection.

**Important nuance:**
- Standard acrylic/plastic does block some UV naturally.
- But museum/conservation glazing usually blocks 92–99% UV and explicitly states this.
- If a frame doesn't advertise UV protection, framers generally assume it is not suitable for protecting valuable prints, photographs, signed posters, watercolours, or textiles long-term.

**Practical use-case assessment:**

| Use case | IKEA RIBBA / SANNAHED OK? |
|---|---|
| Casual posters / inexpensive prints | Yes |
| Art in low-light rooms | Mostly fine |
| Sunlit rooms | Risk of fading over time |
| Valuable photography / art prints | Not ideal |
| Archival or collector display | No |

**Good hybrid approach:**
- Buy the IKEA frames for the aesthetics/value.
- Replace the front with UV-filtering acrylic such as:
  - Acrylite OP-3
  - Tru Vue Conservation Clear
  - Plexiglas Gallery
  - Perspex UV100

Many people do exactly this because IKEA frame bodies are good value. Some UK sellers even make replacement acrylic inserts specifically for RIBBA/SANNAHED sizes.

**One subtle point most people miss:**
Even perfect UV glazing does not fully stop fading. Visible light itself also causes degradation. So the biggest protection factor is still:
- avoiding direct sun,
- lower lux levels,
- and rotating displayed artwork periodically.

For expensive giclée prints or photography, positioning matters more than the frame brand.

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights

1. **The tickets are irreplaceable — treat them as archival items, not casual prints.** 80s/90s concert tickets on original stock will fade, yellow, and become brittle over time. Any display solution should account for this; the UV glazing upgrade is worth the small extra cost given the sentimental value.

2. **The hybrid IKEA + UV acrylic approach is the practical sweet spot.** IKEA frame bodies are well-regarded for value; the glazing is the weak link. Swapping in Tru Vue Conservation Clear or Perspex UV100 in RIBBA/SANNAHED sizes is a well-documented approach in framing communities and costs relatively little.

3. **Positioning beats glazing as the primary preservation lever.** Even perfect UV acrylic won't fully protect against fading if the display is in a bright, sunlit room. The most important decision is *where* this goes — a hallway or living room wall away from direct sun is ideal.

4. **Scan before displaying.** Even with UV glazing, displayed originals will degrade over decades. High-resolution scanning of each ticket before framing creates an archival backup and, optionally, a set of reproduction prints that can be displayed while originals are stored flat in acid-free sleeves.
