# Braindump: Garden Lighting — Redo Cabling & Garage Lights

## Raw Thoughts
Sort out the garden lighting - redo the cabling. Maybe some lights on the garage.

## Content Analysis

### Main Themes
1. **Cabling Overhaul:** Existing garden lighting cabling needs replacing or re-routing — suggests current setup is old, poorly laid, or unreliable
2. **Garage Lighting:** New idea to add lights to the garage — either security, ambience, or practical task lighting
3. **Garden Lighting Refresh:** Broader intent to improve the overall garden lighting scheme, not just a quick fix

### Supporting Ideas
- "Redo the cabling" implies the current wiring is the root problem — worn conduit, surface-run cables, or a layout that no longer suits the garden
- Garage lights could be: security floodlights (PIR-triggered), soffit/fascia downlights, wall-mounted lanterns, or feature lighting
- This braindump follows naturally from the pergola/rockery planning — good lighting design should account for any new structures before cables are relaid
- Garden lighting circuits are typically low-voltage (12V transformer) or mains (230V armoured cable) — choice affects cable spec and install complexity
- In the UK, outdoor mains electrical work typically needs a Part P-certified electrician unless it's a like-for-like replacement

### Questions Raised
- What is wrong with the current cabling — physically damaged, poorly routed, not enough sockets/outputs?
- Is the current system low-voltage (transformer-fed) or mains?
- How many lighting zones/areas are planned — path lights, feature/uplights, pergola area, garage?
- Is the garage detached or attached to the house?
- Are garage lights for security (PIR floodlight), ambience, or both?
- Is there already a power feed to the garage, or does that need running from the house?
- Should the new cabling be armoured SWA cable in conduit for longevity, or low-voltage with a central transformer?

### Decisions Contemplated
- **Mains vs. low-voltage system:** Low-voltage is safer for DIY; mains gives more power and flexibility but needs an electrician
- **DIY vs. electrician:** If mains cabling is involved, a certified electrician is the right call — and cable route planning should happen before the pergola and rockery go in
- **Garage light type:** Security floodlight (PIR) vs. decorative wall lantern vs. soffit downlights — different mounting, different cable requirements

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights
1. **Do the cabling before the landscaping:** This is the most critical sequencing point. If pergola footings and rockery are going in (per the earlier braindump), the lighting cable routes must be planned and ideally laid first — digging up new rockery to run a cable later would be costly and frustrating
2. **One job, one groundwork session:** Pergola footings + new armoured cable runs + any conduit can all be dug in the same groundwork phase — saves significant effort and cost
3. **Garage power feed is the unknown variable:** If the garage doesn't already have a power feed, running one is the biggest job in this whole plan — but also unlocks future flexibility (EV charger, workshop power, etc.)
