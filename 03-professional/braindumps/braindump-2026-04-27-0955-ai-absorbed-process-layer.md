# Braindump: AI Absorbed the Process Layer — David Byttow

## Raw Thoughts

David Byttow's essay. The central claim: agents are collapsing the coordination layer of organisations. For decades, engineering leadership was built around synchronisation — breaking work into tasks, assigning owners, mapping dependencies, running planning cycles, tracking progress. An enormous bureaucratic scaffolding to keep humans aligned. That scaffolding was load-bearing when coordination was expensive. Now it isn't.

Agents decompose, sequence, and execute. What used to require sprint planning, dependency graphs, and a project manager now happens in a tight conversational loop.

**"People whose focus is on process are now redundant."**

The bottleneck was never writing code — it was coordination overhead: ticket grooming, status syncs, handoff docs, meetings about alignment, dashboards tracking dashboards. The real cost was keeping humans synchronised.

When you automate coordination, what remains is judgment. Taste. Direction. Ownership. The ability to define a goal clearly enough that autonomous systems can act on it without constant correction.

**"AI will not replace managers. It will expose the ones whose contribution was procedural rather than directional."**

The core skill is now goal clarity. The quality of AI output correlates almost perfectly with the clarity of the goal — not the implementation steps, not the task breakdown. If you cannot articulate the goal clearly enough for an AI agent to act on it, you could not articulate it clearly enough for your team either. AI just makes the gap visible.

**The darker side:** when execution becomes cheap, bad judgment scales faster. The feedback loops that used to exist — the engineer pushing back in sprint planning, the PM asking "are we sure about this?" — were features of the coordination layer, not bugs. They created natural checkpoints where bad ideas could die. Strip those away and you have whatever judgment sits at the centre, running at full speed with no friction.

**"A brilliant operator with sharp instincts becomes terrifyingly effective. A mediocre one causes damage at a rate that was previously impossible."**

Strategic mistakes no longer announce themselves through slow delivery. They announce through fast delivery of the wrong thing.

## Content Analysis

### Main Themes
1. **Coordination collapse:** The process layer — planning, alignment, dependency management — is being absorbed by agents. It was always a cost, never a value. Now the cost is gone.
2. **Judgment as the new leverage point:** What remains after process is automated is the quality of human judgment. Goal clarity, taste, ownership — these are the scarce resources now.
3. **Accountability without buffer:** Removing the coordination layer removes the friction that protected against bad judgment. Fast execution of a wrong goal is worse than slow execution.

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights

1. **This is the most direct description of why EA needs to reposition now.** The value of EA has often been framed around process — architecture reviews, governance gates, standards bodies. If process is being absorbed by agents, that framing becomes a liability. The repositioning must be toward judgment: EA as the function that provides goal clarity and architectural intent, not process compliance.

2. **"AI makes the gap visible" is the precise mechanism of the transition window.** If Belron's EA function has been providing process value (coordination, compliance), agents will soon make that value redundant and make the gap visible. The transition window isn't just about the Head of EA retiring — it's about whether EA repositions before agents commoditise its current value proposition.

3. **The darker side is an EA governance argument.** "Fast delivery of the wrong thing" is exactly the failure mode that good architectural judgment prevents. As agents accelerate execution, the cost of building in the wrong direction increases. EA's role shifts from slowing down bad decisions (governance as friction) to improving the quality of goals before execution starts (architecture as intent-setting). This is a stronger position.

4. **"One sharp operator orchestrating a fleet of agents" is the individual model.** For Armo personally: the opportunity isn't to do more of what EA has been doing faster — it's to define what Belron's AI goals are clearly enough that agents can execute them well. The leverage point is architectural clarity, not architectural process.
