# Braindump: From Passive to Active — Stakeholder Visibility & Routines

## Raw Thoughts
I need to think about how to be more active at work - I'm being too passive. I need routines to help manage stakeholders and make sure I'm visible in the workplace.

---

## Content Analysis

### Main Themes
1. **Passivity pattern:** There is a recognised gap between current behaviour (reactive, waiting) and desired behaviour (proactive, initiating)
2. **Stakeholder management:** Relationships with key stakeholders are not being actively cultivated — they are being left to chance or event-driven contact only
3. **Visibility:** Presence and impact are not being adequately communicated or demonstrated to the people who matter
4. **Routine as solution:** The instinct is right — structured habits are the fix for passive defaults, not willpower alone

### Supporting Ideas
- Enterprise architects are particularly vulnerable to invisibility — the work is often abstract, behind-the-scenes, and hard to attribute. Without proactive communication, the value is invisible even when the work is good
- Passive behaviour in EA roles often stems from waiting for problems to come to you rather than seeking out the problems before they surface
- Visibility and stakeholder management are distinct problems: visibility is about presence and reputation; stakeholder management is about influence and trust

### Questions Raised
- Who are the 5–7 stakeholders that matter most right now? Are they mapped?
- What does "visible" actually look like at your company — is it meetings, written comms, hallway conversations, all-hands contributions?
- What triggers the passive mode — is it workload, unclear mandate, confidence, or something else?
- What do stakeholders currently know about what you're working on and why it matters?

### Decisions Contemplated
- Move from reactive to proactive stakeholder contact — how often, what format?
- Build a structured routine (weekly, fortnightly?) for stakeholder check-ins
- Define what "visibility" looks like practically — outputs, artefacts, presentations, briefings?

---

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights
1. **The EA visibility problem is structural, not personal.** Enterprise architects are easy to overlook because the discipline sits between business and technology — owning neither fully. Proactive visibility is a professional survival skill in this role, not a nice-to-have.
2. **Routine beats resolve.** A passive default won't be fixed by deciding to be more active — it requires a calendar-anchored system that forces proactive contact before it feels necessary.
3. **Stakeholder management and visibility reinforce each other.** Being visible to the right people *is* stakeholder management. Separate them conceptually but run them together in practice.

### Pattern Recognition
- **Recurring pattern:** The self-awareness to diagnose passivity is strong — the gap is in converting that awareness into a system. This braindump is itself a proactive act; the challenge is building the cadence that makes this the norm.
- **EA role context:** Yesterday's brief noted that EA in 2026 is shifting from "documentation" to "governing autonomy and shaping decisions." That shift requires influence — which requires visible, active relationships with the people making decisions.

### Strategic Implications
- Armo's impact as an EA is limited not by capability but by how well that capability is known and trusted by stakeholders
- A structured stakeholder rhythm would compound over time: each touchpoint builds the relationship; each relationship amplifies the next initiative
- Visibility creates optionality — when decisions are being made, you want to be in the room or at least in the mind of the person making them

## A Starting System (Draft Routines)

### Weekly Cadence
- [ ] **Monday — Stakeholder radar check (15 min):** Review your stakeholder list. Who haven't you spoken to this week? Any upcoming decisions they're facing where your input would be useful?
- [ ] **Friday — Brief wins summary (10 min):** Draft 2–3 bullet points of what EA contributed this week. Send to one relevant stakeholder or manager. Keeps you visible without being noisy.

### Fortnightly Cadence
- [ ] **Stakeholder coffee rotation:** Identify 5–7 key stakeholders. Book a 20-min informal catch-up with one per fortnight on a rotating basis. No agenda — relationship maintenance.
- [ ] **Architecture health snapshot:** Prepare a one-page view of current EA focus areas, decisions in flight, and risks on the horizon. Share with your manager or sponsor.

### Monthly Cadence
- [ ] **Stakeholder map review:** Update who the key influencers and decision-makers are. Roles change, priorities shift — your stakeholder map should too.
- [ ] **EA impact narrative:** One paragraph or slide on what architecture has enabled, unblocked, or prevented this month. Useful for performance cycles and for keeping leadership informed.
