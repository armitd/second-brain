# Braindump: Architecture Guide — Scope and Engagement Model

## Raw Thoughts

I need to update the architecture guide to explain to people the areas that I cover, plus how they can engage with architecture.

---

## Content Analysis

### Main Themes
1. **Defining the EA scope:** Making it explicit what areas of the business architecture covers — so people know what to bring to you
2. **Engagement model:** How people should engage with architecture — when, how, through what channels, what to expect in return
3. **Internal communication:** The guide as a visibility and trust-building tool for the EA function
4. **Discoverability:** People can only engage with architecture if they know it exists and what it does

### Supporting Ideas
- The two problems this guide solves: (1) "I didn't know architecture covered that" and (2) "I didn't know how to get architecture involved"
- Scope definition prevents both under-engagement (people not involving EA when they should) and over-engagement (people expecting EA to do things outside its remit)
- The engagement model needs to cover: when to engage (triggers), how to engage (channels/process), what to expect (outputs, turnaround, involvement level)
- Different roles engage with architecture differently — a project manager needs different guidance than a senior leader or a developer
- The guide should probably live somewhere central and discoverable — intranet, wiki, or a shared document — not buried in a SharePoint folder
- Tone matters: the guide should feel like an invitation, not a gate

### Questions Raised
- Does an architecture guide already exist in some form, or is this a greenfield document?
- Who is the primary audience — all staff, project teams, leadership, IT?
- What are the most common scenarios where people *should* engage architecture but currently don't?
- What channels exist today for architecture engagement (email, Teams channel, formal request form, etc.)?
- What are the outputs/deliverables people can expect from architecture involvement?

### Decisions Contemplated
- Format: one-pager vs. multi-section guide vs. wiki page
- Whether to include a visual (e.g. a simple diagram of EA domains or an engagement flowchart)
- How formal vs. approachable the tone should be
- Whether to publish immediately or validate with a few stakeholders first

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights
1. **An architecture guide is a marketing document as much as a reference document.** Its job is to make people *want* to engage, not just tell them how. Tone and clarity are as important as completeness.
2. **"Areas I cover" should be expressed in business language, not EA framework language.** Instead of "application architecture, data architecture, technology architecture," think "systems and integrations," "data and reporting," "technology decisions and standards."
3. **The engagement model is the highest-value section.** Most people already vaguely know EA exists — what they don't know is *when* and *how* to engage. A clear, low-friction engagement path removes the biggest barrier.
4. **Scenarios beat abstractions.** Include 3–5 concrete examples of when to engage: "Starting a new system purchase? Talk to architecture first." "Redesigning a key process? Architecture can map the impacts." These land immediately.
5. **The guide implicitly sets expectations both ways.** It tells people what architecture will do for them — but it also implicitly commits the EA function to being available, responsive, and useful. Make sure what's in the guide is deliverable.

### Pattern Recognition
- **Connection to BCM braindump:** Both tasks are about making EA tangible and accessible to a non-EA audience. The architecture guide is the "front door" of EA; BCM materials explain one of the key tools inside the door.
- **Recurring theme:** The EA communication challenge — abstract discipline, business-critical value. Both braindumps this session are about closing that gap.

### Strategic Implications
- A well-written architecture guide reduces the "shadow IT" problem — people making technology and process decisions without EA involvement because they didn't know to involve it
- Positions EA proactively as a partner, not a reviewer who shows up late to critique decisions
- Creates a reference point for escalation: "As per the architecture engagement model, this decision should have come through architecture"
- Can be used in onboarding new staff and new project kick-offs

## Suggested Guide Structure

### Section 1: What Is Architecture?
- One paragraph, plain English, no framework jargon
- Anchor to the business: "Architecture helps [Company] make better decisions about our systems, data, and technology — so we build the right things, avoid duplication, and reduce risk."

### Section 2: What Areas Does Architecture Cover?
Expressed in business-friendly language, e.g.:

| Area | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Systems & Integrations | Which systems we use, how they connect, and decisions about buying or building new ones |
| Data & Reporting | How data flows through the business, where it lives, and how it's used |
| Technology Standards | The tools, platforms, and technologies we use and how we govern new additions |
| Business Capabilities | Understanding what the business does and how technology supports it |
| Solution Design | Shaping how new projects and changes are structured technically |

### Section 3: When Should You Engage Architecture?
Trigger-based, scenario-driven — e.g.:
- You're evaluating or purchasing a new system or tool
- A project is changing how two or more systems interact
- You're designing a new process that involves data or technology
- You're unsure whether a technology decision aligns with company direction
- You have a strategic initiative that needs a technology roadmap

### Section 4: How to Engage
- **Channel:** [Teams channel / email / request form — to be confirmed]
- **What to include:** Brief description of what you're working on and what you need
- **What to expect:** [Initial response within X days; involvement options from light-touch review to full engagement]
- **Who to contact:** [Name / role]

### Section 5: What Architecture Will Do
- Review and provide input on technology and design decisions
- Flag risks and dependencies you might not be aware of
- Connect your work to the broader technology and business landscape
- Help document decisions for future reference
