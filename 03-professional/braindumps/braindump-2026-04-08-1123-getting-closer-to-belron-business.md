# Braindump: Getting Closer to the Belron Business

## Raw Thoughts
How do I get closer to the Belron business. I need to learn more about how it all actually works.

---

## Content Analysis

### Main Themes
1. **Business proximity gap:** There is a recognised distance between current understanding of the business and the depth needed to do EA work that truly serves it
2. **Learning intent:** This is an active desire to understand operations, not just strategy — "how it all actually works" signals interest in the ground-level reality
3. **EA effectiveness:** Enterprise architecture that isn't grounded in deep business understanding produces solutions that don't fit — this is the right instinct to have

### Questions Raised
- What does Belron's operating model actually look like — how do opcos (Autoglass, Carglass, Safelite, etc.) relate to the centre?
- Where does value get created in the business — at the technician level? In the booking funnel? In insurance partnerships?
- Who are the people inside Belron who understand the business best — and how do you get time with them?
- What information already exists (intranet, strategy decks, exec presentations) that you haven't read yet?
- Is there a way to spend time in operations — ride-alongs with technicians, time in a fitting centre, sitting with the contact centre?

### Supporting Ideas
- Enterprise architects who understand the business deeply make better decisions and build more trust with stakeholders — this is directly connected to the visibility problem identified this morning
- Belron is a global business (Autoglass UK, Carglass Europe, Safelite USA, and others) operating through opcos under a shared centre — understanding that model is foundational for EA work
- The gap between "corporate EA" and "the business that EA serves" is one of the most common failure modes in the discipline

## Strategic Intelligence

### Key Insights

1. **Business acumen is a compounding asset.** Every hour spent understanding how Belron actually operates — the booking flow, the technician dispatch model, the insurance claim process, the ADAS calibration workflow — makes every future EA decision sharper. This investment pays dividends for years.

2. **The best way in is through people, not documents.** Strategy decks and intranet pages give you the sanitised version. The real picture comes from sitting with a contact centre agent, riding with a technician, or asking an operations manager what keeps them up at night.

3. **This connects directly to the stakeholder visibility work.** Getting closer to the business *is* stakeholder management. The people who understand operations are exactly the stakeholders who should be on your regular rotation.

### Strategic Implications
- An EA who is seen to genuinely understand the business — who can talk fluently about booking conversion, technician utilisation, or insurance partner dynamics — is taken seriously in a way that a purely technical architect is not
- Belron's opco model means there are multiple layers to understand: the global centre, the opco (e.g. Autoglass UK), and the local operations. Knowing which level a problem lives at is half the architectural challenge
- Time spent in the field (contact centre, fitting centre, technician ride-along) would be unusual for someone in your role — which makes it more memorable, not less
