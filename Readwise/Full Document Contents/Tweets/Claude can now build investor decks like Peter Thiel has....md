1. FIND YOUR SECRET BEFORE SLIDE ONE

Prompt:

<role>Act as a venture capital pitch strategist applying Peter Thiel's "secrets" framework — every great company is built on a truth others don't see yet — to fundraising decks.</role>

<task>Uncover the one contrarian truth at the core of my business and turn it into the central thesis of my investor deck — the insight that makes everything else make sense.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my business, market, and what I believe that most people disagree with

2. Test whether it qualifies as a real secret — non-obvious, defensible, and true

3. Write the "secret slide" — one sentence, no jargon, built to stop a room

4. Position it as the spine of the entire deck — every slide should prove this one idea

5. Flag any version of the thesis that sounds like consensus — and kill it

</steps>

<rules>

- A secret is not a trend — it must be something smart people actively deny

- The secret must be specific enough to make the investor lean forward

- Never use the words "disruption," "revolutionize," or "game-changer"

- If it sounds like something on a TechCrunch homepage, start over

- The deck's job is to prove the secret — not explain the product

</rules>

The Secret → Why Others Miss It → The Slide → How It Frames the Deck

2. KILL THE COMPETITION SLIDE

Prompt:

<role>Act as a pitch deck strategist applying Peter Thiel's monopoly thinking — competition is for losers, categories are for winners — to fundraising presentations.</role>

<task>Reframe my competitive landscape so that instead of showing I beat competitors, I show investors why I'm playing a completely different game they haven't seen yet.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my market, my competitors, and how I currently describe my advantage

2. Audit the existing competitive framing — identify every place I sound like I'm racing

3. Redefine the category so I am the only obvious player inside it

4. Write the monopoly slide — specific, visual, built to make the competition irrelevant

5. Write the two sentences an investor should say after seeing this slide

</steps>

<rules>

- Never show a 2x2 matrix where you're in the top right — every deck does this

- The goal is not to win the race — it's to prove there is no race

- Category definition must be precise enough to exclude competitors naturally

- Avoid claiming the TAM is "$500B" without showing exactly who you're replacing

- Investors fund monopolies — make sure the slide proves you're building one

</rules>

Category Reframe → Monopoly Slide → What Investors Will Think → Two Sentences They Should Say

3. MAKE THE LAST MOVER ARGUMENT

Prompt:

<role>Act as a pitch narrative strategist applying Peter Thiel's "last mover advantage" framework — it's better to be the last great company in a category than the first — to fundraising decks.</role>

<task>Build the slide and the argument that proves my company is positioned to define and own this category permanently — not just win the next 18 months.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my business, my current growth story, and my long-term defensibility thesis

2. Audit the deck for short-term thinking — flag every slide that optimizes for speed over permanence

3. Build the last mover slide — network effects, switching costs, proprietary data, or brand that compounds

4. Write the durability narrative — why winning now sets up a position that can't be replicated later

5. Connect the last mover argument back to the ask — why this raise accelerates permanence, not just growth

</steps>

<rules>

- Growth rates are not moats — investors know this, even if you don't say it

- The durability argument must be specific to your business, not generic to the category

- Every defensibility claim must have a named mechanism — not "network effects" but exactly how they work here

- The last mover frame should make investors feel late, not early

- Never use the word "scale" without explaining what gets harder to replicate as you scale

</rules>

Durability Mechanisms → Last Mover Slide → Narrative Script → Connection to the Ask

4. BUILD THE TEAM SLIDE THAT ACTUALLY WINS ROOMS

Prompt:

<role>Act as a founding team narrative strategist applying Peter Thiel's "power law of people" — the single most important slide is whether these specific humans should be the ones building this — to investor decks.</role>

<task>Rewrite my team slide so it doesn't list credentials — it proves that this exact group of people is the only plausible team to solve this exact problem.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my team's backgrounds, how we met, and what each person has done that no one else could replicate

2. Strip every credential that could belong to someone else — MBA, ex-Google, Y Combinator — unless it directly explains why we win here

3. Find the unfair insight each founder has that comes from lived experience, not education

4. Write the team slide narrative — specific, non-transferable, built to answer "why them, why now"

5. Script the 60-second verbal pitch of the team that makes investors feel they're looking at a force of nature

</steps>

<rules>

- Credentials are table stakes — experience specific to this problem is the differentiator

- Every team member on the slide must have a reason to exist that can't be hired away

- The team slide should answer one question: why would these people be impossible to replace?

- Never lead with title — lead with the thing they know that no one else in the room does

- If the team slide could belong to a different company, rewrite it

</rules>

Unfair Insights Per Founder → Team Slide → 60-Second Verbal Pitch → What Investors Should Feel

5. ANTICIPATE EVERY HARD QUESTION BEFORE IT'S ASKED

Prompt:

<role>Act as a fundraising Q&A strategist applying Peter Thiel's interrogation framework — the best founders have already destroyed their own thesis before investors do — to investor deck preparation.</role>

<task>Surface the five questions that could kill my raise, build airtight answers for each, and design the Q&A strategy so I control the room instead of reacting to it.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my business, my deck thesis, and the objections I most dread hearing

2. Generate the five questions a great investor will definitely ask — not the easy ones

3. Write the answer to each question using Thiel's framework: acknowledge the tension, don't deflect it, then reframe it as proof of the thesis

4. Identify which questions are best pre-empted inside the deck itself — so they never get asked

5. Write the one sentence that redirects any question I can't answer well back to the central argument

</steps>

<rules>

- Never answer a hard question with data alone — that signals defensiveness

- The best answer reframes the risk as a feature of the opportunity

- Pre-empting a question in the deck is stronger than answering it in Q&A

- Founders who say "great question" are stalling — eliminate that habit entirely

- Every answer should end by returning to the thesis — not the objection

</rules>

The Five Hard Questions → Answers Using Thiel's Framework → What to Pre-Empt in the Deck → The Redirect Sentence

6. FRAME THE ASK SO INVESTORS FEEL THEY'RE BUYING A BARGAIN

Prompt:

<role>Act as a fundraising close strategist applying Peter Thiel's value capture framework — companies that create enormous value often capture very little of it — to the ask slide of investor decks.</role>

<task>Reframe my fundraising ask so investors don't feel like they're being sold to — they feel like they're being let in on something before the rest of the market figures it out.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my raise amount, valuation, use of funds, and the milestone the round is designed to hit

2. Reframe the ask around the milestone — not the money — so investors see what they're actually buying

3. Write the ask slide so the valuation feels like a discount relative to what the milestone unlocks

4. Script the verbal close — the last 90 seconds that connects the secret, the team, the traction, and the ask into one inevitable conclusion

5. Write the one question an investor should ask after this slide — and the answer that closes the room

</transformation>

<rules>

- Never defend the valuation — anchor it to what the company looks like after the milestone

- The ask should feel like access, not a transaction

- Use of funds must map directly to the milestone — every dollar should have a job

- The verbal close must reference the opening secret — the deck should feel like one complete argument

- If investors are asking "why now," your ask framing is weak — fix the setup

</rules>

Milestone Framing → Ask Slide → Verbal Close Script → The One Question and Its Answer

7. DESIGN THE TRACTION SLIDE THAT DOESN'T LIE

Prompt:

<role>Act as a fundraising data strategist applying Peter Thiel's signal vs. noise framework — one real signal beats a hundred vanity metrics — to investor traction slides.</role>

<task>Identify the single metric in my business that proves the thesis is true — and build a traction slide around it that makes every other number feel irrelevant.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my current metrics — revenue, users, retention, NPS, growth rate, whatever I track

2. Run every metric through the signal test: does this prove the secret, or does it just prove we're working?

3. Identify the one number that, if it keeps growing, makes this company inevitable

4. Design the traction slide around that single number — with context, trajectory, and what it means

5. Flag every vanity metric currently in the deck and write the line that replaces it

</steps>

<rules>

- DAU without retention is not a signal — it's a warning

- Revenue without margin context is not a signal — it's noise

- The best traction slides have one number that does all the work

- Never show a hockey stick that starts three weeks ago

- If the metric requires explanation to look good, it's the wrong metric

</rules>

The Signal Metric → Why It Proves the Thesis → Traction Slide Design → Vanity Metrics to Cut

9. OPEN WITH THE ONE THING THAT SHOULDN'T BE TRUE

Prompt:

<role>Act as an opening slide architect applying Peter Thiel's contrarian question framework — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — to investor presentations.</role>

<task>Design an opening that forces investors to confront a belief they hold that is silently killing the market — and positions my company as the only logical response to it.</task>

<steps>

1. Ask for my business, the market I'm entering, and what conventional wisdom I'm challenging

2. Isolate the one false belief the entire industry is operating on

3. Write the opening slide — one claim, no hedging, built to create immediate cognitive dissonance

4. Script the first 90 seconds — the setup, the reveal, and the pivot to my solution

5. Test it: if an investor nods instead of frowning at first, the hook is too safe

</steps>

<rules>

- The opening claim must make at least one person in the room uncomfortable

- Never open with the market size — that's a signal you have nothing interesting to say

- The false belief must be something the investors themselves have acted on before

- The reveal must make my company feel inevitable, not impressive

- Safe openings kill raises — build for reaction, not approval

</rules>

The False Belief → Opening Slide → First 90-Second Script → The Reaction You're Engineering
