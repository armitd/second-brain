[1/4] UNDERSTAND YOUR ATTENTION

ADHD = Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder.

But that’s stupid.

It should be attention DIFFERENCE. Not deficit.

Your brain isn’t damaged - it’s just build different.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWVrBwawAAukud.jpg)

---

Seriously, calling ADHD ‘a disease’ is dumb.

Just because you can’t focus ‘the normal way’ doesn’t mean you can’t focus.

It’s like being told you can’t write because you're left-handed … while being forced to write with your right hand.

Let’s fix that:

There are two types of attention:

1) Bottom-up: unconscious, driven by stimuli (noise, visuals)  
2) Top-down: conscious, driven by thoughts (goals, intentions)

What does this mean for people with ADHD?

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWVyxuacAAsH5Q.jpg)

---

1.1/ Distractions

With ADHD, bottom-up attention overwhelms top-down attention.

(you get easily distracted by all the external stimuli around you)

To fight this:

Clean your desk. Put your phone away. Avoid working in public and use noise-cancelling headphones if you have to.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWVzrxa0AA0NZc.jpg)

---

1.2/ Discipline

Weak top-down attention makes ADHD brains struggle with discipline.

Forcing yourself to do something boring?  
Impossible.

Doing something you love for 10 hours straight?  
Laser-focused.

1.3/ Intentions

Finally, ADHD impairs your intentions.

You know you should do something, you intend to do it, yet you don't.

Forgetting birthdays. Procrastinating on a 15-minute task for months. Having to physically force yourself to take out the trash.

It is what it is.

Okay, quick recap.

ADHD brain struggles with top-down attention. This makes it:

• vulnerable to distractions  
• difficult to consciously direct attention  
• difficult to follow through with your intentions

[2/4] FIX YOUR HABITS AND ADDICTIONS

How normal brain forms habits:

1) Get dopamine from rewards (good grades/money)  
2) Connect rewards to cues (sitting down to study/work)  
3) Get motivation (dopamine) from early cues  
4) Habit is created

But ADHD brain works differently…

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWV-zTbEAEv-LM.png)

---

People with ADHD need the reward IMMEDIATELY.

Otherwise, the habit doesn’t form.

That’s why they get so easily addicted to video games and social media - those give the reward INSTANTLY, every single time, day after day.

Here’s what it means for anyone with ADHD:

1) Avoid highly addictive behavior at all costs  
2) Gamify the ‘boring’ habits you’re trying to form

For example, I autistically track everything important in my life:

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWWFfabQAAwDHD.png)

---

Quick recap:

• Your brain is vulnerable to addictive behaviors, so avoid them  
• You CAN create habits, you just need to gamify them  
• Motivating yourself for delayed rewards is difficult

Next step - rewire your brain for proper focus:

[3/4] SUPERCHARGE YOUR BRAIN

To focus, you need:

• GABA (impulse control)  
• Dopamine (motivation)  
• Adrenaline (energy & alertness)

But 24/7 stimulation fries your brain. Your chemical levels are all messed up. You feel tired, anxious, and depressed. Here’s how to fix it:

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWWHNObYAAYwBW.jpg)

---

First, do a ‘system reset’ to restore chemical balance:

• No caffeine  
• No social media  
• No video games

It takes around 30 days. Yes, it will suck. Especially the first week. You will feel horrible. But you must do it. And the reward is 100% worth it.

---

Next, increase your dopamine NATURALLY:

• Exercise  
• Sunlight  
• Meditation  
• Good sleep  
• Novelty in work

Thanks to the detox you did, these ‘boring’ activities will start to feel enjoyable and naturally boost your motivation.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GjWWI_ybUAAv20Y.png)

---

Finally, avoid addictive behaviors.

Remember, ADHD brain is extra vulnerable to addictions.

One episode of Squid Game can easily turn into a 7-hour binge.

(don’t ask me how I know)

---

To restore your neurochemical balance:

• Reset your brain with 30-day detox  
• Naturally increase your dopamine  
• Avoid addictive behaviors

Final step - peak performance.
