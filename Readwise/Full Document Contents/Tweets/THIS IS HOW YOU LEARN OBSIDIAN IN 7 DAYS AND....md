#### What Obsidian Actually Is

Most note apps store your notes for you. 

Obsidian stores your notes as you. 

It’s a **local-first, plain-text note-taking app** built around a simple idea: your notes are Markdown files saved directly on your device. 

But the real power isn’t the app. It’s the philosophy behind it. 

Obsidian treats your notes like a **personal knowledge graph** - a web of connected ideas that grows and evolves the more you use it. 

Every note can link to another. 

Every link builds a map of how you actually think. 

The difference from other apps:

Notion → Database thinking (rows, tables, structure first)

Evernote → Filing cabinet thinking (folders, tags, search)

Obsidian → Network thinking (links, connections, emergence) 

If Notion is a spreadsheet for your brain and Evernote is a filing cabinet, Obsidian is the whiteboard where ideas actually connect.

#### How Obsidian Works

Obsidian runs on three core mechanics. Understand these, and everything else clicks. 

1. **Vaults:** A vault is just a folder on your computer. Every note you create inside it is a .md (Markdown) file. You own it. It never disappears because a company shuts down. You can open it in any text editor. It works offline, always.
2. **Internal Links**: Type [[note name]] Inside any note, Obsidian creates a live link to that note. This is where the magic starts. One link is nothing. A thousand links become a map of your mind.
3. **The Graph View**: Every time you link two notes, Obsidian draws a line between them in the Graph View - a live visual of your entire knowledge base. Notes that connect to many others become hubs. Clusters of related ideas reveal themselves. You start to see patterns you didn’t know existed.![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HKCKxerbUAAOvZH.jpg)

These three mechanics - vault, links, graph - are the engine. Everything else is customization.

#### Quick Pro Tips

* **Use** hashtags (#)  **for status, not topics:** Tags like # todo, # review, # waiting Let you filter notes by their position in your workflow. Topics belong in note titles and links, not tags.
* **Write note titles as full sentences:** “Why sleep affects memory consolidation” is 10x more useful than “Sleep notes.” Full sentences force clarity and make the graph view readable.
* **Link before you search:** If you reference something, link to it. Every [[link]] You make five minutes of future searching.
* **The 2-minute note rule:** If a note takes less than 2 minutes to create, do it now. Don’t bookmark. Don’t screenshot. Write the note, drop it in the inbox, and link it later.
* **Review your Inbox every Friday:** The Inbox is not permanent storage. Process it weekly - move, link, or delete. A full Inbox is a cluttered mind.

#### Obsidian Note Formats

Different notes need different structures. Here are the five formats I use for everything: 

1. **Evergreen Note**: A permanent idea, written in your own words. The title is a full claim. Links to 3+ other notes.

```
# Consistent systems beat motivation every time  
The science behind habit formation shows...  
[[Why habits beat willpower]] | [[James Clear's framework]]  

```
2. **Meeting Note:** Use the template from Step 7. Fill in before and during. Link action items to project notes.
3. **Daily Note:** Date as title. Loose capture. Link freely. Process weekly.

```
# 2025-06-02  
Working on: [[Obsidian Newsletter Issue]]  
Idea: Daily notes are underrated because...  
Follow up: [[Content Pipeline Project]]  

```
4. **Project Note:** One note per active project. Top section = goal. Middle = running notes. Bottom = linked resources.

```
# Project: Q3 Content Calendar  
Goal: 12 newsletter issues planned by July 1## Status  
In progress

## Notes + Decisions

## Resources  
[[Newsletter Format Guide]] | [[Topic Ideas]]  

```
5. **Book/Article Note** Source → Key ideas → Your reaction → Links to your existing notes. 

```
# Book: How to Take Smart Notes — Ahrens  
Core idea: Writing is thinking, not recording thinking.  
My reaction: This changes how I treat drafts.  
Links: [[Why first drafts should be ugly]] | [[Zettelkasten method]]
```

#### Most Common Mistakes to Avoid

The worst mistake I made wasn't installing 30 plugins. It was copying. For the first month, I highlighted articles and pasted them into Obsidian. I had 200 "notes." I understood nothing. One day, I tried to explain an idea I had "noted" to a friend. Blank. I couldn't do it. Notes aren't knowledge. Copying isn't thinking. Writing the idea in your own words is when understanding happens. I deleted 150 notes that week. Kept 50. The 50 I actually understood. Here is the list of the most common mistakes you should avoid: 

* **Building the system before using it:** The perfect folder structure means nothing if you have no notes. Write first. Organize later.
* **Treating it like a filing cabinet:** Obsidian is not Evernote. If you’re putting notes in folders and never linking them, you’re using a Ferrari as a parking spot.
* **Installing 30 community plugins in Week 1:** Every plugin adds friction and potential conflicts. Use core plugins for 30 days. Add community plugins only when you feel a specific gap.
* **Orphan notes:** A note that links to nothing is a note you’ll never find again. Every note should link to at least one other note before you close it.
* **Copying instead of writing:** Highlighting an article and pasting it into Obsidian is not note-taking. It’s digital hoarding. Write the idea in your own words. That’s when understanding happens.
* **Quitting after one week:** Obsidian compounds. At 100 notes, it feels useful. At 500 notes, it feels magical. The readers who quit after 20 notes never find out.![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HKCKxerbMAEqEmo.jpg)
