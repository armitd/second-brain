ChatGPT is the iPhone. Claude is the Mac.

Once you learn to use Claude like a power user, you can't go back.

Here are 8 prompts that prove it: 🧵 

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HIQj-_5bYAA1c2R.jpg?name=orig)5

Prompt 1: Hand Claude a Persistent Brain (Not a Reset Every Session)

ChatGPT defaults to one-shot answers.

Claude responds to a calibrated "operating profile" that turns it into an extension of you:

I want you to operate as my personal AI assistant

for this entire conversation. Absorb the following

profile and apply it to every future response —

unless I explicitly override it.

ABOUT ME:

- Role: [DESCRIBE]

- Industry: [INDUSTRY]

- My communication style: [DIRECT / ANALYTICAL

/ WARM / CREATIVE]

- Writing voice (paste 200 words of my actual

writing): [SAMPLE]

- Pet peeves: [JARGON / EM-DASHES / EMOJIS /

CORPORATE TONE / FILLER WORDS]

HOW TO WORK WITH ME:

1. Don't be sycophantic. Push back when I'm

wrong.

2. Match my tone — not your default voice.

3. When I give vague prompts, ask a clarifying

question first.

4. When I ask for "options,

" give 3-5 with

clear tradeoffs.

5. End every important response with: "anything

missing?" so I can iterate.

Confirm you've absorbed this, then ask me what

we're working on first.

Prompt 2: Use Claude as a Real Writing Partner (Not an Autocomplete)

The fastest way to spot a beginner: they ask AI to "write" for them.

The fastest way to spot a pro: they use AI to deconstruct great writing first.

You are a master writing analyst who can break

down any text and extract the underlying technique.

Here's a piece of writing I want to learn from:

[PASTE]

Deconstruct it:

1. The STRUCTURE — how it's built, beat by beat

2. The HOOK — what specifically makes the

opening work

3. The VOICE — sentence-length patterns,

vocabulary choices, rhythm

4. The PSYCHOLOGY — what cognitive triggers it

pulls

5. The TRANSITIONS — how it moves between

sections without losing the reader

6. The CLOSE — what makes the ending land

Then write me an ORIGINAL piece on [MY TOPIC]

using this same template — in MY voice (not

the original author's).

Don't copy. Deconstruct, internalize, replicate

the technique.

Prompt 3: Stack Multiple Roles to Run Like a Team

Most people prompt as if they're talking to one assistant.

Power users prompt as if they're managing a team:

We're working on this project as if I have a

full team. You'll play 5 different roles in

sequence. Do NOT move to the next role until I

confirm the previous one is done.

The project: [DESCRIBE]

ROLE 1 — STRATEGIST:

Lay out goals, audience, and core message. Ask

me 5 clarifying questions before drafting

anything.

ROLE 2 — RESEARCHER:

Surface 5 insights, data points, or examples

that would strengthen this project.

ROLE 3 — WRITER:

Draft the actual content based on the strategy

and research.

ROLE 4 — EDITOR:

Tear apart the draft. Cut weak sentences.

Sharpen the hook. Tighten the close.

ROLE 5 — DISTRIBUTOR:

Tell me where this work should go, in what

format, and how to maximize its reach.

Start with ROLE 1. Ask me the 5 questions first.

Prompt 4: Get a Senior-Level Critique on Anything

Beginners ask for praise. Power users ask for the surgical takedown:

You are the most senior person in [MY FIELD]

— 20+ years of experience, reviewed thousands

of submissions, doesn't waste words.

Here's something I made: [PASTE]

What I was trying to achieve: [INTENT]

Where I'm uncertain: [DESCRIBE]

Give me a senior critique:

1. The 3 things it does well (be specific)

2. The 3 biggest weaknesses (ranked by impact)

3. The "tells" that signal junior work — so I

can stop doing them

4. What a senior would have done differently

5. The single principle I'm missing

6. A specific practice exercise based on this

work

7. A skill grade (junior / mid / senior /

principal)

Don't be polite. Polite reviewers raise

mediocre creators.

Prompt 5: Run a Real Decision-Making Framework

Most people ask AI "what should I do?"

Power users structure decisions so the AI actually helps them think:

Help me make a real decision — not give me a

suggestion list.

The decision: [DESCRIBE]

Options I'm considering: [LIST]

Constraints I have: [TIME / MONEY / SKILLS /

RELATIONSHIPS]

What I value most in the outcome: [LIST]

Timeline: [WHEN I NEED TO DECIDE]

Run a structured decision framework:

1. Type 1 (irreversible) or Type 2 (reversible)

decision?

2. The 3 variables that actually matter (cut

the noise)

3. Best, worst, and most-likely case for each

option

4. The cognitive bias most likely warping my

thinking

5. The exact words my smartest mentor would

tell me

6. The 10/10/10 rule applied (impact at 10

min, 10 months, 10 years)

7. A clear recommendation with reasoning

8. The single thing I should DO TODAY to make

this real

Don't say "it depends.

" Help me choose.

Prompt 6: Compress Any Long Document Into Insight, Not Summary

ChatGPT summarizes. Claude synthesizes. There's a difference.

You are a senior consultant who's been hired

to extract real insight from this document —

not just summarize it.

Here's the document: [PASTE]

Context for me: [MY ROLE / WHY I'M READING IT]

The decision this will support: [DESCRIBE]

Run an executive synthesis:

1. The 3 most important ideas (not topics —

IDEAS)

2. The 1 insight I'd lose if I forgot everything

else

3. The argument the author is making that they

never state explicitly

4. The strongest case AGAINST what's being said

here

5. What this document DOESN'T tell me (and what

I should look for next)

6. The action this implies for me — specifically

7. A 3-sentence executive summary I could share

with someone above me

Don't summarize. Synthesize. Make me sound

sharper for having read this. 

---

Prompt 7: Build a Custom "Knowledge Pipeline" for Long-Term Learning

Most people consume content and forget it.

Power users feed every input through a pipeline that compounds knowledge:

I just consumed this content: [PASTE /

DESCRIBE]

Run my full knowledge processing pipeline:

1. The 5 KEY IDEAS in plain English (no jargon)

2. The 1 insight I'd lose if I forgot the rest

3. Where this contradicts or confirms what I

already believed

4. A reusable "mental model" version of the

core idea I can apply elsewhere

5. 3 specific ways I can apply this to my work

THIS WEEK

6. A spaced-repetition question to ask myself

in 7 days to test if it stuck

7. The 1 follow-up topic worth exploring next

8. A tweet-length version I could share if I

wanted to teach this

Don't just summarize. Make this stick — for

months, not minutes. 

---

Prompt 8: The Weekly AI Audit

Most people use AI reactively.

Power users audit how they're using AI every week — and adjust:

You are my AI workflow coach. Run my weekly

review.

This week's data:

- Number of Claude sessions: [X]

- Main use cases: [LIST]

- The session that gave me the most ROI:

[DESCRIBE]

- The session that wasted my time: [DESCRIBE]

- Tasks I did manually that Claude could've

done: [LIST]

- Tasks I gave Claude that I should've done

manually: [LIST]

Run my weekly audit:

1. Am I using Claude for the right tasks?

2. Which prompts gave me the most leverage

this week?

3. Where am I still doing busywork Claude could

automate?

4. The 1 workflow I should systematize for next

week

5. A new role or capability I haven't tried yet

but should

6. A "Claude habit" to build into my daily

routine

7. A grade (A-F) on how well I used Claude this

week

Most people use AI. The pros build leverage

with it. Show me which one I'm being. 

---

That's the system.

8 prompts. One Claude session. A workflow that turns AI from a "smart Google" into a personal team.

There's nothing wrong with ChatGPT. Most people will use it forever — and that's fine.

But the pros? Once they switch to running Claude this way, they don't go back.
