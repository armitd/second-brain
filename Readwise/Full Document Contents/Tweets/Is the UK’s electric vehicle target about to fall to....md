## Full Document
Is the UK’s electric vehicle target about to fall to pieces? And why might it all hinge on parking in East London?

I shouldn’t tell you – yet – because our report only goes online the new year. But, since it’s running in the papers, here’s a thread.🧵🔌🚗

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFlP30XoAAspqr.jpg)

---

(Here’s the Guardian article)

[theguardian.com/environment/20…](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2024/dec/29/ev-electric-cars-vehicles-charging-points-uk-report)

---

Electric vehicle sales were booming. Around 2021, you couldn’t forecast high enough. The OBR revised their forecasts up three times in two years, and were still a third short of reality.

The explanation is simple - drivers loved electric cars, and government incentives worked.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFlkq5W4AAHc82.jpg)

---

But now, sales have plateaued.

Which has the motor industry very worried – laying off thousands of workers and shutting down big plants.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFl03JXAAAL-nD.jpg)![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFl03PWIAAVg2E.jpg)![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFl03gXQAAV_jR.png)

---

Carmakers are now obliged to sell certain proportions of zero-emission vehicles, and get fined £18,000 per off-target vehicle.

But – significantly – the public aren’t obliged to buy them.

Spot the problem?

---

The UK’s EV transition assumes limitless enthusiasm. Which wasn’t unreasonable until 2023

But now, the data suggests we’ve hit a ceiling. And, given the UK buys about 1.9m cars a year, this can be a multi-billion pound problem

The question of ‘why’ is suddenly very, very urgent

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFmJDtXYAAToLY.jpg)

---

(That graph undersells the problem – that early growth was driven by real consumer enthusiasm. The projected 2027-8 ‘restart’ in rapid growth is mandated by regulations😬)

---

Back in November, we ran some polling. Not because of the carmakers’ problem, but because of charging.

In October, I’d seen this graph from the Resolution Foundation about the cost of EV charging, and could see another storm brewing

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFpZ1cXAAApLk_.png)

---

In recent years, a huge gap has opened up on the price of recharging an EV

[@OctopusEnergy](https://twitter.com/OctopusEnergy) had its groundbreaking home-charging package, that let you recharge an EV for 7p/KWh. About 2p/mile

Contrast that with a commercial ultra-rapid charger at 75p/KWh - pricier than petrol

---

But you can only do home charging if you’ve got a driveway. And a lot of people don’t. Including me, FWIW.

A 6-10x difference in fuel costs might be a good place to start looking for the cause of that plateau.

---

And also – charging without a driveway isn’t fun. You have no special right to park outside your house. You run a cable across the road at your own legal risk

That looks like a big source of friction too.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFqJ5AWQAAwdXI.jpg)

---

We asked respondents how interested they were in shifting to EVs, and what would most affect their decisions.

Subtext – was it support to buy (which is a central govt subsidy, well explored) or issues around charging (local government action, mostly unexplored)?

---

We looked at 58 subgroups, and 57 of them gave the same answer.

It’s all about the charging.

The thing we don’t have much policy on.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFqegtWUAEuTOo.jpg)

---

I’m being a bit unkind. Early EV policy was designed to prove EVs could work by:

1) Bringing down purchase prices  
2) Tackling range anxiety  
3) Helping home charging

It got EVs from <1% to 25% market share. Without that ‘generation 1’ policy there would be no EV story.

---

But if you want to make EVs universal, you’re not thinking about that first car. You’re thinking about the average car. Making everyone think going electric is natural.

And that requires ‘generation 2’ of policy

---

And that’s a lot harder

Because you’re now having to deal with the tricky bits. It’s easy to sell an EV to a tech-mad high-income driver with a driveway.

Not so easy for streets like this.

Or for e.g. tradespeople who need their van next to their job, not laid up by a charger.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFqqMjX0AATzII.jpg)

---

You can see this with Uber, who have a big push to be 100% electric in London by 2025. I think they've spent more than City Hall’s entire electrification programme

Uber drivers can get over £20k off a new EV, and spread costs by leasing

But many drivers still aren’t switching

---

And to see why – look at these two maps.

The first shows where the public charge points in London are.

The second shows where the people working in transport live.

Not much of a match, is it?

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFrPwPXEAAb9op.png)![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFrQMtWMAAtYx8.png)

---

The RAC Foundation did clever work to find how many houses in the UK have the potential for off-street parking – and thereby home charging

Nationwide, it’s not a bad number. But in inner London 4 homes out of 5 have no capacity for it.

Across London, 1.8 million households

---

Our polls said 61% of Londoners either have an EV, or are thinking about one next.

If everyone curious about buying an EV did so, half the households in London would have one within 2 years.

But, clearly, that’s not happening unless policy changes

---

Hence our new report. We think it’s time to write that ‘generation 2’ policy.

People we’ve spoken to – transport providers, politicians, green NGOs and more – are interested in getting round a table to talk about a solution

Feels like time to book a table.

![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GgFslFUXcAAq8kb.png)

---

And I think London – with its extra-large group of potential EV buyers, and its tricky-to-tame kerbside is the place to solve this.

It can do the most to change the UK market. It can save the most carbon fast – especially if it targets high-mileage drivers.

---

There is a mayor whose greatest efforts have been to deliver cleaner air and a greener city.

And its politicians will benefit most from taking the pain out of electrification before their constituents discover it first-hand. Which more of them will do by the 2028 elections.

---

2025 could be the year London shows how an old city meets this new challenge.

Our paper comes out in a few days

Until then, don’t tell anyone 😉
