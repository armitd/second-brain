### New Report Warns of Rising ‘AI Replacement Dysfunction’ Threatening Today’s Workforce

A recent study said ‘psychological and existential distress’ is affecting many workers worried that AI tools will rob them of their jobs and careers.

Up to now, most [medical concern](https://www.inc.com/ava-levinson/this-scientist-warned-of-ai-psychosis-now-he-says-were-in-dire-straits-and-new-research-backs-him-up/91302605) about surging use of [artificial intelligence](https://www.inc.com/kit-eaton/why-microsoft-is-cautioning-businesses-about-launching-ai-tools/91196982) ( [AI](https://www.inc.com/artificial-intelligence "AI")) apps have focused on the threat of some users growing intimate with [chatbots](https://www.inc.com/ross-borden/how-to-drive-engagement-with-ai-chatbots/91212486 "chatbots"), and possibly acting on self-harming advice those virtual confidantes might urge. Now researchers are warning about another kind of risk — “AI replacement dysfunction,” or AIRD, which manifests in employees [fearing the tech](https://www.inc.com/bruce-crumley/new-research-finds-that-ai-is-creating-more-jobs-and-higher-pay/91199930) will deprive them of a job, entire career, and usefulness in the world.

Though AIRD is not yet recognized in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Stephanie McNamara and Joseph Thornton, the two University of Florida researchers who identified the dysfunction, say it’s real, spreading, and needs a clinical, standardized definition, identification of symptoms, and treatment protocols. [Their study](https://www.cureus.com/articles/407877-artificial-intelligence-replacement-dysfunction-aird-a-call-to-action-for-mental-health-professionals-in-an-era-of-workforce-displacement#!/) on the ailment says it may be appearing in some workplaces that adopt AI as “psychological and existential distress that could be experienced by individuals facing the threat or reality of job displacement due to artificial intelligence.”

Some evidence suggests most workers are indeed anxious about the AI apps already being introduced and used in countless businesses to automate work tasks.

A Reuters survey last August found fully 77 percent of respondents feared the tech was already “causing [permanent job loss](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/americans-fear-ai-permanently-displacing-workers-reutersipsos-poll-finds-2025-08-19/)” on a mass scale. Even [CEOs of companies](https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/mark-cuban-and-anthropics-ceo-are-arguing-about-how-many-jobs-ai-will-replace/91196271) developing those tools have warned they [may eliminate half](https://www.axios.com/2025/05/28/ai-jobs-white-collar-unemployment-anthropic) of all entry-level jobs, and [take over most](https://www.ft.com/video/2c428045-bf4f-45bd-ada2-8ba53983cd81) white-collar positions.

Those warnings — and workers witnessing AI actually taking over their work tasks — are already generating AIRD symptoms in a growing number of people, the researchers said. Their paper noted those troubles include “anxiety, insomnia, depression, or identity confusion symptoms that may reflect deeper fears about relevance, purpose, and future employability.”

The study’s authors think those negative side effects of AI are likely to spread as businesses accelerate their use of the tech. For that reason, they urge medical and business professionals to start creating models to identify and treat AIRD immediately, rather than wait until the problem swamps them.

“AI displacement is an [invisible disaster](https://medicalxpress.com/news/2026-02-mental-health-effects-ai-driven.html),” said paper coauthor and clinical associate professor of psychiatry Thornton in a statement about the study. “As with other disasters that affect mental health, effective responses must extend beyond the clinician’s office to include community support and collaborative partnerships that foster recovery.”

There are two big challenges in battling AIRD. First and foremost, it has only recently been analyzed and defined as an ailment, with most people unaware the concept even exists. Second, researchers said the exact kinds of symptoms produced differ significantly between individual sufferers, and can also be accompanied by seemingly unrelated secondary conditions like substance abuse or psychiatric troubles.

That’s why the study’s coauthors urge medical and business leaders to recognize the existence and threat of AIRD now, and start laying the foundation for standardized identification and treatment.
