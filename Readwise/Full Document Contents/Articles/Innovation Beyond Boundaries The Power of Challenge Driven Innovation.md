####  The stages of CDI

CDI represents a departure from the conventional stage-gate framework, offering a more dynamic and adaptable approach to innovation. While it might be tempting to draw parallels between CDI stages and traditional stages, it's crucial to recognize that CDI stages are not mere renamings but a transformative rethinking of the innovation process. These stages often overlap and iterate, ensuring that innovation is an ongoing, iterative journey. However, what truly distinguishes CDI from any other innovation process is the emphasis on collaboration and the deliberate inclusion of external resources to enhance the final product/concept/service. 

1. Idea Gathering: At the start of CDI, numerous opportunities are collected, aiming to select the most promising ones for further development. CDI encourages diverse idea sources, including external contributors.
2. Filtering: Here, the focus is on selecting projects aligning with capabilities and strategic goals, filtering out those that don't meet these criteria to optimize resource allocation.
3. Dissection: CDI transforms innovation by breaking complex projects into modular 'Challenges.' These Challenges are substantial, yet independently executable, promoting collaboration and diverse expertise.
4. Channel Distribution: Challenge modules are strategically placed into various channels, like research organizations, collaborations, and internal initiatives, diversifying the innovation ecosystem.
5. Evaluation/Confirmation: After Challenge modules' completion, the results are assessed against predefined criteria to ensure alignment with goals and quality standards.
6. Assembly and Integration: Refined Challenge modules are integrated into a functional whole, leveraging CDI's flexibility for a seamless process.
7. Launch: The innovation is finally introduced to the market, emphasizing speed and effectiveness, marking the culmination of the CDI journey and the start of a new cycle.

####  The proof is in the pudding: NASA

NASA faced an enormous challenge in measuring strain on Kevlar and Vectran straps within the demanding temperature range of 25 to 125°C. Despite three years of dedicated efforts, their in-house research team struggled to devise a universally effective method for this critical task. Faced with a roadblock, NASA made the strategic decision to leverage the collective problem-solving capabilities of the global [Wazoku Crowd](https://uk.linkedin.com/company/wazokucrowd?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-mention) .

The collaborative effort yielded impressive results. Among the contributors, one standout Solver lacked a formal background in materials science BUT brought a particularly unique perspective to the table. They conducted rigorous testing of samples under exceptionally stringent conditions, whilst working at a medical college. This unconventional approach led to a breakthrough solution.

Ultimately, NASA's decision to tap into external expertise, through the Wazoku Crowd, not only ended a three-year impasse, but also liberated their internal team to refocus on pressing priorities – demonstrating the power of CDI.
