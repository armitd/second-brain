Sure, content is king. But Google is the king of content.

As the keeper of the World Wide Web’s content, Google determines what ranks-and what doesn’t. For many businesses, this can be the difference between success and failure.

An Inc.com Featured Presentation

In an effort to rank highly within Google search results, digital content is all about writing to hack the Google algorithm. The result often comes packages with infomercial-style writing that can feel reminiscent of a game of “Where’s Waldo?” where you must scour an article to find the information you clicked on it for. If you were to actually read the article in its entirety, it would cost you your time-and in some cases, sanity.

The dreadful practice is exhausting for the reader. But the reader isn’t alone.

It’s a shared sentiment among many writers who are simply doing their job and following their directives to write SEO-driven content that ranks in Google. More, it costs your business in terms of time that the marketing team must devote to writing for two audiences–people and search engines.

As the focus of content becomes centered around appealing to Google, the result is that many stop writing for the reader. So what happens when AI technology, such as [ChatGPT](https://openai.com/blog/chatgpt/) starts crafting content?

Many fear the potential implications of trading people for tech-and rightfully so. However, as an industry-insider with years of experience in digital publishing and marketing (and someone who is admittedly a very late adopter with a bit of an aversion to new tech), I hold the strange perspective that ChatGPT could prove highly beneficial. Not just to the reader who doesn’t want to read a 2,000 word article about how to take a screenshot on a Mac. But also for the writer.

#### Content Is Being Written To Appease Google-Not People

What many businesses and bloggers have found-large and small-is that search engine optimization (SEO) works. It’s what helps search engines such as Google identify certain content online as the best answer to the reader’s question. And when content ranks highly in Google, it yields a large readership-the goal behind most published content. But it can be in your best interest to [ignore SEO](https://www.inc.com/entrepreneurs-organization/why-its-in-your-best-interest-to-ignore-seo.html) and the fleeting whims of Google.

Yet, in a bid to attract Google by using [SEO tactics](http://www.inc.com/adam-heitzman/5-essential-seo-tactics-for-small-business-owners.html) excessively, a lot of content is written to appease Google-not readers like you and me.

The simple question of which type of lawyer handles wills quickly turns into an exhaustive article that defines what a will is, who should have one, when to get one, and what should be included. With ChatGPT at the world’s fingertips, businesses and blogs will have to step it up. Instead of writing for Google, content will need to be written for people.

#### With AI, Content Will Become More Human

Google is constantly evolving its algorithm to determine [ranking results](https://www.google.com/search/howsearchworks/how-search-works/ranking-results/). But there’s one thing that never changes: Google is ultimately looking to find the best answers to people’s questions.

In time, it will be able to decipher between content written by AI vs humans. But it will also separate content that’s written for Google vs humans.

In turn, it will help bolster the individual: their voice, their unique experience and their perspective. It will make our own unique human experience more valuable. Because while ChatGPT can pull any research available online to craft a paper or string words together delightfully to compose a poem, it has no first-hand knowledge of the research it quotes. And it surely doesn’t know the emotional backstory that led you to write poetry (should you be so included)-never mind how to encapsulate your emotions and perspective into words.

It’s only a matter of time until Google refreshes its algorithm yet again, and at some point this will involve detecting content written with AI. As we wait for that to happen, we will slowly start to see content get worse before it gets better.

#### The Individual Voice Will Become More Valued

Content has become so homogenous that it almost looks as though it’s already written by AI. When AI writing tools, such as ChatGPT become commonplace, it will become evident whether a news article, column or blog post was written by technology or by a human.

It will also create more space for the individual writer to produce more creative work based on first-hand experience. No longer will writers be tasked with as many assignments like “what is mulch?” or “how to write a check.” Articles won’t have to be thousands of words to indicate to Google what it’s about or that it’s on-topic to the search query. And they won’t have to be mind-numbingly sterile.

With the white glove service ChatGPT could provide informational content, it could help release writers from the confinements of SEO and open the floodgates for online content to become more unique, more insightful, and more human.

After all, while technology may raise fears of [human vulnerability](https://hbr.org/2003/09/technology-and-human-vulnerability), what technology teaches us is that what humans need are humans. With AI writing tools, content will become more human-not just for those reading it, but also those producing it.
