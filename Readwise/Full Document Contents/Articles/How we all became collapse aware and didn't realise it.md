## Full Document
##### And what to do next

I recently stumbled across Rosie Spinks' essay, '*[How I Became Collapse Aware](https://rojospinks.substack.com/p/how-i-became-collapse-aware)'*, and it really stuck with me. She reframes collapse, not as an apocalypse, but as a transition we could embrace—a perspective that feels both urgent and worth sharing. Felt very now...

Spinks describes an unease that many people have been talking to me about: “something about the way we live is coming to an end.” This feeling, sharpened by events such as 9/11, the 2008 financial crash, or the pandemic, highlight a stark reality: our political, economic, and ecological systems are faltering. And if incremental fixes no longer suffice, the question becomes: what comes next?

Spinks offers a framework of *There* and *Here*. *There* is the status quo: a world obsessed with relentless consumption, economic growth, and individual achievement. It’s a space where problems must be solved without disrupting the flow of profits or the illusion of infinite progress. *H**ere* represents a simpler, more grounded existence. In *Here*, wealth (things) gives way to sustenance and connection, and the emphasis shifts to interdependence and community.

> Living through collapse isn’t a factual statement, but an emotional one. It feels like we are approaching the end of a specific social contract. Modernity is a project founded on patriarchal domination, on linear time, infinite extraction, and unstoppable accumulation. In its five centuries, it has evolved into such an unnatural paradigm that it now only survives through extreme and perpetual violence; perpetrated indifferently against both humans and non-humans alike.
> 
> 

What makes Spinks’s POV so poignant is her refusal to treat collapse as an end. I really like that. Instead, she sees it as an opportunity—a chance to rediscover what sustains us at the core. Marginalised communities have long endured collapse, finding ways to thrive despite systemic failure. Their resilience offers valuable lessons for navigating our own transitions.

Spinks also talks about moving from *There* to *Here* could alleviate many of the societal issues we face today—mental health crises, social isolation, and the normalisation of anxiety and despair. Collapse, then, becomes less about what we lose and more about what we can rebuild. Could a life stripped of excess and rooted in community offer deeper psychological and spiritual fulfilment?

As uncertainty looms, try and see a call to action versus resignation. The shift from *There* to *Here* is an invitation to reimagine consumption, community, and care—not out of fear, but with a cautious hope that a richer, more meaningful way of living awaits.
