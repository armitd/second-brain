What is Mixed Reality? 

Mixed Reality (MR) is defined by Oxford Languages as “a medium consisting of immersive computer-generated environments in which elements of a physical and virtual environment are combined,” and described by Microsoft as “the next wave in computing followed by mainframes, PCs, and smartphones .” At [VISR](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fvisr-vr%2Ecom%2F&urlhash=4PBU&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block), we see MR as a gateway to the metaverse, something that will help to significantly change the way we interact with computers and the world around us and bring us to the next digital frontier.

While MR may seem like the technological advancement of the future, it’s not a new idea, first being defined back in 1994. 

Mixed Reality differs from Virtual Reality (VR) in that rather than immersing the user in a fully simulated virtual environment, it creates an environment in which simulated objects coexist with material reality. It’s similar to Augmented Reality (AR), but the development of more powerful computers with greater processing power has meant that we’ve been able to develop it into a more sophisticated technology. 

This technology has numerous applications such as design, education, gaming, military training, and job simulation. 

Mixed Reality brings a lot of exciting prospects to the world of education. MR allows for an immersive, interactive simulated learning environment. For example, a history student might use it to see hundreds of soldiers clash on a historic battlefield, or a destroyed building restored to its former glory. Universities have also found teaching with MR to be beneficial in such subjects as medicine and health sciences where 3D models of objects are useful, particularly in anatomy and physiology. 

A number of encouraging studies into the educational use of MR have yielded fantastic results. A [study by PwC](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Epwc%2Ecom%2Fus%2Fen%2Fservices%2Fconsulting%2Ftechnology%2Femerging-technology%2Fassets%2Fpwc-understanding-the-effectiveness-of-soft-skills-training-in-the-enterprise-a-study%2Epdf&urlhash=_H7o&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block) found huge improvements over typical classroom learning; users learned and completed training faster and were more focused and emotionally connected than classroom or e-learners. Microsoft reported a 22% improvement in test scores from those who used immersive tech.

With such positive results, we can therefore expect that in the future we will see further adoption of similar technologies in the classroom. VISR has seen a great deal of interest from colleges keen to explore working MR into the curriculum; particularly from those who specialise in vocational courses. MR helps the workforce of tomorrow get to grips with the technology they’ll likely use in future, as well as providing a hands-on learning experience. 

Virtual Reality has been part of computer gaming since 1989, with the release of Nintendo’s Power Glove for use with the NES console, which tracked the wearer’s hand movements in-game. Mixed Reality tech joined the gaming world in the new millennium when Sony released the Eye Toy for use with the PlayStation 2 in 2003, which used a camera to track users’ movements while portraying them on-screen in the game. We saw a similar, yet more sophisticated thing when Microsoft released Kinect for the Xbox 360 in 2010. The evolution of the mobile phone has also played its part in MR gaming, with the Pokémon Go craze of 2016 and subsequent MR mobile games. Meta’s acquisition of the Oculus platform in 2014, garnered more exposure for the world of MR gaming as the company made the product compatible with the Facebook platform to bring a social aspect to its games.

Virtual Reality technology has been used in a military context since 1992, when the United States Air Force developed Virtual Fixtures, the first ever fully immersive augmented reality system. Such technology is used as a training tool to offer personnel combat simulations, in which one may experience the immersion of a dangerous situation without the risk. MR systems have battlefield applications, potentially showing maps, thermal imaging, and the range of weaponry. In March 2021, Microsoft won a lucrative contract with the US Military, agreeing to deliver more than 120,000 devices based on the HoloLens, which could earn the company up to $21.9 billion over the next decade. The US Army recently confirmed that its [Integrated Visual Augmentation System (IVAS) project would deploy in late 2022](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Exrtoday%2Ecom%2Fmixed-reality%2Fus-army-confirms-ivas-to-deploy-in-2022%2F%23%3A%7E%3Atext%3DThe%2520Army%27s%2520IVAS%2520project%2520allows%2Cmissions%252C%2520among%2520many%2520other%2520features%2E&urlhash=oUPM&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block), following successful trials.

Numerous platforms can display Mixed Reality technology; the image that most people will have when they think of MR technology is that of a Head-Mounted Display (HMD). HMDs use optics to directly project an image in front of the wearer’s eyes, and are most popularly applied in entertainment, however these HMDs are immersive VR and do not integrate the real world into their displays. Other devices that do integrate physical reality such as Microsoft’s HoloLens and Google Glass have been found to have useful applications in areas such as medicine and engineering. 

A Head-Up Display (HUD) uses similar technology, projecting additional information onto an environment without causing unnecessary distraction to the user. HUD technology was first used by the military, to aid fighter pilots. Later HUDs were adopted as an aid to all forms of flight, both military and civilian. HUD technology also found its way into cars, projecting information into windscreens without need for a driver to take their eyes off the road. General Motors first put HUD units in cars back in 1988; the last few decades have seen manufacturers such as BMW, Nissan, Cadillac, and Toyota offer the technology in their models.

A Cave Automatic Virtual Environment (CAVE), is a form of virtual reality where images are projected onto the walls of a cube-shaped environment, first developed in 1992 at the University of Illinois. Motion capture systems locate the position of the user, whose movements control the environment generated by the projectors. 

More tech companies are committing themselves to the development of MR software; we recently saw Facebook change its name to Meta “to reflect its focus on building the metaverse”. there is no doubt that the technology is here to stay and will play a greater part in our lives in the future.
