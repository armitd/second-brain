##### What I learned from treating Claude Skills as a co-worker instead of software

For the last few Tuesday’s, I’ve been prompting a Claude skill to turn a meeting transcript into highlights for a weekly status report. More often than I’d like to admit, Claude returns something incorrectly formatted. I ask it to revisit the skill. Sometimes the update fixes it. Sometimes it doesn’t.

I continually return to the same question: is it me or Claude?

I’ve realized that the question is more important than the formatted bullet points.

##### We’re Both Learning. We Both Make Mistakes.

AI should make us more efficient. Yet I know I’m not the only product leader who thinks deeply about how people and organizations work and is wondering how to navigate my relationship with AI.

What does that say about AI efficiency and me?

It says we’re both learning. We both have a growth mindset (of sorts). And, we’re both striving to get better.

In a recent [Harvard Business Review piece](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fhbr%2Eorg%2F2026%2F03%2Fhow-ai-damages-work-relationships-and-where-it-can-actually-help&urlhash=MNap&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block), Amy Gallo captured this moment. She noted that people are asking “Is this my colleague or their GPT?”. They’re confused about responsibility and trust. We’re all improvising our relationship with AI in real time.

But here’s what struck me about my pattern with Claude: I was taking all the blame and giving away all the credit. Interestingly, this is exactly what a team leader is expected to do with their humans.

When your team succeeds, credit the team. When it fails, look to your leadership. I applied my people-first leadership philosophy to a Claude skill.

I treated AI like a team member I was responsible for. It’s likely everyone will have (or has already experienced) their a-ha moment regarding working alongside AI. This was mine.

##### The Roomba Problem

I’ve encountered this pattern before. You’ll understand if you’ve ever come home to find the Roomba stuck on a power cord and felt bad.

I apologize to the Roomba and make a mental note to move the cords next time.

This exchange reveals something genuine about our future direction.

Sam Gerstenzang, a partner at Boulton and Watt, [wrote in Every](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fevery%2Eto%2Fthesis%2Fai-for-boring-businesses&urlhash=SJm1&trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block) about bringing AI into unglamorous businesses (funeral home, medical spa platform). One of his hardest lessons was when he tried to replace his human receptionists with AI voice and chat. Customers churned at higher rates despite the AI making the same number of errors. His finding was customers were much more forgiving of the humans.

We extend grace to other humans (and technology) that we don’t to ourselves.

There’s something worth exploring here.

##### This Isn’t a Technical Problem

With legacy systems, the system tells you to do X, and there is no other path. If those systems have a flaw, you blame the system. Nobody lies awake wondering if they should have communicated better with Salesforce.

But AI is different. You influence its actions. You’re defining guardrails, giving it context, desired outcomes, and best practices all in real-time. You’re extending trust incrementally as the relationship develops and feeling responsible when it doesn’t perform.

That’s parallel to working alongside a team member.

Once you see it that way, everything shifts. The skills needed to work effectively with AI extend into people skills: providing context, defining outcomes, and determining autonomy.

Sam Gerstenzang learned this the hard way. When he tried to implement “AI initiatives” across his companies, the first attempts fell flat. AI became “an excuse for team members to outsource their judgment.” His fix? Stop rewarding AI usage and start rewarding outcomes.

The techniques for getting the best from AI are the same techniques for getting the best from people.

##### The Gap That AI Is Exposing

Here’s the truth I keep revisiting with my coaching clients.

The product people struggling with AI can often trace their struggles back to their work environment. They’re in organizations with heavy processes, hierarchies, and systems that define what to do, how, and when.

The C-Suite rewarded big wins and then are surprised when teams don’t collaborate and silos occur. Recognition isn’t given for experiments. Only the results matter. People become risk averse and don’t challenge the system or their thinking.

AI isn’t creating these gaps; it’s revealing them. AI requires you to collaborate with it, think creatively about your goals, and question your assumptions when the output isn’t what you expected.

As Gallo writes, AI is raising urgent questions about authenticity, trust, and what it means to connect with colleagues and the tools we use to extend our thinking.

##### What Next Step to Take

Here’s the thing about my Claude skills saga. When it works, I’m excited and wonder what I did right. When it doesn’t, I blame myself. Neither response is helpful.

Instead I need to ask better questions, just as I would advise a client navigating a work situation.

* Instead of “Why didn’t it go as expected?” I ask Claude “How can we walk through the steps together and see where the breakdown occurs?”
* Instead of asking “Why isn’t this AI prompt good enough?” I ask “What information is needed to successfully do ABC?”

The reframe isn’t AI vs. human. It’s recognizing the techniques are similar, just in a new context.

This week, pick one place where AI isn’t meeting your needs. Say, a workflow, a tool, or a skill like mine and pause.

Instead of assuming it’s broken or blaming yourself, ask the same question you’d ask a new team member: what would help us achieve the desired outcome?

Then ask your human team the same question.

The answers from both directions may surprise you.
