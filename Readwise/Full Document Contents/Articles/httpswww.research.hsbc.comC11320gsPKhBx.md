**The Edge of Disruption** 

**A disruptive year** –2020 is likely to be a tipping point in the adoption of digitalisation. We can

see this in the way people are behaving as employees (working from home), as students (online

education), as consumers (e-commerce), when seeking entertainment (virtual events, e-sports),

and when needing medical advice (e-medicine). We also see this in business, especially with

asset-heavy industries seeking efficiencies and disruptive new business models through

artificial intelligence and automation. In our view these are likely to be long-lasting changes in

behaviour which could lead to the next phase of technology-led growth around remote access.

We examine four themes linked to technology and related infrastructure that we think are key to

these shifts, and hence well-placed to drive long-term outperformance in an already growing

technology sector – **connectivity**, **automation**, **experiential**, and **digital health**.

Our analysis uses the *HSBC Disruption Framework*, which classifies technologies into five

phases of evolution. We focus on the important transition at the end of the cycle - from the “real

applications” phase to the “new normal” - and the resulting 1st tier (intended) and 2nd tier

(unintended) disruptive impacts on industries and consumer behaviour.

**Disruptive ESG** – Our report also dives into the key Environmental, Social and Governance

implications from this technology wave, including cybersecurity, automation and labour issues,

alternative income needs, the ‘digital divide’ risk in areas like access to e-payments and

telemedicine, ‘funding green’ and decarbonising a fast-growing data centre industry.

**Bridging emerging/developed markets** – We see areas where these disruptive themes could

help the emerging world leap-frog the rest, but also highlight where there are growing risks of a

‘digital divide’ for those who lack access to technology.

**Technology to watch** – illustrating some of the most exciting themes we see on the horizon:

 Forecast AI chip demand CAGR of 28% out to 2027

 ‘Cloud’ is still growing, with some 70% of software yet to migrate

 Automation in logistics, with only 5-6% of warehouses/factories currently automated

 IoT connected devices to double by 2025

 60% of people to have mobile internet by 2025, up from ca.49% now

 Data growth, with 5G likely to make up 20% of mobile connections by 2025

 Digital tech/augmented reality (AR) enabling online for personal care/cosmetics

 Digital health/telemedicine worth up to USD250bn annually in the US alone by 2025; see a

need for 60,000 medical robots globally by 2025, 4x that in 2018

 Hardware/software market for drone-related businesses to reach USD120bn by 2030

**The HSBC Disruption Framework and investing** 

It can be argued that technologies are at the heart of the modern business. However, not all

technologies are at the same level of maturity and ready to disrupt1 at scale, even if they are at

the edge of disruption.

From an investor’s point of view, it’s important to identify when a technology stack is ready to

disrupt and help business models leap forward. In this vein, we look at the HSBC Disruption

Framework, which classifies innovations into major five phases – see chart below.

To cut to the chase, from a commercial and thus investment implications perspective, the most

important transition is from phase “real applications” to “new normal”. The “real applications”

phase is when a technology becomes adopted by companies (often public) and they are either

using it behind the scenes in trials or small scale roll-outs but even though it isn’t necessary

ready for primetime, it will begin opening new engines of growth.

Then as the innovation starts scaling up, it will begin to disrupt the industry and the way

business is done. The magnitude of the revenues generated from the technologies will also

ramp up and become significant.

At this stage of the disruption framework, one will begin to see not only the technology

disrupting businesses in intended ways (1st tier implications) but due to the complex nature of

knock-on impact, the technology may very well have unintended consequences (2nd tier

implications). When the technology moves into this part of the framework, it’s then said to be

firmly in the “new normal” phase.

**Logistics** 

 **ESG: Robotic arms in warehouses impact DM today, EM tomorrow:** Warehouse

pickers will increasingly use computer vision technologies in conjunction with soft-grippers.

If the cost of these technologies fall, sooner rather than later, similar issues could arise in

the EM (as their use grows), especially in regions where wages are rising. The question is

what percentage (if any) of the displaced workers can re-train for different jobs.

 **ESG: Automated warehouses are more resilient to labour issues but are vulnerable**

disrupt the company and distribution of goods in the economy.

**Moving over distance** 

 **ESG: Can hydrogen eventually power drones safely?** As a market and society, we have

already discussed and funded the pipeline of making passenger vehicles green, meaning

battery electric powered. The next transport category that is currently the hot topic is

decarbonising HGVs, through either hydrogen or battery electric. However, what is not

widely discussed yet is what will power drones, and this may become an important

discussion point if drones are going to become a USD43bn market by 2025.

There are a few options on the horizon for drones, including gas, battery and hydrogen.

Gas will be able to get the drone further, in terms of distance, but isn’t green, so should

probably be discouraged from the outset. Battery electric power drones are the best bet in

 **ESG: Pressure for 3D production to drive CO2 down:** One of the big theme discussions to

 **ESG: 3D printing in industry can reduce waste, cost and time:** Many industries like

automotive keep spare parts in stock. Not only does this add to the cost but also creates

potential waste. 3D printing using AM techniques has the potential to reduce this waste

going forwards, with lower volume production (say vs. injection moulding as an example).

There can also be cost savings associated with optimised printing from single builds.

For example, a company called Betatype used laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) to create 300+

metal auto parts. Using the optimised system, they produced each part for USD4 vs. USD40+

un-optimised 3D printing. They were also able to reduce the lead times from 444 to 34

hours.54 In another example GE used 3D printing to reduce the part count from their catalyst

turboprop engine from 845 to only 11 – meaning fewer parts to certify. It not only reduced the

cost but also fuel consumption by 20% and reduced the overall engine weight.55

 **ESG: 3D printing and IP issues:** The ability to 3D print a host of things, anywhere in the

world, raises issues of IP. For example, an object can be reverse engineered and made into

a 3D computer model (e.g. an STL file), from using a combination of 3D scanning tools and

design work. This means that teams with skills can replicate almost any object thus

lowering the barrier to entry for manufacturing a variety of widgets. There was an interesting

case in the United States, where a company (ClearCorrect) which made 3D printed

orthodontic aligners, had their 3D model sent over the internet from their overseas internal

designers. Another company (Align Technology), also operating in the United States, filed a

patent infringement against ClearCorrect. The Federal Circuit overturned the initial US ITC

(International Trade Commission) decision that they had no jurisdiction over digital data,

including 3D printing files sent into the United States from overseas, as they can’t be

considered ‘material things’.56

In some areas like medical equipment, countries may face a dilemma going forward; should

existing IP be respected, or should IP laws be changed to adapt to new technologies, to

lower cost for health-related products, for the public good. This kind of easing of patents

has happened previously during wars and also during the current pandemic (for potential

COVID-19 vaccinations). Going forward governments will have to re-visit IP in the digital

economy to balance the public good and the ability to foster investment in innovation via

patents – perhaps even enabling pooling of patents for easier use.

began over a decade of hype around the notion of a decentralised ledger. The idealised and

purest notion of the blockchain is a permission-less ledger, where no entity is in control of the

system – bitcoin and other decentralised crypto currencies are examples of this. On the other

hand, one can have so called permissioned blockchain, where the ledger is owned by a group of

trusted participating players, for example a central bank decentralised currency (CBDC).

fall, there will be increasingly more points in the system that could allow genome

sequencing. Everything from direct-to-consumer sequencing, prospective parents screening

for hereditary illnesses of children, teenagers or adults who get sequenced to understand

their medical issues better.

There is the risk that this data can be used against the person, an example could be for

health or other insurance purposes. For example, 23andme had sold 1m customers’

genomic information to pharmaceutical companies for USD60m in 2015.122 It’s entirely

within the realm of possibility that the initially captured data could land in the hands of third

parties without the permission of the individual, from any number of origin points.

Today in the US, there are federal regulations which bar genetic information from being

used in health insurance (GINA, Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, 2008) but not

against life, disability and long-term care insurance. However, this may not be the case

everywhere (India and China have lower regulatory thresholds for use of genetic

information123), and moreover, regulations can change over time, especially if insurance

lobbying takes place etc.

 **ESG: Implications from genomic data accelerating longer and healthier lives:** The

idea of bespoke medicine using genomic data in a clinical setting is that it can in theory be

preventative medicine, to stop the person being analysed having the on-set of symptoms

and illness. If genomic testing falls below USD100 (from a starting point of USD100m in

people to live longer and healthier lives – another accelerant to the aging population. One

number of care workers required to help look after the elderly population.

 **ESG: Will genomic health care widen the health divide?** There is also the opposite risk

from clinical genomics, that unless the technology is applied on a national level by the

government, that one could see a further splintering of the health system. For example, will

applying clinical genomics to lower the cost of overall healthcare in the UK. However, in

regions like the United States (or even poor countries in the EM), it’s less understood what

the strategy might be going forward, where health systems can be different to the NHS.

 **Genomic health could lead to fewer hospitalisations:** Bespoke and preventative health

care using genomic technology are positive from a patient point of view but widespread use

of this technology could also lead to fewer hospitalisations, lowering incomes for hospitals.

On the flip side, hospitals could be the hub of processing and giving medical treatments

from genomic sequencing data. So in theory, it could work out well for both patients and

hospitals in the longer term.

partnering up with JPMorgan Chase and Berkshire Hathaway (to provide healthcare needs
