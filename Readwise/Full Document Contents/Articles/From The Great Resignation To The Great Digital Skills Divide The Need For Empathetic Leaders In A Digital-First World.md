Designing a next generation workplace for a hybrid workforce

LUCA BRAVO @LUCABRAVO, UNSPLASH
*New research shines a light on the next phase of talent and organizational disruption.*

When the world shifted to remote working and learning overnight, it also accelerated a human digital transformation that permanently changed the trajectory of work and education. People all around the world suddenly had to learn new skills, routines, and disciplines to keep up. Now entering year three of the pandemic, it’s becoming clear that a hybrid digital/physical model represents the next normal. Add to that, since the dawn of Covid-19, the fact that enterprise digital transformation has also [accelerated](https://hbr.org/2021/09/ai-adoption-skyrocketed-over-the-last-18-months) the adoption of AI, machine learning, and automation. Now, employees are faced with an ever-evolving future of work that prioritizes not only resilience, but also digital-first and open mindsets, digital literacy, and continuous learning.

In a world of employers still reeling from the “great resignation,” another wave of workforce disruption appears on the horizon. Next up, is a “great digital divide,” one that exposes the expertise needed to thrive in a digital-first world and those who largely possess pre-pandemic skills. And that divide will upend work sooner than we might think.

*76% of workers do not feel prepared for working in a digital-first world.* 

This is the incredible finding from the [2022 Global Digital Skills](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/salesforce-digital-skills-index-details-major-gaps-across-19-countries/) Index published by Salesforce. The study surveyed over 23,000 workers, ages 18 to 65, across 19 countries between November and December 2021.

#### **Measuring the Digital Skills Divide**

As part of the research process, workers also took part in a Digital Skills Readiness Index. Out of a possible 100 points, workers only scored 33 in fundamental areas such as preparedness, access to learning resources, skill level, and participation in training. Of the included 14 workplace digital skills in the survey, only 17% consider themselves “advanced” in workplace digital skills, while 49% consider themselves “beginner.”

Since the future of work is expected to shift to a hybrid model, the digital skills divide isn’t going to close on its own. If anything, it’s only going to increase in size without attention. In a separate, earlier Salesforce [study](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/tech-skills-research/), 54% of workers believe technology will advance faster than the skills of the workforce. The only way to bridge the gap is to assess the existing divide within the organization between current skills, skills in development, and skills needed in the next five-to-10 years.

What are the key [digital skills](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/what-are-digital-skills/) necessary to thrive in this next chapter of hybrid work? According to the Salesforce report, they are as follows…

Entry-level digital skills include:

· Computer literacy

· Data entry

· Social media

· Web-based communications and research

· Word processing

· Email and chat

· Secure information processing

Advanced digital skills include:

· Programming, web, and app development

· Digital business analysis

· Digital marketing and content creation

· Digital design and data visualization

· Digital product management

· Data science

· User experience design

#### **The Three Major Skills Divides Impeding the Future of Work**

In its research, Salesforce identified three major skills gaps that every business needs to assess and close as they venture toward.

**1) Everyday skills gap**

In a hybrid world, more than 6 in 10 respondents say skills in collaboration technology such as Slack are among the most important skills needed by businesses today and over the next five years. Yet only 25% rated themselves “advanced” in collaboration technology skills needed specifically for a digital-first workplace. And only one-third feel prepared for the workplace social media skills needed over the next five years.

**2) Generational skills gap**

Beyond technology, Salesforce also identified important generational differences. Roughly half (51%) of all respondents said they were interested in acquiring new digital skills, but the desire to take action isn’t similarly shared across age groups. For example, while 83% of Gen-Z respondents, considered the first truly digital native generation, said that they are actively involved in learning new skills, just 12% of baby boomers reported the same. At the same time, few Gen Z respondents believe they have “advanced” digital skills with only 31% feeling “very equipped” for a digital-first job at this point.

**3) Leadership skills gap**

Survey results signal a major disconnect within organizations, one which will prove critical in solving the digital skills divide. The majority of senior leaders (54%) actually believe they are prepared with the necessary digital skills today. In contrast however, less than half of managers and workers agree. This reveals a perilous gap that will only benefit from a sense of humility and urgency for leaders to address the uncomfortable truth, they are not as digitally savvy as they might think.

My colleague [Peter Coffee](https://twitter.com/petercoffee) recently pointed me to this exceptional and apt quote by Jeanne Ross, organizational theorist, principal research scientist at MIT Sloan School of Management, MIT Center for Information Systems Research.

"Leaders must reassess how digital technologies and information can create new customer value,” Ross observed. “The alternative is to try to succeed in a digital economy with a pre-digital value proposition."

Executives are not above or insulated from personal digital transformation. And they can’t lead digital value creation in the new world unless they are versed and competent in doing so. More so, they can’t lead or expect managers to lead teams in a hybrid world if they’re inexperienced. Empathetic leadership starts with understanding what it takes to engage employees and inspire them to excel in this new world and adapting how someone leads accordingly.

A recent article in MITSloan Management Review explored C-Suites and the glaring lack of “[digital smarts](https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/does-your-c-suite-have-enough-digital-smarts/).”

As described by authors Peter Weill, Stephanie L. Woerner, and Aman M. Shah, digital savviness requires "an understanding, developed through experience and education, of the impact that emerging technologies will have on a business's success over the next decade."

However, only 23% of CEOs are considered digitally savvy. And just 7% of large companies currently employ digitally savvy executive teams.

The authors found that executive teams that understand how to wield the power of digital technologies can deliver huge premiums in corporate growth and valuation. Organizations with digitally savvy leaders outperformed peers on revenue growth and valuation by over 48%

#### **Closing the Digital Skills Divide is the Key to Business Growth in the Novel Economy**

All signs point to a reality where the great resignation gives way to a great digital skills divide. Depending on the global response, the economic impact could be significant, for better and worse. In 2021, Salesforce commissioned RAND Europe research to assess the cost of the digital skills gap. It estimated that 14 G20 countries could lose upwards of $11.5 trillion cumulative GDP growth as a result.

The digital skills gap needs immediate attention. But don’t expect it to be a one and done process. AI, cloud, automation, robotics, data-centricity, are upping the need for specific digital skills — by up to 50% in Europe and the United States according to [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/business-functions/operations/our-insights/building-the-vital-skills-for-the-future-of-work-in-operations). Digital transformation has become infinite.

Closing the digital skills divide now and proactively managing it in the future is essential. Following are six steps organizations can follow to evaluate, narrow, and eventually close the digital skills divide.

##### **1) Form a Digital Skills Taskforce**

HR must lead an effort to assess short- (1-5 years) and long-term (5-10 years) trends in skills and partner with functional leads to develop a realistic job requisition roadmap supported by detailed job descriptions.

##### **2) Audit Existing Skill Sets**

Understand the existing digital divide within the organization (including the C-Suite and boards). Plot current states against skills needed for immediate and longer-term trends. As one example, workers [ranked](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/salesforce-digital-skills-index-details-major-gaps-across-19-countries/) these five digital skills as being most critical for businesses to invest in over the next five years: 1) collaboration technology, 2) digital administrative, 3) encryption and cyber security, 4) e-commerce and digital trade, and 5) project management technology.

##### **3) Assess Current Skills Training Programs**

Pre-pandemic learning and development programs are likely in need of upgrading or reimagination to accelerate the convergence of the digital skills divide. Identify areas of success and where existing L&D programs are falling short.

##### **4) Design Innovative Development Initiatives Centered on Equality and Diversity**

While some talent will need to be recruited, inhouse talent can also be cultivated. Both should be done with equality and diversity front and center. L&D leaders will need to assess existing systems for their capacity to solve the skills divide at speed and scale. Sunken cost bias should not prevent investing in the right tools for the job at hand.

Eighty-two percent of workers plan to learn new skills to help them grow in their current career or a new career. Training programs should be designed to be inclusive, to mobilize the workforce, and inspire them to learn, engage, and grow. Look inside and outside the industry for successful examples that leverage AI, machine learning, personalization, UX/UI, and gamification to accelerate the development of individuals and groups at large.

##### **5) Upgrade Leadership Mindsets and Skills**

The C-Suite and boards are not immune to the digital skills divide. To transform, innovate, and drive growth, all leaders must learn and develop not only digital skills, but also people skills to lead teams in an era that transcends pre-pandemic experiences. Empathy, the ability to understand and share the feelings of another, is a critical asset. It helps leaders see and feel the hardships of human digital transformation because they too are part of the existing divide.

Develop programs that create empathetic leaders. Partner executives with Millennials and Centennials as part of reverse mentoring programs. Position executives in scenarios that help them understand industry trends and new skills firsthand. Enlist digital experts and leaders from outside companies to partner with executives and mentor relevant leadership skills.

##### **6) Incentivize People to Change and Grow**

Change is hard because it’s human. Change is often met with resistance.

Why do I need to change? What’s in it for me? These are just a couple of the common questions people have.

Even though employees see that the world is changing, they need help, guidance, and motivation to understand their role in transformation, the new opportunities and benefits that face them, and the skills they need to succeed now and in the future. This comes down to more than L&D programs. Corporate culture also needs an upgrade to define and articulate a new vision forward, empower people to become part of the change, and to incentivize and inspire them to take new steps forward.

The great resignation is just one wave in a set of disruptions that will continue to push companies away from yesterday’s normal. Without a proactive and energetic push across the organization, it may be difficult to attract and retain top digital talent. This could lead a great resignation 2.0 with digitally savvy employees seeking more promising opportunities at digital-first companies.

This is a unique CTRL-ALT-DEL moment, an opportunity to rethink work, employee engagement, and employee experiences (EX) to better connect with employees, customers, and communities, to drive collaborative growth and reshape the future.
