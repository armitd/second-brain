## Full Document
![Reinvention at age 57](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*FenhMR0dKBcHROnDZ1VbiA.jpeg)
At 57, many of my friends are now winding down, planning their retirement, or seeing out the last few years of their careers. In some ways, I envy them, but this is not an option for me both financially and the fact that I still have a raging fire in my belly to make a difference. So I decided 3 months ago to do this: start a local EdTech business in Buckinghamshire.

This is not a side hustle. It's a full-fledged, franchise-backed technology education business for young learners— [ComputerXplorers South Buckinghamshire](https://computerxplorers.co.uk/south-buckinghamshire/). No safety net. Just a belief in the value of tech and IT literacy, a desire to impact young minds, and an appetite to fulfil my own reinvention.

Let me be honest: the first few months have been a mix of excitement, sleep disruption, Excel spreadsheets, imposter syndrome, and serious questions about whether I have what it takes to make it work.

I’m writing this article not because I’ve figured it all out, but to help myself manage the uncertain first 12 months and also to inspire others like me, standing at the cliff edge of their second act in later life, wondering whether or not to leap.

Here are the six biggest steps and lessons learned on the climb towards just starting and hopefully reaching profitability.

### Step 1: Check Your Appetite, then start

Starting a business at 57 isn’t about building a unicorn in Buckinghamshire. It’s about purpose, and still wanting to make a dent in the world, contributing and making my Son proud of me.

But physical fatigue is a real challenge but the last 4 years of Open Water Swimming has really helped me build resilience and strength (thank you [Taplow Lake Swimming community](https://taplowlakeside.co.uk/open-water-swimming/)) so I built the necessary foundations. But for me it's about complete clarity. I got clear about *why* I wanted this, I simply wanted to empower kids through tech, IT and the future and show others that it’s never too late to build something new.

### Step 2: Nail the Numbers, But Don’t Let Them Paralyse You

This is a weakness from my previous business experiences. Cash flow is not a spreadsheet. It’s currently my oxygen.

I quickly learned that enthusiasm for emerging tech tools like chatgpt and Virtual Reality doesn’t pay suppliers or bills. Understanding breakeven points, pricing models, and payment cycles is critical.

I have created two scenarios: My Minimum survival covering all costs and then breakeven. This gives me clarity and helps me avoid anxiety-driven decision-making. But its not easy especially coming up to Summer holidays.

### Step 3: Profitability Starts With Value, Not Scale

In the past, I have been guilty of “scale fast.” That advice doesn’t help when you’re doing your own invoicing, lesson planning, and marketing.

I am focused this time on delivering real value for my first few primary schools in Buckinghamshire: well-prepared, engaging sessions that made kids want to come back and excellent tutors

My first few happy schools will be my core foundation.

### Step 4: Embracing Age and experience— It’s a Superpower

I’ve seen things and failed, and I’ve worked with different personalities. I know how to read a room and have learnt loads of useful skills

I now bring calm, clarity, and compassion to parents and schools. I am reliable, mature adult in a sea of fast-changing education trends.

I don’t plan to hide my age, I just plan to leverage it.

### Step 5: Structure my Week Like a Business Owner, Not an Employee

I have often been guilty of being “reactive” I am having to painfully unlearn this.

I now have strategy and planning, Delivery Days running sessions, and Growth Days (sales, networking, content. That rhythm is the core of my business right now but I know I can get better.

I am and will run your calendar.

### Step 6: Community is the Fuel

Entrepreneurship can sometimes feel isolating, especially if your peers are all talking about pensions, not pitches.

I am liaising daily with my fellow franchisees, local business groups, and my fantastic contacts at Bucks Business First, Bucks College Group and Bucks Council

I am journaling like this, and, yes, speaking to my GenAI buddy for daily accountability (shout-out to Jagger, my digital co-pilot! ). I can’t do this alone.

### Conclusion: It’s Not Too Late

Starting an EdTech business at 57 isn’t about proving anyone wrong. It’s about proving myself right.

It’s harder than I expected, but more rewarding right now than I had imagined. My next challenge is finding a tech venue and some laptops to host my Summer Tech Camps. It will come, I just need to be ultra patient.

So if anyone who is reading this is on the fence like I was in January: if your appetite for impact is still strong, and you’re willing to go deep on mindset, money, and make a difference you’re not too late. In fact, you might even be better and more fulfilled than you ever imagined.
