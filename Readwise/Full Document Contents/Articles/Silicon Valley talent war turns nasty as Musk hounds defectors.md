One recent morning, a former finance executive at Elon Musk’s artificial intelligence company, xAI, received an ominous email from Alex Spiro, Musk’s attack-dog lawyer.

“We have learned of several breaches by you of xAI’s confidentiality obligations. Please cease and desist,” Spiro wrote.

The reply was brief and to the point: a base insult telling Spiro where to go.

The spat was contained in a US lawsuit filed by xAI against rival OpenAI, the developer of ChatGPT. Musk has sued OpenAI alleging trade secrets theft and unfair competition.

Musk’s legal offensive may appear to be the typical litigious behaviour of any billionaire, but it underscores how a growing war for AI talent and a race for superintelligence is boiling over into increasingly acrimonious court battles.

Meta, Google, xAI, OpenAI and other Silicon Valley giants are locked in a brutal war for talent that has vastly inflated pay deals – sometimes spiralling into the hundreds of millions of dollars.

Now, Musk has launched a series of lawsuits against deserters from xAI, which develops X’s Grok chatbot, accusing them of jumping ship to OpenAI and trying to syphon off xAI’s technology.

In the OpenAI lawsuit, Musk has accused the former finance chief and two engineers – Xuechen Li and British engineer Jimmy Fraiture – of defecting to OpenAI and taking valuable information with them.

“OpenAI clearly will do anything when threatened by a better innovator, including plundering and misappropriating the technical advancements, source code, and business plans,” the filings said.

OpenAI denies any wrongdoing and accuses Musk of a “campaign of intimidation”.

Musk’s business has separately sued Li, accusing him of taking files from xAI and “covering his tracks”. A US judge issued a temporary restraining order last month blocking him from joining OpenAI. Li was contacted for comment.

The legal battle has also landed on UK shores after Musk’s company filed a claim against Fraiture at the High Court in London.

The technology firm is suing Fraiture, who was one of the company’s first 50 employees, for £200,000 over claims he had “wrongfully copied, retained, disclosed and/or otherwise misused trade secrets”, court documents show.

According to US filings, Fraiture allegedly downloaded xAI’s source code using the Airdrop feature on his laptop the day before he resigned.

Travers Smith, Fraiture’s lawyers, say he made a “hasty and regrettable” decision, but “vehemently deny” he intended to use the code to benefit OpenAI, according to the US filings.

They say he has since deleted all the data. Fraiture and his lawyers were contacted for comment. The cases are all ongoing.

#### ‘Weapon of choice’

The legal battles come as Musk – [whose net worth last week ballooned to $500bn (£370bn)](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/09/05/elon-musk-could-become-worlds-first-trillionaire-with-tesla/) – fights to stem an exodus of senior staff from his business empire, including Tesla.

Leaders and whole teams have been poached for life-changing sums of money.

Meta even [offered more than $1bn](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/07/30/ai-researcher-turns-down-1bn-pay-offer-mark-zuckerberg/) to poach one researcher from the start-up Thinking Machines earlier this year, reports said. The researcher turned him down, although Meta has called the claims “ridiculous”.

Zuckerberg’s company has also spent billions securing leading AI engineers from rivals and scooped up whole teams from start-ups to lead its Superintelligence Labs under Alexandr Wang, the former boss of ScaleAI.

Musk, meanwhile, has boasted of poaching engineers from Meta who are “joining xAI and without the need for insane initial comp”.

Despite helping to found and fund OpenAI, Musk has been locked in a bitter feud with founder Altman for years after he quit the start-up in 2018. Since then, others have followed Altman out the door.

According to the court filings, eight senior xAI executives and engineers have quit his business for OpenAI. Senior leaders such as David Lau, Tesla’s former head of software, have also joined Altman’s group.

Amid increasing competition between their rival chatbots – ChatGPT and Grok – Musk has accused Altman of anti-competitive behaviour and has attempted to crush its efforts to change its business model to a for-profit. OpenAI and Altman deny the claims.

Some former employees of Musk have criticised his lurch to the right and his meddling in elections. Others have struggled to keep up with his relentless work ethic.

Rob Keele, xAI’s former top lawyer, recently said there was “daylight between our world views” compared to Musk, adding he had to choose between “the family and the job”.

Separately, Linda Yaccarino, the chief executive of the social network X, [announced she was resigning in the summer.](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/07/09/x-boss-quits-days-elon-musk-launches-new-us-political-party/)

With so much money changing hands in the AI talent war, lawyers expect more work to follow. Sarah Tishler, a partner at Beck Reed Ridden, said trade secret claims had become “the weapon of choice” in US employee disputes.

In California, more traditional non-compete clauses are difficult to enforce, she says.

OpenAI, however, argues in a legal filing that the case against it is not really about trade secrets.

On Thursday, OpenAI said Musk’s aggressive pursuit of leavers amounted to bullying his former employees.

It also alleged that some staff exits “coincide with a series of incidents this summer” when Musk’s Grok spouted anti-Semitic responses to users. xAI did not respond to requests for comment.

“To be clear: OpenAI does not need or want anyone’s – much less xAI’s – trade secrets,” the Silicon Valley lab said.

“This case is clearly designed to generate publicity to bully and threaten those employees who exercised their right to leave and work elsewhere in the AI industry and to try to chill further flight from xAI.”

According to OpenAI, in the AI war “xAI’s employees are fleeing”.
