#####  Uğur Şahan

######  Founding Partner at CoreXas Consulting Group | Head of Strategy, Innovation and R&D at Twinseye Technology | Futurist | Innovator | Foresight Strategist

In today’s world, technological innovations are triggering profound transformations across all facets of life, from business to society, and are becoming a decisive factor in long-term success and sustainability. Positioned at the core of strategic planning processes, technology foresight reduces uncertainty about the future and enables organizations to take proactive and informed steps. However, these processes are not without risk. One of the most critical risks is the presence of so-called “false signals,” misleading or overhyped technological trends. Although these signals may initially appear highly promising, they often fail to deliver long-term value or mature at the anticipated pace. As such, they can severely distort organizational strategy and investment decisions, leading to consequential missteps.

Historical analyses of technological innovation reveal that false signals tend to emerge during periods when expectations are rising rapidly, yet real-world applications remain limited. Organizations drawn in by these signals may invest at scale, resulting in the misallocation of financial resources, weakened internal motivation, and diminished confidence in strategic decision-making. Therefore, foresight processes must not only identify potential innovations but also rigorously assess their impact, timing, and feasibility within realistic parameters.

##### Technological Mirages

Technology foresight involves systematic analysis processes aimed at identifying emerging technologies, mapping their potential areas of impact, and projecting their practical applicability. Supported by advanced methodologies such as Delphi studies, scenario planning, technology road mapping, and trend analysis, these processes can be influenced by external forces in the early stages, media attention, investor pressure, and popular narratives often distort perceptions beyond the underlying technological reality. This leads to the formation of seemingly strong but ultimately weak “mirages” or false signals.

The dot-com bubble of the late 1990s is a typical example of such a signal, stemming from a misinterpretation of the internet technology’s potential. Similarly, in the early 2000s, the so-called “nanotechnology revolution” failed to deliver the anticipated economic impact due to premature assessments of market readiness. More recently, the fields of blockchain and artificial intelligence have seen exaggerated expectations, pushing organizations toward unrealistic investments.

These misleading scenarios arise from the non-linear nature of technological maturity and the conflation of technical potential with broader socio-economic effects. For innovations to create actual value, they must be supported not only by technical capability but also by favorable market dynamics, regulatory compliance, and user adoption. Ignoring these elements fuels the strength and persistence of false signals.

##### Strategic Deviation

The most detrimental impact of false signals is the misdirection of institutional resources. Overinflated expectations may prompt premature investment in immature technologies, leading to financial loss, strategic disorientation, and depletion of capital needed for future, more viable innovations.

When the inflated promises of these signals fail to materialize, they often generate broader skepticism toward innovation. Unmet expectations from leadership and investors can cultivate an overly cautious or even distrustful approach toward future technologies. This not only erodes a company’s competitive edge but also undermines its innovation culture.

Furthermore, organizations heavily influenced by false signals risk overlooking less conspicuous but potentially more impactful technologies. This opportunity cost can significantly affect long-term growth and competitiveness. Telecommunication companies that underestimated early mobile technologies serve as a cautionary example, having ceded market leadership and missed critical strategic opportunities.

##### Interdisciplinary Realism

To guard against the influence of false signals, foresight must extend beyond technical analysis to encompass sociological, economic, and regulatory dimensions. Gartner’s “Hype Cycle” offers a useful model for a more realistic assessment of technological maturity, providing valuable input for strategic decision-making.

However, the effectiveness of interdisciplinary analysis requires more than empirical data. Organizational behavior, cognitive biases, and public perception must also be considered. The gap between public perception and real technological potential can be substantial, especially in the early stages when media narratives and investor enthusiasm may lead to collective delusions. Therefore, foresight processes must be carried out not only by engineers and financial analysts but also by social scientists, ethicists, economists, and public policy experts within a multidisciplinary framework.

Evaluating a technology’s maturity also requires attention to external adoption and ecosystem alignment. Factors such as regulatory gaps, consumer habits, and economic scalability may hinder even technically feasible technologies from achieving widespread deployment. Artificial intelligence, for example, may be progressing rapidly from a technical standpoint, but unresolved issues around data privacy, ethics, and legal frameworks continue to limit its broader implementation. Unless organizations develop the capability to assess technologies in a multi-layered context, they remain vulnerable to the influence of false signals.

##### Beyond the Image: Strategic Reflex

False signals pose not only temporary strategic risks but also long-term structural vulnerabilities. Hence, the purpose of foresight should not be limited to predicting the future; it must focus on understanding the nature of uncertainty and cultivating systemic agility to respond to it. Foresight strategies should be structured to detect false signals early, thereby ensuring the prudent allocation of financial resources, safeguarding decision quality, and sustaining trust in innovation. True foresight is about navigating ambiguity with clarity. Institutions unable to develop this capacity will find their competitive edge eroding in the age of digital transformation.

Consequently, managing false signals demands a holistic strategic framework powered by technology radars, scenario analyses, and interdisciplinary impact assessments. Such a framework must focus not only on the technology itself, but also on its surrounding cultural, political, and economic context. Organizations that embrace this approach will not only make sounder investment decisions but will also reinforce their long-term strategic vision and resilience in the face of volatility. Ultimately, interpreting the future accurately depends on the ability to distinguish transient hype from enduring value, and that capability will define which institutions lead tomorrow’s transformations.
