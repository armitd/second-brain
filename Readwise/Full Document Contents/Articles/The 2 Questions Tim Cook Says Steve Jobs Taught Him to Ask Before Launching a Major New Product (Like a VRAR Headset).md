A new [profile of Tim Cook](https://www.gq.com/story/tim-cook-global-creativity-awards-cover-2023) in *GQ* magazine seems like as sure a sign as any that Apple is getting ready to launch some version of an [augmented reality (AR) headset](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/3-reasons-why-apples-upcoming-vr-headset-could-be-end-of-facebook.html). While [Cook wouldn’t confirm](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/why-tim-cooks-50-million-pay-cut-is-his-smartest-leadership-move-yet.html) to GQ that an announcement was imminent, or even that Apple is working on such a device, the very fact that he talked so candidly about it seems like the kind of thing you only do if you’re [getting ready to unveil something big](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/tim-cooks-most-important-presentation-rule-is-simple-less-tim-cook.html).

Cook even described how he evolved from a skeptic about the idea of people wearing computers on their faces, into someone who might be stepping on a stage in just a few weeks to announce what some people think could be the most transformative piece of technology since the iPhone.

“My thinking always evolves,” Cook told GQ. “Steve taught me well: never to get married to your convictions of yesterday… If presented with something new that says you were wrong, admit it and go forward instead of continuing to hunker down and say why you’re right.”

Getting there, however, has been an interesting process, and Cook says it’s guided by two questions [he learned from Jobs](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/tim-cook-still-keeps-this-habit-he-learned-from-steve-jobs-why-every-leader-should-try-it.html):

“Can we make a significant contribution, in some kind of way, something that other people are not doing? Can we own the primary technology? I’m not interested in putting together pieces of somebody else’s stuff. Because we want to control the primary technology. Because we know that’s how you innovate.”

Of course, Cook wasn’t just talking about a new headset. Those two questions are at the core of how Apple thinks about launching every new product. They’re also a powerful lesson for every company.

First, Apple only wants to enter a new product category if it feels like it can make a unique contribution. It’s not interested in following along just to do what everyone else is doing already. For it to be worth launching something new, Apple has to have its own take.

The iPhone is the most obvious example. There were smartphones before [Steve Jobs stood on stage in 2007](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/steve-jobs-once-prank-called-a-starbucks-its-an-incredible-lesson-in-emotional-intelligence.html) and unveiled what has since become [the most iconic product](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/these-are-4-words-steve-jobs-used-to-change-everything.html) of all time. The iPhone was different from all of the smartphones that came before. Apple certainly made a significant contribution because it made something very different. It wasn’t just a smartphone, it was the iPhone.

Contrast that with a company like Meta. Sometimes it seems as though every product decision it makes is [in response to the competition](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/mark-zuckerberg-cant-stop-copying-competition-why-this-time-it-could-be-end-of-facebook.html). Instagram Stories and Reels are direct copies of Snapchat and TikTok. Its high-end Quest Pro headset is meant to anticipate what Apple is doing and try to beat it to market.

As for the second question, Apple believes it shouldn’t do something unless it’s able to do it all the way. It wants to “control the primary technology.” That means it doesn’t primarily build on someone else’s platform. Apple wants to control the platform. That’s its core business.

Sure, it’s good at making devices, but what it really wants you to buy is its ecosystem. Apple wants you to buy iPhones and an Apple Watch, and AirPods, and a MacBook Air because they all work together seamlessly.

More importantly, Apple wants you to fill all of those devices with software from its App Stores. Apple also wants you to buy AppleCare, and purchase iCloud storage, and sign up for Apple Music. All of those services run on Apple’s platform–the primary technology.

When you think about it, those two questions are a powerful lesson for every leader. Your job isn’t just to make more stuff. Your job is to ask yourself whether your company has something to contribute–something different from your competition. It’s to ask whether you’re able to do something better that adds value to your customers.
