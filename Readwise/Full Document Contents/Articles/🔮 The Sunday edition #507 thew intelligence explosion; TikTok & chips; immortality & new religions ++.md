##### An insider's view on AI and exponential technologies

Hi, Azeem here. 

“It just keeps going up... and up... and up”. That’s how famed AI blogger Gwern describes the remarkable acceleration in AI capabilities. Today’s edition examines this potential intelligence explosion alongside the UK’s ambitious new AI strategy – a 10-year plan that could redefine how nations approach AI. It’s the best government plan I’ve seen so far. 

This and more to get into in today’s weekend edition of Exponential View!

---

###### **Are we on the edge of an intelligence explosion?**

[In my essay earlier this month](https://substack.com/redirect/d1e0992b-5e51-4e58-ba1a-f757cc1f98ce?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), I argued that we should take Sam Altman and other industry insiders’ claims about imminent, transformative AI capabilities by 2025 quite seriously. The evidence is there in massive infrastructure investments and in experts’ increasingly compressed timelines. [AI analyst Gwern](https://substack.com/redirect/f54979cc-2e0b-4703-a590-4a06db59857f?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) highlights a powerful data flywheel effect taking shape, where each new model spawns data for training an even better one:

> Every problem that an o1 solves is now a training data point for an o3 (eg. any o1 session which finally stumbles into the right answer can be refined to drop the dead ends and produce a clean transcript to train a more refined intuition).

He also notes that: 

> If you’re wondering why OAers are suddenly weirdly, almost euphorically, optimistic on Twitter, watching the improvement from the original 4o model to o3 (and wherever it is now!) may be why. It’s like watching the AlphaGo Elo curves: it just keeps going up… and up… and up…

> There may be a sense that they’ve ‘broken out’, and have finally crossed the last threshold of criticality, from merely cutting-edge AI work which everyone else will replicate in a few years, to takeoff – cracked intelligence to the point of being recursively self-improving and where o4 or o5 will be able to automate AI R&D and finish off the rest.

So what to make of this? Unlike Gwern, I wouldn’t use the term “broken out”. It is too laden with allusions to [the containment problem](https://substack.com/redirect/bf73ed64-43ad-463e-9fa1-c5cf1e5d9058?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM). Instead, I see AI as a tool that can improve the creation of the next generation of AI tools — possibly to a point where the self-reinforcing flywheel truly speeds up.

###### **God save the Machine**

We now have a high-level plan for AI by the UK – one that, rather than merely fearing the technology (EU) or fixating on Beijing (US), seeks to shape its trajectory. The UK government is placing a large bet on [“enabling and embracing” AI](https://substack.com/redirect/6f9e8214-aaf0-49ef-b18f-62e68d71eccb?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), with a decade-long roadmap for infrastructure. This includes a proposed 20-fold expansion of state-backed computing power and “AI Growth Zones” to anchor new data centres. Just as significant is the National Data Library’s pledge to release at least five “high-impact” datasets to spur AI development. 

There are many more excellent initiatives, including making it much easier to build both the electricity systems and the infrastructure required for data centres and doubling down on industrial centres of excellence for this new technology. 

The plan, put together by Matt Clifford, a long-time Exponential View friend, is a comprehensive statement of intent about the importance of industrial renewal and the attention a new general-purpose technology requires to prepare society for its deployment. Of course, the benefits will depend on the exact nature of implementation, but as far as government plans go – **this is the best I’ve seen so far!**

###### **A two-front confrontation**

In the past few days, America’s technological and cultural stand-off with China has matured. We’re amid another milestone in techno-geopolitical fragmentation. The tensions have been apparent for years but the speed with which they’re now playing out is accelerating.

As it concludes its last days in power, the Biden administration intensified export controls on high-end chips that power advanced AI research. At the same time, TikTok lost its Supreme Court appeal, possibly one of the most important First Amendment cases of our time. 

TikTok’s owner, ByteDance, was given nine months to respond to the legislation prohibiting social media platforms “controlled by a foreign adversary” from operating in the United States unless sold to domestic or allied buyers. Freedom-of-speech advocates, including the Electronic Frontier Foundation and the Freedom of the Press Foundation, lobbied against what they see as a heavy-handed approach that punishes users more than it addresses real privacy or data security concerns. Yet America’s stance reflects a pattern of decoupling first seen in India’s 2020 ban of TikTok. [At the time](https://substack.com/redirect/077cebec-6203-40b2-ba3d-64fb0ab35a9b?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), I wrote:

> Global decoupling is not just a gruelling sparring session between China and America. 

And again, [in 2020](https://substack.com/redirect/d37f65bd-cc5e-466a-ba19-03b38e510fb6?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM):

> The free, end-to-end internet has always sat uneasily with national sovereignty. And nations have at various times, and increasingly, used the arguments of privacy, crime, competition and the ultimate stick, national security, to mediate Internet services in their borders. 

This time, though, the geopolitical context is deeper. The Chinese government regards TikTok’s recommendation engine as a prized strategic asset. Its diplomats have [lambasted Washington’s threats](https://substack.com/redirect/5a9bbe52-704e-4e06-bd57-622b82de5666?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) as “an act of bullying”, “an abuse of national power” and another instance of Western double standards. Nor does it help that platforms like TikTok – built around short videos and fast content switching – may [impair users’ memory](https://substack.com/redirect/20d12da9-f722-4c5a-bb3b-5692b2655f80?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) and cognition. Still, American sentiment is hardly unified: almost [80% of Americans distrust](https://substack.com/redirect/302cf1d7-4c7d-4869-b882-2baff298287c?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) their own government and the most downloaded iPhone app in the US last week was [Xiaohongshu](https://substack.com/redirect/13d9b0fc-1804-469a-a468-97b69adb6754?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), a Chinese social platform that many embraced partly in protest against the ban.

Meanwhile, the Biden administration has [broadened export controls](https://substack.com/redirect/39b3e9f5-e024-4c85-ba03-046a1d06bb88?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) on AI chips, clamping down on the grey-market channels that China has used to circumvent earlier restrictions. These measures divide the world into three tiers, imposing strict limits on GPU purchases for lower-tier countries. Notably, half of NATO’s members find themselves lumped into the same category as Pakistan, Mexico and Yemen, a reflection of how sweeping and blunt Washington’s approach has become…

###### **Hope amid decline**

If the US and UK hope to maintain the same rise in GDP per capita that they’ve achieved over the past 25 years, productivity will have to expand at [2.3 times the trend rate of 2012–2023](https://substack.com/redirect/d6fc6b40-45bf-48dc-baed-b3a060b485a1?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM).The story is much the same across other advanced economies, where falling fertility rates create headwinds to growth. Most of these countries will need to double or even quadruple their current productivity gains to cope with demographic pressures over the next century.

Could AI help? A [paper by the OECD](https://substack.com/redirect/717e8344-2b61-4c59-9978-8de029d3f846?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) sees AI as a likely source of meaningful (on the order of half a percentage point) productivity gains. But today’s AI alone probably will *not* deliver productivity booms on the scale of mid‐20th‐century leaps. It’s worth noting that estimates in the paper reflect capabilities as of the studies’ publication. Future developments—like [advanced agents](https://substack.com/redirect/8cb66b28-d072-422d-8c71-f64a979d28c1?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) or integration with robotics—may lift productivity beyond the levels currently projected.

**Materials discovery.** Microsoft researchers released a new AI model [MatterGen](https://substack.com/redirect/771649f4-5630-44e3-961d-6535a00f3420?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) that can create designs for new materials. They trained the model to “understand” the relationships between different chemical elements and their properties. Materials created by MatterGen are twice as likely to be novel and stable than previous AI models.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2800,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3d445677-9beb-4e05-be42-647845d0e2c1_1400x788.heic)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3d445677-9beb-4e05-be42-647845d0e2c1_1400x788.heic)Source: Microsoft**Adaptable AI.** Sakana.AI have developed [Transformer²](https://substack.com/redirect/b758fa25-21e2-4daa-a745-fadeaf146cee?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), an AI architecture designed to let language models adjust their internal workings rapidly for different tasks. By dividing an LLM’s “brain” into components and selectively strengthening or weakening them, the system can adapt to new challenges without retraining. This is a really exciting area. The vision is to create AI systems that can function more like living organisms, continuously adapting, learning and evolving to meet new challenges, rather than remaining static after training.

*See also:* In a nod to open research, the NovaSky team at UC Berkeley has unveiled [Sky-T1-32B-Preview](https://substack.com/redirect/4cdc7fb2-b3a9-4066-b200-333d70c3e5dd?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) – a fully open-source reasoning model that competes with o1-preview on maths and coding tasks, all trained for less than $450.

**AI and physics.** Most [current AI video models show limited understanding of physics principles](https://substack.com/redirect/20d12da9-f722-4c5a-bb3b-5692b2655f80?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), even when they create videos that look realistic. Some basic concepts can be learned just from observing videos, but there isn’t a direct connection between how realistic a video looks and whether the AI understands the physics behind what it’s showing. 

**The Blueprint religion.** In his debut week on Substack, journalist [Ashlee Vance](https://open.substack.com/users/307831456-ashlee-vance?utm_source=mentions) (👋 welcome [Ashlee Vance](https://open.substack.com/users/307831456-ashlee-vance?utm_source=mentions)!) brings to life [the religion Bryan Johnson is building](https://substack.com/redirect/c87de61f-b6f4-4086-b3d8-38ad912b56b1?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) under his goal of not dying. Ashlee writes: 

> If AI really hits the takeoff point and goes Singularity mode, I imagine there will be great religious upheavals as people try to find meaning and to adjust to all-knowing computers in the sky. Blueprint seems like an early stab at this.

*See also*: the reliance on ultra-processed foods in the American diet has made processed chemicals, not nutrients, the [cornerstone of eating](https://substack.com/redirect/c60da78b-fc79-4937-bef3-ddf13274ef69?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM).

**Humans are remarkably good at continuing to exploit** any viable resource. Even when synthetic nitrogen became mainstream, the [Chilean caliche industry cut costs](https://substack.com/redirect/5825f432-2240-41d3-bc9d-12dc71d47f8f?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM), streamlined operations and still found ways to turn a profit. Large-scale caliche mining and refining continue to this day only with fewer workers and more advanced technology. via [Ed Conway](https://open.substack.com/users/115207446-ed-conway?utm_source=mentions)

**Reversible computing.** EV member Rodolfo Rosini’s team at Vaire Computing (disclaimer: I’m an investor) is close to launching a near-zero energy chip. We wrote about [reversible computing](https://substack.com/redirect/d107c52e-3378-49af-bc13-b6970ad196bf?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) before – this week a [great YT video came out](https://substack.com/redirect/d7a0f12f-42ae-401f-86a9-df71a66a704a?j=eyJ1IjoiajF4MSJ9.M9Q5duGbk5HzCqNmuRbfRflKywh75T0AwXf1RA-czAM) explaining the technology behind Rodolfo’s work. It’s a must-watch.
