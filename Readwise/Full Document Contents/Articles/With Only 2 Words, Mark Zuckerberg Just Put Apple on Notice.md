## Full Document
We’re about to see whose vision of the future is more likely to end up on our faces.

![zuckerberg_meta_connect_orion](https://img-cdn.inc.com/image/upload/f_webp,c_fit,w_1920,q_auto/images/panoramic/zuckerberg_meta_connect_orion_545413_zhgkbv.jpg)

In general, there’s a delicate balance between a company wanting to [get people excited about whatever it’s working on](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/metas-quest-3-announcement-makes-it-official-apple-is-living-rent-free-in-mark-zuckerbergs-head.html) and keeping products secret until they are ready to ship. Generally, the only time a company will lean in the direction of talking about things in advance is when it feels some measure of external pressure to show its hand or risk appearing behind.

If all you ever do is show off concepts–but never ship anything–you lose credibility. You might show off impressive technology, but, eventually, no one will believe that you can turn it into products. You also [tip off your competition,](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/mark-zuckerbergs-response-to-vision-pro-gets-1-thing-right-its-bad-news-for-apple.html) who might decide to ship something of their own.

This week, at its developer event, Meta’s CEO, Mark Zuckerberg, unveiled a pair of [augmented reality glasses called Orion](https://www.theverge.com/24253908/meta-orion-ar-glasses-demo-mark-zuckerberg-interview). The glasses are a sort of proof-of-concept that Zuckerberg says the company has been working on for a decade. More importantly, they are–by all accounts–impressive.

Unlike most virtual reality headsets, some of which include cameras for pass-through video to allow a user to interact with the world around them, Orion are actual glasses, with transparent lenses. The lenses feature a complex system of projectors and waveguides, which allow them to overlay apps and information within your view of the world.

They are a dramatically different form factor from say a Quest headset or Apple’s Vision Pro. And, according to Zuckerberg, that’s the point.

“They need to be glasses, not a headset, no wires, less than 100 grams,” [Zuckerberg said](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I7JyydkqDeI). “They need wide field of view, holographic displays. Sharp enough to pick up details, bright enough to see in different lighting conditions, large enough to display a cinema screen or multiple monitors for working wherever you go, whether you’re in a coffee shop or on a plane or wherever you are, and you need to be able to see through them. And people need to be able to see that through them too, and make eye contact with you. This isn’t pass through, this is the physical world with holograms overlaid on it.”

There is so much going on in that paragraph, but the biggest thing that jumped out at me is two words, right in the middle of the first sentence: “no wires.” The glasses connect wirelessly to a small puck that you can fit in your pocket. The puck handles all of the compute for apps or notifications and has a direct, low-latency wireless connection with the glasses. Zuckerberg also explained that in addition to eye tracking, they included a wristband that uses neural tracking to control the interface. That means you can interact with apps or information without taking your hands out of your pocket.

But, the “no wires” part is actually a really big deal. Apple made very different choices with Vision Pro, one of them being that it is tethered to a battery. There is no question that Zuckerberg was taking a direct shot at Apple and Vision Pro when he called out that you can use Orion without any wires, and–even more so–when he pointed out that Orion isn’t using pass-through video, but rather just lets you see the real world.

Look, I think Vision Pro is an incredibly useful computing platform for a lot of use cases. I’ve used mine every day since I’ve had it, and I find it helps me be productive. The problem is, it’s heavy and it never leaves my house.

There is a sense that Apple made the wrong choices with Vision Pro, which is essentially a very high-end virtual reality headset. It’s not that it’s a bad product. It’s just that Apple made the very best version of the wrong thing. What Meta just showed off is, unquestionably, an incomplete version of the actual thing everyone wants.

To be fair, Orion is not shipping. It currently costs Meta around $10,000 to produce, which means it is nowhere near becoming a product. A large part of that cost is in the lens and display system, which is made of silicon carbide. The key thing, however, is that Meta has shown it can be done. That’s something no other company has been able–or, at least, willing–to demonstrate.

Not only that, but we have no idea what Apple is working on–it doesn’t talk about its concepts. I’d be incredibly surprised if Apple doesn’t have a dozen or so variations on the theme Zuckerberg demonstrated this week–it just isn’t willing to show them to anyone yet.

There is no question that [Zuckerberg’s life mission](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/mark-zuckerberg-is-picking-fights-with-apple-again-tim-cooks-response-is-a-brilliant-lesson-in-emotional-intelligence.html) at this point is for Meta to control its own destiny. For the past 17 years, his company has been at the mercy of Apple and Google, which control the smartphone. That means its [entire business can be upended](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/mark-zuckerbergs-year-of-efficiency-at-meta-is-getting-brutal-for-everyone-but-mark-zuckerberg.html) when one of those companies decides to [change the rules.](https://www.inc.com/jason-aten/apples-ios-145-is-imminent-what-it-means-for-your-iphone-your-privacy.html) Zuckerberg desperately wants to break free from the smartphone, and believes that AR glasses are what will make that happen.

The more obvious outcome is that he just put Apple on notice that Meta is getting close, which will motivate Apple to push forward with whatever it’s working on. When that happens, you can be sure your glasses won’t tether to a puck, they’ll tether to an iPhone. If nothing else, we’re closer than ever to seeing whose vision of the future we’re all about to wear on our faces.
