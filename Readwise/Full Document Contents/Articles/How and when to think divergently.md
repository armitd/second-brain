## Full Document
##### It's easier than you think(!)

Creativity doesn’t occur by chance; it is crafted through a balance of divergent and convergent thinking. Divergent thinking focuses on generating ideas without judgment, while convergent thinking refines those ideas into actionable solutions. Knowing how and when to use each approach can reshape problem-solving and foster innovation.

**Divergent thinking involves casting the mental net as widely as possible.** Brainstorming without limits, freewriting without editing, and welcoming the chaos of raw ideas are central to this process. Most ideas will fail, but failure is not a flaw—it is essential. The process allows you to sift through possibilities, leading to the one concept that could redefine the problem. Many struggle with this approach, largely because modern education emphasises correctness over exploration, conditioning individuals to fear mistakes rather than embrace them.

Overcoming the resistance to divergent thinking can be transformative. The tendency to focus solely on outcomes often obscures the iterative, often chaotic, path to creativity. Survivorship bias further reinforces the false notion that others create effortlessly, as we rarely see the countless discarded ideas behind any successful output.

**Convergent thinking, the counterpoint to divergence, analyses and evaluates ideas.** The process focuses on narrowing options, applying constraints, and honing in on solutions. Divergence expands; convergence refines. Neither should dominate a creative process. Together, they form a dynamic cycle that fuels progress.

###### Try this exercise when stuck (or starting out)

One effective method to integrate both approaches involves moving through distinct stages. Start by deepening knowledge, defining the problem, and researching extensively. Once the groundwork is set, shift into divergence, generating ideas freely without judgement. Consider what might be possible if resources, time, or constraints were removed. Following this, employ convergent thinking to evaluate the ideas and identify those most viable. Finally, return to divergence, refining or iterating on selected ideas to explore hidden potential.

Many mess up by stopping too early, or becoming overly attached to the first workable idea (hello, me). Pushing beyond this stage often reveals opportunities that elevate outcomes from good to exceptional. Creative processes thrive when people embrace discomfort, challenge habits, and switch between divergent and convergent modes intentionally.

For those naturally inclined to one type of thinking, deliberate practice is key. Building comfort with divergent thought often requires unlearning ingrained aversions to mistakes, while sharpening convergent skills may involve training in structured evaluation and analysis. Strengthening both muscles ensures flexibility in tackling any creative challenge.

Divergent thinking isn't just producing ideas; it is about embracing risk and uncertainty to uncover new possibilities. Creativity relies on the willingness to navigate ambiguity and reject the impulse to judge too quickly. Combined with the focus and precision of convergence, it becomes possible to generate bold ideas and transform them into impactful solutions.
