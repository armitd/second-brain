## Full Document

**High-resolution audio** (**high-definition audio** or **HD audio**) is a term for audio files with greater than [44.1 kHz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/44,100_Hz) [sample rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampling_(signal_processing)) or higher than 16-bit [audio bit depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audio_bit_depth). It commonly refers to 96 or 192 kHz sample rates. However, there also exist 44.1 kHz/24-bit, 48 kHz/24-bit and 88.2 kHz/24-bit recordings that are labeled HD Audio.

Research into high resolution audio began in the late 1980s and high resolution audio content started to become available on the consumer market in 1996.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-melchior-1)

#### Definitions

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/High-resolution_audio.svg/300px-High-resolution_audio.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:High-resolution_audio.svg)
Approximate dynamic range and bandwidths of some high-resolution audio formats

High-resolution audio is generally used to refer to music files that have a higher sampling frequency and/or bit depth than that of [Compact Disc Digital Audio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_Disc_Digital_Audio) (CD-DA), which operates at 44.1 kHz/16-bit.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-giz0613-2)

The [Recording Industry Association of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America) (RIAA), in cooperation with the [Consumer Electronics Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Association), DEG: The Digital Entertainment Group, and The Recording Academy Producers & Engineers Wing, formulated the following definition of high-resolution audio in 2014: "[lossless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lossless_compression) audio capable of reproducing the full spectrum of sound from recordings which have been mastered from better than CD quality (48 kHz/20-bit or higher) music sources which represent what the artists, producers and engineers originally intended."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-3)

File formats capable of storing high-resolution audio include [FLAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FLAC), [ALAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_lossless), [WAV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WAV), [AIFF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIFF) and [DSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_Stream_Digital), the format used by [Super Audio Compact Discs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Audio_Compact_Disc) (SACD).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-what0313-4)

#### History

[![Super Audio CD](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SACDlogo.svg/175px-SACDlogo.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Audio_CD)
[![DVD-Audio](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/DVD-Audio_Logo.svg/175px-DVD-Audio_Logo.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DVD-Audio)
One of the first attempts to market high-resolution audio was [High Definition Compatible Digital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Definition_Compatible_Digital) in 1995.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-russo-5) This was followed by three more [optical disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_disc) formats claiming sonic superiority over CD-DA: DAD in 1998, [SACD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Audio_CD) in 1999, and [DVD-Audio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DVD-Audio) in 2000. These formats offer additional benefits such as multi-channel [surround sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surround_sound). Following a [format war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Format_war), none of these achieved widespread adoption.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-pcmag-6)

Following the rise in online music retailing at the start of the 21st century, high-resolution audio downloads were introduced by [HDtracks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HDtracks) starting in 2008.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-pcmag-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-7)

Further attempts to market high-resolution audio on optical disc followed with Pure Audio [Blu-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blu-ray) in 2009, and [High Fidelity Pure Audio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Fidelity_Pure_Audio) in 2013.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-dvdab-8) Competition in online high-resolution audio retail stepped-up in 2014 with the announcement of [Neil Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_Young)'s [Pono](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pono_(digital_music_service)) service.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-forbes0314-9)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Hi-res-audio-logo.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hi-res-audio-logo.png)Official logo for products meeting JEITA/JAS's "Hi-Res Audio" specification.
In 2014, the Japan Electronics and Information Technology Industries Association (JEITA) announced a specification and accompanying "Hi-Res AUDIO" logo for consumer audio products, administered by the Japan Audio Society (JAS). The standard sets minimums of 96 kHz sample rate and 24-bit depth, and for analog processes, 40 kHz.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-hi-res-10) The related "Hi-Res Audio Wireless" standard additionally requires support for the [LDAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LDAC_(codec)), [LHDC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LHDC_(codec)), [LC3plus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LC3_(codec)) and [MQair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_Quality_Authenticated) codecs.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-hi-res-10) Sony reaffirmed its commitment towards the development in the high-resolution audio segment by offering a slew of Hi-Res Audio products.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-11)

#### Streaming services

As of 2021, several [music streaming services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streaming_media) such as [Tidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_(service)), [Amazon Music HD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_Music), and [Apple Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Music) all have hi-res audio options.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-12)

#### Controversy

See also: [Super Audio CD § Sound quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Audio_CD#Sound_quality)

Whether there is any benefit to high-resolution audio over CD-DA is controversial, with some sources claiming sonic superiority:

* "The DSD process used for producing SACDs captures more of the nuances from a performance and reproduces them with a clarity and transparency not possible with CD."[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-13)—The [Mariinsky record label](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariinsky_Theatre#The_Mariinsky_record_label) of the [Mariinsky Ballet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariinsky_Ballet) (formerly [Kirov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergey_Kirov) Ballet), [St. Petersburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Petersburg), [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia), that sells [Super Audio CDs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Audio_CD) (SACDs)
* "The main claimed benefit of high-resolution audio files is superior sound quality [...] 24-bit/96 kHz or 24-bit/192 kHz files should, therefore, more closely replicate the sound quality that the musicians and engineers were working with in the studio."—[What Hi-Fi?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What_Hi-Fi%3F_Sound_and_Vision)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-what0313-4)
* "...music professionals with access to first generation data have widely reported subjectively better sound, and a meta-analysis of previously published listening tests comparing high resolution to CD found a clear, though small, audible difference that significantly increased when the listening tests included standard training (i.e., with experience in listening)."—Journal of the [Audio Engineering Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audio_Engineering_Society), Volume 67, Issue 5[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-melchior-1)

...and with other opinions ranging from skeptical to highly critical:

* "If [the music business] cared about sound quality in the first place, they would make all of the releases sound great in every format they sell: MP3, FLAC, CD, iTunes, or LP."—[cnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNET)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-cnet-14)
* "Impractical overkill that nobody can afford"—[Gizmodo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gizmodo)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-giz0613-2)
* "A solution to a problem that doesn't exist, a business model based on willful ignorance and scamming people."—[Xiph.org](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiph.org)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-xiph_24_192-15)

Business magazine *[Bloomberg Businessweek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_Businessweek)* suggests that caution is in order with regard to high-resolution audio: "There is reason to be wary, given consumer electronics companies' history of pushing advancements whose main virtue is to require everyone to buy new gadgets."[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-bloom-16)

High-resolution files that are downloaded from niche websites that cater to [audiophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audiophile) listeners often include different [mastering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mastering_(audio)) in the release – thus many comparisons of CD to these releases are evaluating differences in mastering, rather than bit depth.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-17)

Most early papers using blind listening tests concluded that differences are not audible by the sample of listeners taking the test.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-drew-18) [Blind tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blind_test) have shown that musicians and composers are unable to distinguish higher resolutions from 16-bit audio at 48 kHz.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-19) One 2014 paper showed that [dithering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dither) using outdated methods[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-20) produces audible artifacts in blind listening tests.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-Stuart-21)

A 2007 study showed no audio difference between CD quality audio and higher resolution audio.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-drew-18) [Joshua Reiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshua_Reiss) performed a meta-analysis on 20 of published tests, saying that trained listeners could distinguish between hi-resolution recordings and their CD equivalents under blind conditions.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-reiss-22) Hiroshi Nittono pointed out that the results in Reiss's paper showed that the ability to distinguish hi resolution audio from CD quality audio "was only slightly better than chance".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_audio#cite_note-23)
