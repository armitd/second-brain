#### In a world full of complex problems, these companies are proof that design isn’t just about simplicity, it’s about context.

![The 10 most innovative companies in design for 2023](https://images.fastcompany.net/image/upload/w_2480,ar_16:9,c_fill,g_auto,f_auto,q_auto:best/wp-cms/uploads/2023/02/p-1-90846965-MIC_Design.webp)
*Explore the full 2023 list of Fast Company’s [Most Innovative Companies](https://www.fastcompany.com/most-innovative-companies/list), 540 organizations that are reshaping industries and culture. We’ve selected the firms making the biggest impact across 54 categories, including [advertising](https://www.fastcompany.com/90846433/most-innovative-companies-advertising-2023), [architecture](https://www.fastcompany.com/90846657/most-innovative-companies-architecture-2023), [beauty](https://www.fastcompany.com/90846820/most-innovative-companies-beauty-2023), and more.*

The best designs are products of their unique circumstances. Whether a piece of architecture, fashion, graphic, or consumer good, an innovative design—by its very nature—often defies easy definition. It should come as no surprise then that this year’s Most Innovative Companies in design span a wide range of disciplines and themes, ranging from addressing deep inequity to providing some amusing cultural criticism.

Some, like our top company, [Mschf](https://mschf.com/), are undefinable themselves. The part-art collective, part-marketing agency, part-industrial design firm launched some of 2022’s most viral design projects, including popsicles in the likeness of billionaires like Jeff Bezos and a pair of [color-changing sneakers](https://www.fastcompany.com/90770304/this-sneaker-gets-more-colorful-the-more-you-wear-it) designed with Jimmy Fallon. Other honorees, like [Defender](https://footdefender.com/) and [Standard Industries](https://www.standardindustries.com/), brought a laser-focus to problem-solving with a [therapeutic boot](https://www.fastcompany.com/90755550/ex-nike-designer-builds-a-boot-that-could-prevent-80000-amputations-a-year) designed to reduce the number of diabetes-related amputations and a [power generating solar shingle](https://www.fastcompany.com/90706260/this-new-solar-shingle-can-get-nailed-right-on-to-your-roof), respectively.

Across the spectrum, the companies on our list have changed the way people experience and move through the world—in some ways quite literally. Last year, [MAP Project Office](https://mapprojectoffice.com/) completed a redesign of the trains for London’s Elizabeth train line, which now shuttles 1,500 passengers into central London daily in carriages featuring soft lighting and beautiful graphics. And [Ralph Appelbaum Associates](https://raai.com/) designed a 23,000-square-foot first-floor exhibition space for Charleston’s newly opened African American Museum that guides visitors through an affecting chronology that traces slavery to the racial inequities of today.

Of course, there are plenty of moments of pure delight on the list, too. [Instrument](https://www.instrument.com/)’s website for the Eames Institute of Curiosity shows just how playful online archives can be. And [Enlisted Design](https://www.enlisteddesign.com/) is honored for the breadth and reach of its culturally relevant design work. Meanwhile, companies like [Figma](https://www.figma.com/) and [Fable](https://makeitfable.com/) are redefining what it means to make design accessible to all.

These companies are proof that design doesn’t fit into a tidy box. But all of them do have one thing in common: They are actively working toward making the world a better and more interesting place.

#### 1. Mschf

*For making the good kind of marketing trouble*

Since its founding in 2016, [Mschf](https://mschf.com/) has blurred the lines between art, commerce, and comedy with its Warholian biweekly product drops, including a high-profile sneaker collaboration with Lil Nas X. In 2022, the company pushed into the mainstream by selling $10 popsicles in the likeness of billionaires like Jeff Bezos and Jack Ma and collaborating with Jimmy Fallon on a pair of Gobstomper sneakers whose gray suede wears away to reveal a multicolored layer beneath. And it produces pieces that get sold through its gallerist, Perrotin, which held a solo show of Mschf’s work at the end of 2022. Entitled “No More Tears, I’m Lovin’ it,” the show featured dozens of pieces produced in 2022. Many of them were commentaries of contemporary business and culture, from a Boston Dynamics robot dog fitted with assault weapons (Spot’s Rampage) to a package of Peeps candies shaped in the style of the Venus of Willendorf (Peeps of Willendorf) to an arcade game entitled BTS in Battle, which plays with the idea of whether the global pop stars will indeed be compelled to serve their mandatory military service in South Korea. At heart, Mschf is undefinable—part viral marketing agency, part art collective, part industrial design firm—which explains why it’s so adept at capturing the world’s attention.

*Read more about [Mschf](https://www.fastcompany.com/90850320/art-collective-mschf-capitalism), honored as No. 50 on Fast Company’s list of the [World’s 50 Most Innovative Companies of 2023](https://www.fastcompany.com/most-innovative-companies/list).*

#### 2. Figma

*For making collaborative design accessible to all*

When [Figma](https://www.figma.com/) was founded in 2014, Adobe dominated the world of design software. Figma’s vision—that well-designed digital products require cross-functional collaboration—was a challenge to Adobe’s suite of software that catered to the individual creative. Fast-forward to 2022: Adobe acquired Figma for $20 billion, cementing the idea that web-based collaborative design tools are the future of creating, and that they are useful to a wider swath of users than previously believed. In 2022, Figma expanded its reach through a new partnership with Google for Education that brings its core tools, a collaborative web app for interface design, and its new whiteboard tool, FigJam, to high school students across the country. The pro bono program (the typical $45/user software fee is waived) reaches tens of millions of students who now use Figma on Chromebooks to do everything from learn the basics of interface design to create designs for shop class. In the past few months, school districts in Anaheim and Modesto, California, (among others) joined the program, and Figma is currently onboarding major school districts including those of New York City, Los Angeles, and Austin. The company says its education signups more than doubled in 2022.

While the program isn’t meant to generate revenue, it creates a pipeline of people who are now literate in the digital design space, and who might become future customers. That’s the bet Adobe is making on Figma, which has annual recurring revenue of $400 million. As Figma continues to expand into new markets—it recently launched a Japanese-language version of its software and has plans to release Figma and FigJam in other languages—it’s advancing the idea that the software isn’t just for designers or design organizations but for anyone who wants to collaboratively brainstorm over the web.

#### 3. Enlisted Design

*For taking an expansive approach to industrial design and branding*

[Enlisted Design](https://www.enlisteddesign.com/) might have started as an industrial design firm, but its vast array of recent projects show just how expansive it has become. In late 2022, the Bay Area-based studio—known for its work on products like Pax vapes and Fellow kettles—designed a playful brand identity for the Sun Day Carwash, a new design-forward car wash in the Bay Area. Enlisted also launched its first incubated-from-scratch product, a line of essential oils called Hohm. For Hohm, Enlisted identified a product category with market potential, and from there, developed the product, designed the brand, and set up the omni-channel sales model. The studio has plans to launch one to two incubated products every year to capitalize on its ability to provide start-to-finish design services.

Enlisted also crafted the brand identity for the new Feastables line of snacks from Jimmy Donaldson, aka MrBeast. (This followed the agency’s work on the wildly successful MrBeast Burger restaurant concept.) In the first 90 days after launch, Feastables moved 4 million chocolate bars, took in $10 million in revenue, and garnered 3,500 five-star reviews. Since then, the company has sold an average of 200,000 a week—a testament to both Jimmy Donaldson’s star power and a well-designed brand.

#### 4. Standard Industries

*For designing practical solutions to the complicated problem of solar power*

[Standard Industries](https://www.standardindustries.com/) is the largest roofing company in the world, which makes it uniquely positioned to have an outsize impact on solar power. In 2022, through its GAF Energy arm, the company launched Timberline Solar, a solar shingle that can be installed like a traditional roof shingle—only a nail gun is required. The design process required navigating some difficult material issues (the shingle had to be waterproof, fireproof, and strong enough to walk on), and the resulting design is a major aesthetic upgrade to the standard solar tile racks that are often installed on roofs. As a result of Timberline, GAF Energy expects to more than double its year-over-year revenue for the closeout of 2022. GAF Energy recently opened a new shingle manufacturing facility near Austin, which so far has boosted production by 500%. When it’s fully operational in 2023, the plant will be the largest solar roof facility in the world, making solar shingles available at an impactful scale.

Standard Industries’s GAF roofing division, meanwhile, is continuing to advance its effort to create circularity in the roofing industry. Last year, it unveiled a prototype for shingles made with recycled asphalt. The company is now planning to build a commercial-scale shingle recycling facility in Corsicana, Texas, in an effort to divert the 90% of asphalt shingle waste that it says goes directly into landfills. The facility will instead transform this waste into new roofing material. GAF expects the product to hit the market in 2024, with the first shingles being comprised of 15% recycled material. Eventually, that ratio will rise to around 45% to 50%.

These solar and recycled asphalt shingles live alongside other efforts from Standard Industries to change the way individuals, communities, and businesses deal with the climate crisis. GAF’s Community Cool project, for example, aims to mitigate urban heat islands by applying a layer of StreetBond, a water-based acrylic coating for asphalt that’s been mixed with an additive that reflects heat instead of absorbing it. In Pacoima, one of L.A.’s hottest neighborhoods, GAF applied the coating to 10 square blocks as an experiment. The early results are promising: During the September heat wave in California, ambient air temperature was between 2ºF to 4ºF cooler in the coated area than in the control area.

#### 5. Fable

*For making accessible design accessible for all*

Corporations are stepping up to create products for the one in 10 people around the world who live with a disability—and 2022 brought an estimated $10 billion in spending dedicated to accessible design. But how does a company engage with the disability community to scrutinize its products before release?

Launched in 2018, [Fable’s](https://makeitfable.com/) platform connects companies to hundreds of people living with disabilities, who can test the usability of websites, apps, and other digital products from home. Fable’s clients, which include Microsoft, Walmart, and Shopify, pay a monthly subscription cost, for which they receive credits to test products and collaborate with Fable’s remote experts. Unlike the gig workers of Uber or Amazon Web Services, which tie compensation to rapid output, Fable’s testing team is paid a flat rate of $35 to $75/hr.

In 2022, the company marked a year of impressive growth. It closed a series A round for $13.5 million, completed more than 2,100 usability tests, and grew its full-time team by 81% to roughly 70 people. It also marked the launch of Fable Upskill, a customized curriculum for businesses, in which people with disabilities train corporations to create more accessible designs. Fable reports that 25% of its subscribers have already taken part.

#### 6. Defender

*For designing a shoe that saves feet*

Of 200,000 people in the U.S. who have amputations each year, 130,000 are specifically from complications with diabetes. Podiatrist Jason Hanft and former Nike designer Michael DiTullo paired up to lower this statistic with a new boot called [Foot Defender](https://footdefender.com/). The $250 medical boot looks like a slip-on Nike Air Jordan high-top, but it’s actually a medical device. It’s designed to reduce the number of diabetes-related amputations every year by redistributing weight off of the ball of the foot, where 83% of diabetic wounds happen, across the arch and the leg. The boot is currently being used as the “standard of care for off-loading” by a number of FDA-approved clinical trials studying the healing of diabetic foot ulcers, as well as in an NIH study of the diabetic patient population. So far, clinical studies have found that patients wearing the Foot Defender healed in an average of 5.2 weeks, compared with the national average of 20 weeks in both the U.S. and the U.K. The company is currently working with more than 100 sales representatives to expand the boot’s footprint in the medical device industry, and thousands of patients have purchased the Foot Defender directly through its website.

#### 7. NoA Ignite

*For giving first responders eyes on the scene*

[NoA Ignite](https://noaignite.com/) is a Nordic digital agency that makes infrastructure-scale complex tasks—think measuring ocean water health or fielding emergency calls—simpler. Last year the company redesigned the experience for Norway’s equivalent to the U.S.’s 911, the AMK emergency call service. The new experience adds video capabilities to any AMK call through a link that callers can click to give video access to the emergency dispatcher. It’s a simple interaction with a powerful outcome: This access gives first responders eyes on the scene within 45 seconds through live-streamed video. The new feature cut down on unneeded ambulances (172 in the first 10 months) and airlifts, while increasing coordination between Norway’s emergency response services. Norway’s firefighters are now using the feature, with the eventual goal of all emergency services being able to “see the same thing, at the same time.”

#### 8. Instrument

*For bringing the Eames archive to life online*

When [Instrument](https://www.instrument.com/), an agency in Portland, Oregon, partnered with the [Eames Institute](https://www.eamesinstitute.org/) in 2022, Llisa Demetrios, granddaughter of legendary midcentury designers Ray and Charles and chief curator of the foundation, asked a provocative question: Can a website have a soul? Instrument’s answer was a website for the Eames Institute of Infinite Curiosity that taps into the storied designers’ singular blend of playfulness and clear-eyed functionality. The website’s centerpiece is an interactive gallery of collections that tell the story of the Eames through thousands of objects that have been dug up from storage after 40 years of hibernation. Instrument built a backend API to port information from the Eames Institute’s artifact database into the website’s CMS, creating a one-click solution for continually updating the site as the Eames team digitizes the archival objects. (Demetrios expects that the collection will exceed 20,000 objects after the project is complete.) The result is a uniquely expressive blend of history, editorial, and visuals—a web experience that’s more akin to browsing a dusty archive than navigating a website.

Instrument also partnered with BankBlackUSA, an independent, grassroots organization with a mission to promote financial advocacy in Black communities and close the racial wealth gap. As part of its pro bono services program, the design studio led BankBlack’s strategic relaunch with a vibrant and sophisticated rebrand and website for the platform.

#### 9. Ralph Appelbaum Associates

*For translating history into an interactive museum experience*

From its earliest work on projects like the U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum, [Ralph Appelbaum Associates](https://raai.com/) has transformed historical moments into compelling and thoughtfully designed museum experiences that engage the public on a visceral level. In 2023, the firm will unveil its work for the new International African American Museum in Charleston, South Carolina, a highly anticipated museum built on the land of Gadsden’s Wharf—the site where more than 45% of all African slaves entered America. The museum is dedicated to studying the African diaspora, the spread of African American culture and influence, and the movements for justice and equality.

Ralph Appelbaum Associates designed the museums’s 23,000-square-foot first-floor exhibition space. That includes the American Journeys exhibit where visitors can explore the history and experiences of African Americans in South Carolina through custom-designed louver walls that reveal artifacts, images, audio, and media in a chronology that traces slavery to racial inequities of today. RAA also led the design of the Center for Family History, a resource center for helping visitors trace their genealogy. There, a “Response Story Booth” gives visitors the opportunity to reflect on and record their experience of the museum.

#### 10. Map Project Office

*For showing us a more human side of technology*

London’s [MAP Project Office](https://mapprojectoffice.com/) wants to redefine our relationship with technology by bringing a thoughtful, playful approach to its design. For its speculative project Little Signals, MAP partnered with Google Seed Studio to design a series of whimsical objects that reimagine how push notifications can quietly alert us to important information without triggering cortisol levels. On the other side of the spectrum, MAP led the design of the interiors and branding for London’s new Elizabeth train line, which shuttles 1,500 passengers into central London every day. MAP created the bold graphics on the train’s exterior as well as every detail of the carriage’s interiors, including lighting that shines both top to bottom and left to right, giving the carriage a softer, more natural glow. Though they function at different scales, both projects showcase how design can reorient our default interactions with technology to be softer and more humane.
