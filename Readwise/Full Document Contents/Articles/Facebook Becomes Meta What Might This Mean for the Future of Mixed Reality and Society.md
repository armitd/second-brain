On 28th October, the news broke that Facebook Inc. would change its name to Meta Platforms Inc. The name change came with the company’s intention “to reflect its focus on building the metaverse”. 

In a founding letter, Zuckerberg claimed “Right now our brand is so tightly linked to one product that it can’t possibly represent everything we’re doing today, let alone in the future. Over time, I hope we are seen as a metaverse company, and I want to anchor our work and our identity on what we’re building towards”. 

Logically, we can assume that the multi-billion-dollar company seeks to use its influence to make “Meta” and “metaverse” synonymous. It will be interesting to see what Meta’s competitors will come up with in order to stand out in this rapidly expanding market.

Despite Zuckerberg’s explanation for the rebrand, others have a more cynical view. Some see it as a distraction from the public scrutiny the company has faced following recent criticism. Kyla Lam, research analyst for ARVR/Wearables at IDC, told Trusted Reviews “Facebook is using the new parent name as a distraction to the public to talk about the rebranding despite of (sic) whistleblower Frances Haugen’s testimonies against Facebook.” 

Meta’s journey into the metaverse began with the $2 billion acquisition of Oculus in 2014, which led to the company integrating Facebook’s technology for an enhanced social gaming experience. The rebrand in turn has signalled the retirement of the Oculus brand, in favour Meta’s own. 

Unsurprisingly, Meta’s focal shift has generated a huge wave of interest in the metaverse and mixed reality technology. Google trends have shown a massive spike in searches for the term “metaverse”, coinciding with Mark Zuckerberg’s announcement of the company’s rebrand, peaking most prominently in South Korea. 

![META HQ](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/C4D12AQHF24UQu9NLnA/article-inline_image-shrink_400_744/article-inline_image-shrink_400_744/0/1638975191008?e=2147483647&v=beta&t=OTY9tKlVs0IS8bq2a4JUPrr5O9R6ouridHi1d6EeiZ8)
Meta recently shared its ideas for working Augmented Reality tech into everyday life, discussing its Ego4D project. The project aims to improve AI’s understanding of everyday life from a first-person perspective, through using over 2,200 hours of first-person video captured by smart glasses. The company is also reportedly working on its own AR glasses. 

Material released by Meta suggests its AR technology could be developed to act as an assistant to everyday tasks. A video depicts the hypothetical technology helping its operator cook a meal, saying which ingredients have been added, and which to add next. We later see it used to help a young girl learn how to play drums, the technology having retained drum patterns played earlier by her father. 

Meta’s leading AI research scientist Kristen Grauman said “Ego4D makes it possible for AI to gain knowledge rooted in the physical and social world, gleaned through the first-person perspective of the people who live in it. Not only will AI start to understand the world around it better, it could one day be personalized at an individual level — it could know your favourite coffee mug or guide your itinerary for your next family trip. And we're actively working on assistant-inspired research prototypes that could do just that.”

Undoubtedly, this will raise privacy concerns among those who are uncomfortable with AI technology infiltrating our personal lives to such an extent. The idea of technology tracking our every move, learning our routines and the layout of our homes, as well as forecasting our actions is certainly a frightening one to some. 

With the growing interest in the metaverse that Facebook’s rebrand has left in its wake, it seems highly likely that we will see many other tech companies step up their game to keep up.
