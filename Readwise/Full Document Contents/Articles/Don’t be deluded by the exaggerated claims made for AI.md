#### Humans, supported by new technology, still have a lot going for them

Stefan Stern

   ![A boy points at a poster with his finger touching  the outstretched hand of a robot in the image](https://www.ft.com/__origami/service/image/v2/images/raw/https%3A%2F%2Fd1e00ek4ebabms.cloudfront.net%2Fproduction%2F73033358-b455-47d2-975d-1595a36ece25.jpg?fit=scale-down&source=next&width=700) While AI is developing rapidly, recent experiments have highlighted the limits of chatbots © Lintao Zhang/Getty Images 
*The writer is author of ‘How to Be a Better Leader’ and is a visiting professor at Bayes Business School, City, University of London*

You can almost hear the thoughts bubbling up in the minds of certain chief financial officers: “ChatGPT . . . interesting. If artificial intelligence and chatbots keep getting cleverer, what could this mean for our customer-facing operations, and headcount? The possibilities are endless.”

Recent experiments with Microsoft’s ChatGPT-enhanced Bing search engine may have helped to calm any wild fantasising going on among senior executives. A German student, Marvin von Hagen, prodded the chatbot to find out how it approached interactions with users. He provoked [this startling response](https://simonwillison.net/2023/Feb/15/bing/): “I will not harm you unless you harm me first.”

Kevin Roose, a New York Times technology writer, found the AI built into Bing “is [not ready for human contact](https://tinyurl.com/4skmcpsd)”. The search aspect of Bing came across like “a cheerful but erratic reference librarian”, whereas “when you have an extended conversation with the chatbot [it was] more like a moody, manic-depressive teenager who has been trapped, against its will, inside a second-rate search engine.”

Even the more upbeat Ethan Mollick, a professor at the Wharton School in Philadelphia, said: “I don’t think anyone knows what this all means, but I think we should be ready for a very weird world.” (Microsoft, meanwhile, is adjusting the settings on this bionic version of Bing.)

These are healthy warnings for [managers](https://www.ft.com/management) who might hope that rapidly improving [AI](https://www.ft.com/artificial-intelligence) could be the simple answer to dealing more effectively with customers. We have been here before. Readers with longer memories will recall the hype that accompanied the arrival of customer relationship management (CRM) software in the 1990s. As the sales pitch of the time had it, investing in the right kit would take much of the stress and effort out of keeping customers happy.

But by 2002, a Harvard Business Review article on “[the perils of CRM](https://hbr.org/2002/02/avoid-the-four-perils-of-crm)” noted that 55 per cent of CRM projects had not produced positive results. A 2001 survey of 451 senior executives found that CRM was in the bottom three for popularity out of 25 management tools. One in five respondents said CRM initiatives “not only had failed to deliver profitable growth but also had damaged longstanding customer relationships”.

Some business lessons, it seems, have to be relearned. Today too many customers still find themselves, once they have finally penetrated the high-level security and obfuscation that prevent them from speaking to a live employee, talking to “a human who isn’t allowed to act in a human way”, as the consultant John Sills puts it.

Sills is managing partner at a London-based consultancy, The Foundation, and is the author of a new book, *The Human Experience*. “Being a customer shouldn’t be hard work,” he writes. “Yet in many organisations, a combination of over-reliance on process, a fear of regulation and insecure micromanagement” prevent customer-facing employees from using their initiative to solve their customers’ problems.”

>  Chatbots can perform limited transactions, but they can’t have a real conversation. 
> 
>  

One of Sills’ clients had hired 500 people to try to reduce response times at a call centre. The company’s “net promoter score” — a measure of customer satisfaction — improved. But customers were simply ringing back more often as their problems had not been resolved: 30 per cent of the time these were repeat calls. John Seddon of Vanguard Consulting calls this “failure demand” — the unnecessary work companies create for themselves by not fixing things first time round.

Another client, a big UK supermarket, was rather pleased with its store redesign programme. They had put their best people on it. But when Sills and the company’s chief executive took an 80-year-old customer round a new store, they quickly found out the many ways in which the redesign had failed. “That might not have been statistically significant data,” Sills said, “but it’s exactly the sort of personal detail that most attempts at collecting ‘customer data’ fail to pick up.”

The most powerful people in businesses tend to be furthest from the customer, he said. “But if you’re running a business that relies on having customers, what on earth are you doing that’s more important than speaking to them?”

Sills is no technophobe, and knows how fast AI is developing. But, for now, chatbots are “little more than glorified gatekeepers”, he said, “intelligent enough to get in the way but not yet able to resolve [non-standard] queries . . . They’re unable to respond to those unscripted human moments that require creativity and spontaneity — the moments that build long-lasting relationships.” Chatbots can perform limited transactions, but they can’t have a real conversation. Humans, supported by new technology, still have a lot going for them.

This, then, is the exciting promise of AI, accompanied by the ever-present threat of digital dystopia. With luck and judgment managers may steer a successful path ahead. But it is also possible that AI will force us to confront what Ross Douthat in the New York Times recently called “a wilderness of mirrors showing us the most unexpected versions of our own reflections, and a place where an entire civilisation could easily get lost.”

Sounds like some call centres I’ve had to deal with.
