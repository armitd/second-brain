Companies typically don't suffer from a lack of ideas—they suffer from lacking a consistent way to decide which ideas deserve investment. Committees debate, spreadsheets multiply and road maps drift away from strategy.

What I have found is that the fix is not a bigger pipeline or a louder pitch; it's having a simple way to estimate the value of an idea and compare it with the rest of your portfolio. With that in mind, here is a guide on how you and your team can hold a session to determine the value of ideas and choose which to focus on.

#### Finding A Shared Definition Of Value

The first step is creating a shared sense of value with which to gauge the potential impact of ideas. "Value" should be defined by a single number in currency, such as your incremental revenue, gross margin or even "cost avoided" or "risk reduced" converted into monetary terms. Pick one unit that fits your context and use it for every idea in the session. In my experience, when finance and product and operations are all looking at the same number, decisions tend to get clearer.

#### A Simple Way To Estimate

There are four plain factors you can use to estimate the expected value of an idea: confidence, predicted value, time sensitivity and strategic fit. Write this as a simple multiplication so you can see how each factor changes the result.

**• Confidence:** What is the probability that the idea will work as intended, given the evidence you have today? Prior experiments, customer commitments, technical readiness and regulatory clarity all matter. Score each idea from 0.1 to 1.0, where 1.0 means there is very strong evidence.

**• Predicted Value:** Estimate the financial impact if the idea succeeds. Use a single number in currency so you can compare dissimilar ideas on the same footing. Keep the logic transparent so others can review it.

**• Time Sensitivity:** Does acting now change the outcome in a material way? Some opportunities close fast because of regulation or standards races. Others improve slowly over time. I recommend using a range such as 0.7 to 1.5 to reflect the effect of timing.

**• Strategic Fit:** Does the idea align with where your company has a right to win? You can score fit by rating four questions and adding the results: "Is this a high-value problem?" "Do we have company advantages?" "Is the market attractive?" and "Are we aligned to important trends?" A stronger total means better fit.

When you multiply the four factors, you should get an expected value for the idea based on what you know today. The point is not perfect precision. The point is comparability.

#### The Cost View Equation

Two ideas with the same expected value can have very different costs. To determine which is superior for your context, use this simple efficiency view:

Total Investment ÷ Expected Value = Cost Per Expected Value Point.

This can help you see which delivery approach should create value more efficiently. I've also found that it can help keep the discussion grounded in real trade-offs rather than enthusiasm.

#### How To Run A One-Hour Session

**• Prepare.** Select ten to fifteen active ideas. Ask each idea owner for one sentence that states the problem and one sentence that describes success. Have your finance team check the value logic ahead of time.

**• Score silently.** In the room, each decision-maker should score the four factors for every idea using a shared rubric. I've found that silent scoring reduces anchoring and politics.

**• Compare and explain.** Average the scores and sort the list. Discuss only the largest gaps. If new facts emerge, update the scores in the open.

**• Decide.** Fund the top group, and pause or stop the bottom group. For the middle group, set explicit evidence gates, such as two signed design partners or a working prototype that meets a defined metric.

Record the assumptions behind each factor in clear language. That way, you will know exactly what changed next quarter: evidence, value logic, timing or strategy.

#### When This Method Is Not Enough

No single method fits every decision. Keep a separate lane for ideas with extremely asymmetrical upsides where averages can hide potential. For example, if external risk dominates, such as regulatory or geopolitical risk, you may want to reduce the confidence score or time limit discovery before deciding. Avoid false precision; in my experience, a coarse and honest model beats a complex one that invites debate over decimals.

#### Making It Routine

A one-off session will not fix a portfolio, so consider setting a quarterly cadence. Rescore active ideas based on new evidence, and add new ideas only after discovery. Keep a simple log of scores, decisions and assumption changes so you build an audit trail. Share the top three and bottom three, and explain what changed and why. Consistency can help you build trust in the process and improve the quality of the pipeline.

#### The Takeaway

When asking what an idea is worth, I find it's important to focus on evidence, economics, timing and fit. When you compress those into one comparable number and pair it with a clear view of cost, you can make better choices faster, fund fewer and better ideas, stop the ill-fitting ones earlier, and align investment with strategy and value creation by design.
