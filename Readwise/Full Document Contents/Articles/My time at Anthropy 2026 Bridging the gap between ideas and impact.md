#####  Jenny Burns

####  The Accidental CEO - Part 44

I’ve spent the last few days at Anthropy in Cornwall at The Eden Project (which is stimulating in itself - I’d not been before) and I’ve come away feeling properly inspired. Not in a fluffy, “that was nice” kind of way, but in a more grounded, slightly uncomfortable way. The kind that makes you think we could be doing more… and actually, we should be.

Anthropy’s founding idea is a bold one: bringing together leaders from across business, government, academia and society to ask how we make Britain great again. Not in a nostalgic sense, but in a forward-looking, practical way. How we build a stronger, more innovative, more inclusive economy for the future. And what struck me is how much of that conversation comes back to innovation.

Not innovation as a buzzword. Not innovation theatre. But real, applied innovation. The kind that drives growth, creates new industries, and solves meaningful problems. And, that’s exactly why I was there, alongside Amelia and Ethan from the Magnetic team. Because this is the space we operate in every day - helping organisations think differently, move faster and design better futures. And increasingly, that means helping them navigate how to innovate in a world that’s moving quickly and feels more complex than ever.

What Anthropy does brilliantly is bring together a really unusual mix of people. Business leaders, academics, start-ups, policy makers, creative thinkers. People who don’t typically find themselves in the same room, let alone in proper conversation with each other. And when you give those people the space to talk, something shifts. The conversation moves away from the usual polished updates and into something more honest, more exploratory, and ultimately more useful. It feels less like people presenting and more like people thinking out loud together. Which is exactly what we need right now.

Yesterday I had the privilege of moderating a panel titled ‘From Campus to Commerce’, hosted by Julius Baer the wealth management business, and it really brought something into sharp focus for me. We have an incredible amount of innovation sitting within the UK. In our universities, in our start-ups, and in our large organisations. The talent is there, the ideas are there, the ambition is there. But we’re still not very good at connecting it.

We talked about the journey from research into real-world application and, more importantly, what gets in the way of that journey actually happening at pace. And the honest answer is, quite a lot. Different incentives, different timelines, different ways of thinking about risk. Academia is rightly focused on depth and rigour. Start-ups are built for speed and growth. Corporates are designed for scale and stability. All of that makes sense individually, but it doesn’t naturally line up. So what you end up with is something I’ve seen time and time again in my own career. Great ideas sitting in isolation. Research that doesn’t quite make it into the real world. Start-ups that struggle to scale. Corporates that want innovation but can’t quite move quickly enough to enable it. And everyone gets slightly frustrated with everyone else.

When you step back, it’s a bit bonkers. Because the ingredients for growth are all there, we’re just not combining them in the way we should. Sitting in that conversation yesterday, I kept coming back to something I wrote recently: we don’t lack ideas, we lack connection. And when that connection does happen properly, you can feel the shift almost immediately. The energy changes, perspectives open up, and suddenly things that felt stuck start to feel solvable.

It actually reminded me of the pandemic. When everything became urgent, all the things that normally slow organisations down seemed to fall away. Procurement found ways to say yes, governance compressed, decisions were made more quickly because they had to be. We proved we can work differently when the stakes are high enough. And I’d argue the stakes are just as high now, they just look different.

AI is accelerating at a pace most organisations are still trying to catch up with, entire industries are being reshaped, and the UK is competing globally for investment, talent and growth. If Anthropy is about making Britain great, then innovation has to sit right at the heart of that ambition. But innovation doesn’t happen in isolation. It happens when we connect the dots between research and reality, between start-ups and scale, between ideas and execution. And that requires a different kind of leadership. Because this isn’t just a systems problem, it’s a human one. It requires leaders who are willing to step outside their own context, who can bring together people with different perspectives and incentives, and who are prepared to prioritise progress over perfection. Human-centred leadership, in other words.

The best conversations I had this week weren’t driven by ego or certainty. They were driven by curiosity. By people being open about what they didn’t know, and willing to explore rather than defend. That felt refreshing. And more importantly, it felt necessary. Because in a world that’s getting more complex, not less, we don’t need leaders who have all the answers. We need leaders who can bring the right people together to find them.

I’ve come away from Anthropy feeling optimistic. Not because everything is solved. Gosh, far from it. But because when you get the right people in the room, focused on the right problems, you can feel momentum building. And momentum is what we need.

The ideas are there. The capability is there. The people are there. We just need to connect them.

The Accidental CEO
