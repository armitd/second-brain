## Full Document
*Also authored by Tina M. Park.*

Recently, in a landmark decision, [a jury in Los Angeles found Meta and YouTube negligent](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/25/nx-s1-5746125/meta-youtube-social-media-trial-verdict) for designing social media platforms that allegedly harmed young people, awarding $6 million in damages to the plaintiff. But a closely related case has received far less attention. On February 27, a Delaware judge ruled that nearly two dozen of Meta’s liability insurers [are not obligated](https://www.insurancejournal.com/magazines/mag-features/2026/03/23/862428.htm) to cover the costs of the company’s legal defense for several thousand similar cases waiting in the wings.

The Los Angeles negligence case is pivotal, marking a shift in focus from harmful content distributed on online platforms to concerns about the design of the platforms themselves, and the verdict — which the platforms intend to appeal — seems to open the door to a wider range of complaints against major tech companies, if upheld. The Delaware court’s decision in favor of Meta’s insurers is also significant, and not only because it means social media companies may have to bear their own costs. Some experts have floated liability insurance as a lever of private governance for AI, a mechanism that could help inject responsibility into how AI is designed, developed, and deployed as a condition for obtaining insurance. The Delaware decision offers a window into how that might play out.

Liability insurance typically covers two obligations: covering liability, like settlements and judgements (the duty to indemnify), and covering costs of tort litigation against the policyholder if a claim falls within a policy’s scope (the duty to defend). When the wave of social media litigation kicked off, many complaints hinging on allegations that plaintiffs suffered from physical injury and emotional distress due to actions taken by several user-generated content-sharing platforms, Meta filed claims to its insurers to cover the costs of defense. Insurers largely denied these claims. Not long after, insurance companies including Hartford, Chubb, and ten others sought a summary ruling that, under California law, the allegations against Meta did not give rise to a duty to defend.

At issue in the insurance case was whether the underlying tort cases alleged harm caused by an accident or by intentional conduct. Under California law, as the Delaware court decision [explains](https://law.justia.com/cases/delaware/superior-court/2026/n24c-11-010-skr-ccld.html), an accident is “an unexpected, unforeseen, or undesigned happening or consequence from either a known or unknown cause.” By the same token, it would not be an accident if the insured engaged in a deliberate act, “unless some additional, unexpected, independent, and unforeseen happening occurs that produces the damage.” Delaware Superior Court Judge Sheldon K. Renni found that (1) the underlying complaints allege only deliberate conduct and not “mere fortuity,” (2) and the impacts of the company’s intentional design choices ought to have been foreseen. As a result, the [insurers had no duty to defend Meta](https://www.insurancejournal.com/magazines/mag-features/2026/03/23/862428.htm) in the social media cases.

So why does this matter for AI? The theory as to how insurance could be a lever for governance is that insurance providers could be in a position to shape the behavior of AI developers and deployers by conditioning coverage on the implementation of certain risk controls (actions known to reduce relevant risks to manageable levels), or discounting the cost of coverage if certain safeguards are adopted. If companies determine that they need or want liability insurance to take on the risk of developing or deploying AI, and the requested controls truly help to mitigate risk, insurance would indeed offer an interesting lever of private governance for this advanced technology.

But it’s unlikely to be so simple. Experts [argue](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/why-liability-and-insurance-won-t-save-ai--lessons-from-cyber-insurance) that, as has turned out to be the case in the context of cybersecurity, insurers are unlikely to price coverage in a way that truly incentivizes the mitigation of risk. And even if insurers do manage to reward risk mitigation, the Delaware case highlights another pitfall: in close or large cases, insurers may well look for reasons to avoid paying out. With the Delaware decision in mind, insurers might (successfully) claim that so long as an AI-related harm is alleged to have stemmed from deliberate design decisions, then arguably, there is neither a duty to defend nor a duty to indemnify. In an ideal world, this threat might encourage policyholders to take care as they develop or deploy AI lest their insurance coverage not apply — but the uncertainty around what conditions might make coverage unavailable could also end up having the counterproductive effect of eroding confidence in the role insurers are envisioned to play in managing AI risk.

Artificial intelligence systems by nature involve significant uncertainty. Given the same inputs, outputs can vary and be unpredictable. Harms like an AI agent making errors in its attempts to accomplish a goal, or a chatbot producing an output that includes a confabulation, might seem to fall more clearly in the category of an accident. On the other hand, the process of AI development may contain some analogs to the design decisions in question in the social media cases: if a general purpose AI model is fine-tuned to be a chatty and helpful assistant, and that fine-tuning results in a subset of users developing a relationship with the chatbot that is later alleged to cause them harm, an argument that the harm stemmed from a deliberate design decision might find a toehold. If an AI-powered hiring tool is optimized to surface particular types of candidates but leads to discrimination, will insurers make a similar argument?

Certain types of AI-involved errors have come to be well known, raising questions about whether unintended outcomes may nevertheless be foreseeable. In its argument, Meta pointed to a California Court of Appeals distinction between general knowledge that something could cause harm and specific awareness that harm is practically certain to occur. This raises the baseline question of what safety evaluations results would indicate that a developer knew harm was sufficiently likely to preclude coverage. While courts in many states will find at least a duty to defend even where there is uncertainty, the case also highlights a perverse incentive: if specific knowledge of a potential harm increases liability or jeopardizes insurance coverage, companies may avoid rigorously testing their systems in the first place. Or it could create a conundrum in which insurers require such testing to mitigate risk as a condition of coverage, but that very requirement ends up negating the obligation to pay out.

What’s more, it may be hard to distinguish between design decisions and emergent behavior — outcomes that arise from system components interacting in unplanned ways. If other courts apply the trial court logic from the Delaware case broadly as a precedent, insurers may more aggressively argue that they should be relieved from a duty to defend under commercial general liability policies when complaints allege than an AI-related harm stemmed from a deliberate decision and not an accident (which plaintiffs will be incentivized to claim since that would increase their likelihood of success). If insurers are taken off the hook, the burden of what can become quite significant legal costs in complex litigation would move from insurers (which may be ultimately distributed across many different insurers, and subsequently premium-paying policyholders) to individual policyholders. This may be tolerable for larger, well-capitalized companies that are in a position to self-insure — that is, to set aside their own money for potential litigation costs or damages — but smaller, downstream developers or deployers may not have the same financial resources to cover legal costs and damages.

Ultimately, the role that private insurance will play in managing the risks of AI development and deployment is still to be determined. Insurance coverage of AI systems and AI-related harms is in its early days, with only a few AI-specific insurers in the market. By redistributing the financial risks of developing and deploying new technologies, insurers could potentially support more highly governed adoption of emerging technologies (while still enabling innovation) by requiring risk mitigation strategies as a condition for insurance coverage or lower premiums. However, the Delaware case reveals that insurers have their own interests to balance, and care will be needed if insurance is to be engaged as an alternative governance mechanism. For insurance to emerge as a useful governance lever, developers, insurers, regulators, researchers, and public interest organizations will need to engage actively in defining what reasonable and effective controls look like, how risks should be assessed, and under what conditions coverage should be available.
