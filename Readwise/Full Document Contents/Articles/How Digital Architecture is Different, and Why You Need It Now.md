#####  Rick Pastore

######  Developing business sustainability resources for technology leaders.

If technology is the fuel that innovates business, then architecture is the chassis of the digital business “ride.” It is up to IT to build and migrate the enterprise to digital architecture or risk transformation stalling out. Modern digital architecture differs from traditional IT enterprise architecture not just in technology, with its cloud hosting and other ISMAC technologies; the layers of data and application architectures also differ fundamentally (see comparison chart below). Digital architecture is agile, responsive and scalable. It reduces complexity and legacy cost. And it provides a platform to rapidly deploy innovative, customer-centric services.

**Traditional IT architecture versus modern digital architecture**

![](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/C5612AQHDIPRBw0bZLw/article-inline_image-shrink_400_744/article-inline_image-shrink_400_744/0/1520210884082?e=1735776000&v=beta&t=gxBUEZwNynnujC7hf7hk4R8j0FlatZb_gUQuIoXLcDY)
Digital architecture is one of five digital capabilities that IT organizations must master and put in place for digital business transformation to succeed. The other four are summarized in this [free report](https://goo.gl/zsWPT1) from The Hackett Group.

As with traditional architecture, digital architecture is a stack of layers supporting the business value chain – employees, end-customers, partners, suppliers (see structural stack below). Under that business architecture layer is the relatively new layer of “experience architecture.” This enables precise customer targeting, personalization and engagement. The next layer is a combination off application and data architectures that work together to deliver the customer experience. Underpinning it all is the technology layer, which encompasses core infrastructure.

**Layers of the modern digital architecture stack**

Mixed ownership complicates modern digital architecture for IT leaders. The top two layers – business and experience architectures – are the province of the executive team and of customer-facing business functions, respectively. The bottom two layers more typically fall under IT’s auspices. The challenge is making it all integrate harmoniously, and it is largely up to IT to ensure this happens through management and governance that spans the layers.

For resource reasons, most companies cannot erect a digital architecture stack all at once. Instead, they introduce elements gradually by migrating or replacing legacy systems and by applying application and data rationalization. During platform migration, companies can “buy” digital architecture rather than build it themselves. Public cloud-hosting vendors are one source of digital application and data architectures. Traditional systems vendors, too, offer digital architecture components with their latest releases. For example, Oracle’s banking product includes a device-agnostic mobile-access capability for customers, a key element of the experience architecture layer.

There’s no shortcuts however, for the data layer. This is where IT has its work cut out for it, thanks to an historic lack of data architecture discipline. No companies in The Hackett Group’s IT benchmark database that claim to have well-documented, current or complete data/information architecture definitions. Only 36% say they have well-documented data architecture roadmaps. Half have no roadmap for architecture at all.

Where documented architecture standards do exist, IT has a spotty record of reviewing, updating and tracking compliance against them. Only 20% do so annually; most have no set schedule. To enable digital capabilities, modern digital architecture must be constructed on a sound foundation of data management discipline.

To sum up, there are seven must-haves for the move to modern digital architecture:

1. Improve architecture discipline and maturity of standards, documentation reviews and compliance tracking.

2. Apply formal master data management and data stewardship to improve data quality.

3. Virtualize data to minimize impact of data storage complexity on applications.

4. Develop applications faster and more responsively using orchestrated processes and microservices accessed from libraries or catalogs.

5. Adopt cloud-based platforms, services and data to enhance agility.

6. Leverage “experience architecture” to enable omnichannel, personalized engagement solutions.

7. Incorporate architecture components obtained from application and cloud-hosting vendors.
