## Full Document
![](https://cached.imagescaler.hbpl.co.uk/resize/scaleWidth/1600/cached.offlinehbpl.hbpl.co.uk/news/OTM/BigTraceyFollows_1.jpg)
January brings with it not only the relentless cold and rainy weather but the relentless stream of trends reports that have been been cued up somewhere in the internet, just desperate to burst forth on the first day of a new year.

There’s nothing wrong with a good trends report (heck, I spend most days in any year, reading, writing, observing and speaking about trends) but I do find this increasing pattern of only issuing trends at the beginning of the year somewhat odd.

I think it has something to do with the notion that January is when we now take stock, to prepare for the rest of the year, and try to predict what is going to happen over the next 12 months (personally I prefer September for this).

We often feel the need to arm ourselves with these trend tomes to give us a glimpse into what many believe is absolutely going to happen and hence prepare for it. It strikes me as somewhat ironic then, that this year there are more trends reports than in any previous year and yet we seem to be less prepared than ever.

Something’s off.

What’s off is our understanding of trends and our increasingly slavish behaviour towards them. If you really want to prepare for the future, and at the same time partly create it, then you need to use those trends not as tools for preparation but as tools for challenging your own perspective.

It’s important to remember what a trend is. It is not a fad, or a fleeting fashion. It is merely an observed increase or decrease in something. Trends build or fade over time. When we describe them we should be talking about ‘more’ of something or ‘less’ of something, rather than as something that has just popped up out of nowhere.

If we are in the business of spotting signals of change, we might spot enough signals early enough to give us the first glimpse into the emergence of a trend, a trend that others have yet to notice but one that we can make them aware of.

But the most important role a trend plays is to alter our perspective. We might have thought things were one way, but the trend is indicating that they might be another way. It’s not the trend that is interesting but the assumptions that lurk beneath it.

Those assumptions about the way the world works, the values that are most important, the meaning of something or the status of something, they must have changed. Why? How? What did we assume to be the case before, that we no longer assume to be the case now?

At the end of last year I was reading about Earthrise, that famous photographic image taken by Bill Anders in 1968 of the Earth from space. He framed the shot of Earth as it appeared from behind the Moon with the horizon to the right, and the earth moving from right to left, re-appearing out from behind the Moon.

The perspective is key, it shows the dynamics of space, the earth as one moving orb, of perhaps many, and the weightlessness and vulnerability of our planet floating, just as we the audience seem to be floating - where exactly are we? We’re literally floating in space, all of us.

However, the image is usually depicted rotated 90 degrees, so we are looking at the Earth high up, from a standing position on the Moon. And as the author suggests, “…we immediately reveal our limited planet-bound perspective. We automatically see ourselves on a planet’s surface, watching another planet rise above the distant horizon line”. As he notes, with the former image we see the perspective of the future, with the latter, merely the perspective of the past. The meaning has changed, utterly.

Just as it’s dangerous to take an image at face value, so it is with a trend. A trend can give you that feeling of certainty but in a sense that will merely speak to the past. Trends need to be turned upside down, and looked at back to front, and investigated for the assumptions that lie beneath them, and the different perspectives they can bring.

If you want to really prepare for the future you need to challenge any underlying assumptions and the limited way you look at the world today.

My advice is not to follow trends but to invest time in discussing, debating and properly investigating them, because no report, no matter how good it is, will conjour a new perspective for you.

*Tracey Follows is the founder and CEO of futures consultancy, Futuremade, and author of The Future of You.*
