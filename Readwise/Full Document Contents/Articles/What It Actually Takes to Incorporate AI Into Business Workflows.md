Recently, one of our senior leaders, Megan O’Hern-Crook, spoke at a national conference about AI’s evolution in some more traditional business functions—like finance, marketing, and communications. Her audience included executives much like the ones reading this column, those who work in environments still heavily reliant on paper records, legacy systems, and institutional memory—i.e. the very people who can benefit the most from digitization and AI.

We’ve been tracking the headlines around generative AI and platforms like ChatGPT closely, not because we doubt the power or promise of the tools, but because we have questions about the engines making them run. In our work, we often meet historical records and critical datasets in their most chaotic states. We organize and arrange them into the order that makes AI more viable.

That’s why Megan, and all of us more broadly at HAI, know the real conversation isn’t just about what’s new and shiny. It’s understanding that no matter how smart a tool might be, it’s only as good as the data it’s fed and the environment it’s introduced into—environments we often are responsible for creating.

So whether you’re a passionate advocate or a cautious observer, here are three foundational steps we advise for any AI implementation: setting AI up to succeed, securing institutional buy-in, and starting small with a pilot.

**1. Set AI up to succeed**

We most often hear how AI can boost productivity, automate tedious tasks, or unearth insights buried deep in forgotten corners—which it can, but only if we’re honest about what it takes on the back end.

Too often, organizations implement AI tools without laying the necessary groundwork. The most important of these is access to high-quality, structured, and well-organized data. As a seasoned taxonomist and knowledge manager, I know most organizations do not have this.

At its core, well-organized data depends on metadata, or the data about your data. It’s what allows you to find, sort, and apply information in meaningful ways. Metadata must be accurate, complete, and labeled consistently over time. Without it, even the most powerful AI tools won’t retrieve or process records effectively. If your data isn’t findable to a human, it won’t be usable to a machine either.

Megan often uses this example: No one searches for “group of people standing together” in a folder of company photos. They’re searching for a particular photo from a certain event on a known date.

Creating and maintaining strong metadata isn’t just a task for IT or your digital team—it’s a shared responsibility, which is why this must be the first step. From program leads to records managers to legal and marketing teams, everyone must ensure information is labeled, categorized, and updated according to your organization’s standards.

Truthfully, this most often takes outside support. Going through the raw data files and information sets, creating systems for multiple functions to implement, and then taking the first pass at the organization is something really only archivists are trained to do. Organizations can save many, many thousands of hours and budget by outsourcing this work to professionals who will get it right the first time.

The ROI is simple. You wouldn’t expect a new team member to perform flawlessly without training or background. The same goes for AI. Feed it poor data and it will return poor results, often faster and with more confidence than a human would. Garbage in, garbage out as we like to say.

Now does that sound like innovation to you?

**2. Secure buy-in from the top and the trenches**

One of the biggest missteps we see is treating digitization or AI as a technical initiative when in reality, it’s cultural. If you don’t have buy-in across the organization—from the C-suite to the data custodians—you’re likely to end up with a stalled project (and a receipt for some very expensive software). It might seem counterintuitive to put this step second, but you eliminate the risk in selling this into the C-suite when you can present them the clean data files that will power the more expensive AI tool or technology and get them excited over the improved “returnability” of the results.

This is especially true because buy-in means different things to different people. For leadership, it’s about understanding the ROI and long-term impact, from increasing the bottom line to automating repetitive tasks. For IT, it’s about security, access, and integration. And for the frontline staff, it’s about making their work more accurate and less burdensome (i.e., not just yet another system to learn).

It’s not just about getting everyone to say “yes.” It’s about ensuring everyone understands what problems AI is aiming to address and is on the same page for what success looks like, individually and collectively.

**3. Start small and learn along the way**

With AI or digitization, more isn’t always better. We often advise clients to start with a pilot project as a test case. This allows you to determine whether the tool is truly fit for its purpose, identify any roadblocks early, and gather the lessons that will be invaluable when scaling up.

The pilot should be meaningful enough to matter but contained enough to manage. This approach allows your team to build confidence and experience, and it gives leadership tangible results to justify further investment.

**Close the gap: It starts with people**

The AI conversation is no longer hypothetical, and it’s certainly not reserved for cutting-edge tech firms. It’s here, and it’s finding its way into everything from records management to customer service queries. But unlocking its full value demands thoughtful collaboration between people and technology.

These tools are only as effective as the humans who implement and interact with them. The key lies with building strong foundations—digital, behavioral, and organizational—so that teams are prepared not just to adopt AI, but to do so thoughtfully and strategically, with an eye toward long-term impact, accountability, and ethical use.

Remember: Putting people first, rather than platforms, positions your team, your data, and your business for long-term success.
