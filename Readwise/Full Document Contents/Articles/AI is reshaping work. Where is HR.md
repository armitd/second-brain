#####  Jenny Burns

####  The Accidental CEO - Part 46

Like everyone, I’ve been in a tonne of conversations about AI - boardrooms, client meetings, industry events, and more recently through the NewHuman AI breakfasts we’ve hosted in both New York and London. Different sectors, different contexts, but a very consistent pattern. Everyone is talking about AI in the context of tools, models, use cases, efficiency gains. But far fewer people are really talking about the human impact of all of this, and even fewer are being explicit about who owns that. That’s what’s starting to concern me.

Because for all the focus on technology, AI is fundamentally not a tech story. It’s a people story. It’s about how work changes, how roles evolve, how decisions are made, how organisations are structured and, perhaps most importantly, how people experience all of that change. It touches culture, leadership, capability, trust. All the things that ultimately determine whether an organisation succeeds or struggles through transformation.

Which is why I keep asking myself - where is HR in this conversation?

Now, I know HR teams aren’t ignoring AI. I see a lot of activity around how AI can be used within HR itself - improving recruitment processes, automating onboarding, enhancing learning platforms, streamlining performance management. All of that is important and, frankly, long overdue in some areas. But it feels like we’re focusing on optimising the function, rather than shaping organisations. And that’s the bigger opportunity.

While HR is (understandably) looking at how AI can improve its own efficiency, technology and transformation teams are stepping into the much broader space of defining how AI will reshape the business, how work will flow, and how value will be created. If HR isn’t actively influencing those decisions, then a huge part of the organisational impact is being shaped without the people lens fully in the room. That’s super risky, in my view.

AI is not just another system implementation. It’s a fundamental shift in how work gets done. In some cases, it will augment people. In others, it will replace parts of roles. Increasingly, we’ll see hybrid models where humans and AI agents are working side by side, sharing tasks, decisions and outputs. That changes not just what people do, but how they feel about their work, their value and their future. So the questions become much bigger than technology.

Who is redesigning roles in this new world? Who is thinking about capability, reskilling and career pathways? Who is supporting leaders to lead through this level of ambiguity and change? Who is actively managing the impact on culture as ways of working evolve? And perhaps most critically, who is holding the overall organisational design as agents start to take on a meaningful proportion of work? All of that sits squarely in HR territory. Or at least, it should.

What I’m seeing instead is that many of these conversations are being led predominantly by technology teams. And that’s not because they’re doing anything wrong, it’s because they’re closest to the tools, the data and the implementation. But technology doesn’t set culture. It doesn’t build trust, it doesn’t manage fear, and it doesn’t create clarity for people whose roles are shifting around them. That’s leadership. And that’s where HR should be front and centre.

At the NewHuman breakfasts, this came up repeatedly. Leaders were sharing ambitious AI strategies, talking about pilots and investments, but when the conversation turned to people - how teams would adapt, how roles would evolve, how leaders would cope - there was often a pause. A recognition that this part wasn’t fully thought through yet. This gap is where HR needs to step in. Not as a support function that reacts to change, but as a strategic driver that shapes it. The organisations that get this right won’t simply be the ones with the best technology. They’ll be the ones that integrate AI into their workforce in a way that actually works for the business and for the people within it.

So what does that look like in practice? IMO. 

Firstly, HR needs to own the people narrative of AI. Not just how the organisation is using it, but what it genuinely means for individuals. Clear, honest communication about change, opportunity and risk will matter enormously. If HR doesn’t shape that narrative, others will and not always in a way that builds trust.

Secondly, HR should be leading on organisation design in a much more active way. As AI agents take on more tasks, the shape of roles will shift. Some will become more strategic, others more specialised, and entirely new roles will emerge. Someone needs to step back and look at how work flows end to end, not just how individual jobs are defined.

Thirdly, leadership capability needs real focus. Many leaders are being asked to guide teams through AI-driven change without feeling fully confident themselves. HR has a critical role in equipping them, not just with knowledge, but with the ability to lead with clarity, empathy and confidence in uncertain conditions.

Fourthly, culture needs to be actively managed, not assumed. As technology changes behaviour, culture will shift - sometimes subtly, sometimes significantly. HR should be paying close attention to how trust, collaboration and accountability evolve in this new environment.

And finally, and perhaps most importantly, HR needs to keep the human at the centre. It sounds obvious, but in a world obsessed with efficiency and automation, it’s easy to lose sight of it. Organisations are still made up of people, and how those people feel, engage and perform will ultimately determine whether AI delivers on its promise.

I don’t say any of this as criticism. I say it because the opportunity for HR right now is enormous. AI could easily become something that happens to organisations. Or it could be something that is shaped by them thoughtfully, intentionally, and with a clear view of both business outcomes and human impact. And HR has a critical role in determining which of those it becomes because AI might be powered by technology, but its impact is entirely human. 

The Accidental CEO
