"25% of McKinsey & Company’s global fees now come from outcomes-based pricing"

That's a start. Of course, once you shift to more outcome based approaches then the question rapidly becomes one of demonstrating value (and not just an outcome). 

This what I used to call worth based development back in 2004 (in the old Fotango days where we built systems for free and charged a percentage of the value they created) and it's not as simple as it sounds. There can be many different types of value involved from financial, social to political. This is something which I worked on with [esynergy](https://uk.linkedin.com/company/esynergy-solutions?trk=public_post-text) - [https://lnkd.in/e5jNUR\_s](https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Flnkd%2Ein%2Fe5jNUR_s&urlhash=HwkY&trk=public_post-text) - a group that is well down that path of focusing on value.

In management practices, there's normally a lag between the leading edge and being the norm of about 30 to 50 years. Yes, I know this is far from new and yes, there's a world of difference between outcome and value but it's still encouraging to see these moves by the likes of McKinsey. It means the ideas are becoming mainstream.

[McKinsey & Company](https://www.linkedin.com/company/mckinsey?trk=public_post_reshare-text) just admitted the quiet part out loud: the traditional consulting business model is collapsing with AI as the accelerant. And rivals are scrambling to keep pace.

[Business Insider](https://www.linkedin.com/company/businessinsider?trk=public_post_reshare-text) reports that 25% of [McKinsey & Company](https://www.linkedin.com/company/mckinsey?trk=public_post_reshare-text)’s global fees now come from outcomes-based pricing, not billable hours. That’s a direct challenge to the old “scope + time + team” playbook that [Deloitte](https://www.linkedin.com/company/deloitte?trk=public_post_reshare-text), [Boston Consulting Group (BCG)](https://www.linkedin.com/company/boston-consulting-group?trk=public_post_reshare-text), and [EY](https://uk.linkedin.com/company/ernstandyoung?trk=public_post_reshare-text) still rely on.

What’s changing:
→  Strategy work is now <20% of McKinsey’s revenue. The rest is deep, multi-year AI and transformation execution.

→  Clients no longer ask for a project; they ask for a result with McKinsey paid only if it delivers.

→  EY is openly considering a service-as-a-software model powered by AI agents.

→  Multi-year, cross-functional transformations are pushing firms to behave more like long-term operating partners than advisory vendors.

The stakes? Tens of billions in consulting revenue are tied to models that AI has just made obsolete. Whoever adapts fastest wins the C-suite wallet.

This shift already determines which firms win multi-year AI modernization deals, and which are sidelined. 
But here’s the unanswered question: if outcomes drive fees, who owns the risk when AI underperforms or fails to scale?

Is this the beginning of the end for time-and-materials consulting. Or will the incumbents find a way to protect the old model?
