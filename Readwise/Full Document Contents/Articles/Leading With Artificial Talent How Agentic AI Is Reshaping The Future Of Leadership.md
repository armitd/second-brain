## Full Document
Tech leaders today must navigate a wide range of conversations—from deep technical discussions with engineers to high-level strategy sessions with executives, clients and the public. The ability to translate complex concepts into clear, outcome-focused messages can mean the difference between confusion and alignment.

Below, members of [Forbes Technology Council](https://councils.forbes.com/forbestechcouncil) share strategies to help tech leaders communicate more effectively with any audience. From tailoring messages to specific groups to mastering the art of storytelling, these approaches can strengthen trust and inspire action.

#### 1. Tie Technology To What Audiences Value Most

Always connect the tech to what matters most to your audience. Whether it’s the public, executives or shareholders, tailor your message—show how the tech improves lives, drives goals or boosts profits. Keep it simple and practical. Clear, consistent framing builds trust and makes the impact of the tech real and relatable. - [Dan Priest](https://councils.forbes.com/u/6ad68f4a-54cf-4183-8240-49cd105963ad), [PwC](http://www.pwc.com/us/en.html)

#### 2. Make Space For Genuine Listening

The best ideas often come from open dialogues. A tech leader who listens actively creates trust and uncovers insights that might otherwise be missed. Especially in healthcare innovation, where the stakes are high and the challenges complex, clear communication starts with understanding, not just being heard. - [Andrew Baker-Campbell](https://councils.forbes.com/u/db5aef8c-1efb-4a33-a622-a419f4f81a88), [TTP](https://www.ttp.com/)

#### 3. Don’t Just Transmit—Translate

Great tech leaders turn complexity into clarity by using stories, analogies and empathy. It’s not about sounding smart; it’s about making others feel smart. True influence comes from connection, not explanation. The best tech leaders aren’t just engineers of products—they’re engineers of belief. - [Hesham Zreik](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f98f2b25-c2cb-4070-b45d-6517fdd5a6c4), [FasterCapital](https://fastercapital.com/)

#### 4. Consider The Problem From The End User’s Point Of View

One tip is to talk about the business or customer problem, not the technology. I have often seen tech leaders approach things from a technology-first perspective without clarifying the issue, getting buy-in or reflecting with the audience on the core of the problem. When one practices the skill of framing the problem from the customer’s or business’s point of view, the technology part becomes easier to communicate. - [Bhavin Surela](https://councils.forbes.com/u/b1ac5127-f571-49db-8e34-ad4ad45c15c6), [Twilio](https://twilio.com/)

#### 5. Prioritize Clarity And Simplicity

From an education leadership perspective, tech leaders should prioritize clarity and simplicity. Translate complex tech into understandable language for all audiences. Avoid jargon, use analogies and focus on the “what” and “why,” not just the “how.” This ensures your message truly resonates, fostering better understanding and collaboration. - [Terry Oroszi](https://councils.forbes.com/u/e970fac3-7519-4093-8c08-0997a9ee0eac), Wright State University Boonshoft School of Medicine

#### 6. Be Considerate, Aware And, If Possible, Entertaining

Talk like you’re explaining the issue to your smartest friend who just woke up from a nap—and always tailor your message. Great communication means knowing your audience, cutting the jargon and making your point stick. If they understand it and remember it, you’ve done your job. Bonus points if you made them smile. - [Sakyasingha Dasgupta](https://councils.forbes.com/u/125c6fb5-415f-40c0-822a-aa449ed156d0), [EdgeCortix Inc.](https://www.edgecortix.com/)

#### 7. Connect Through Empathy And Deeper Dialogue

Effective communication is not about saying more, but about connecting better. For tech leaders, three practices elevate impact. 1. Start with what matters to the listener. 2. Address how they feel, not just what they know. People remember tone and intent more than details. 3. The best communicators invite follow-up for deeper dialogue. Lead with clarity, empathy and purpose, not just information. - [Arun Kumar Elengovan](https://councils.forbes.com/u/a730e978-8fd6-45a5-bde1-3521e0ead1e9), [Okta, Inc.](https://www.okta.com/company/)

#### 8. Understand And Speak In Business Language

Understand the business deeply, and speak business language by acquiring domain-specific knowledge. Avoid tech jargon, and don’t try to impress with tech-speak acronyms. Keep your tech knowledge for conversations with the tech team. Improve your executive communication. Also, always remember: “The biggest problem with communication is the assumption that communication has taken place.” - [Rao Tadepalli](https://councils.forbes.com/u/47262da8-bea6-4d68-afa5-e824f6a5264c), Slide Insurance

#### 9. Translate Complex Systems Into Meaningful Outcomes

Great tech leaders are skilled at translating complex systems into clear, meaningful outcomes for people across the business. Whether you’re talking to engineers or execs, always lead with purpose: why it matters, who it impacts and what success looks like. That clarity builds trust and moves things forward faster. - [Damien Niyonshuti](https://councils.forbes.com/u/b7f7aa58-a7ca-445b-a371-e85240821089), Ford Motor Company

#### 10. Educate Without Condescension

Look your audience in the eye, and share your knowledge without sounding condescending. Ensure your audience learns something new and can work more effectively because of the knowledge you’ve passed on to them. Never assume that the information and data you know or the experiences you’ve faced are common knowledge; strive to educate. - [Olga Dogadkina](https://councils.forbes.com/u/aeaff993-9444-4a5b-a11d-2b8295e39add), [Emperia](https://emperiavr.com/)

#### 11. Take A ‘Context First’ Approach

An important communication skill for tech leaders is mastering a “context first” approach. The audience needs to understand the “why” before they can appreciate “how.” Leading with context bridges the knowledge gap and ensures everyone starts from the same foundation. Distilling complex details into clarity through real examples or simple storytelling can create connections and engage the audience. - [Swetha Singiri](https://councils.forbes.com/u/024454ee-8124-4592-811c-d0cd7dd4d780), [Meta](http://about.meta.com/)

#### 12. Validate Concerns

When speaking to non-technical audiences, lead with clarity, patience and empathy. Show confidence without being dismissive, and make space for questions. Validating concerns builds trust and helps bridge the gap between technical expertise and broader understanding. - [Greg Young](https://councils.forbes.com/u/96d58249-a12a-4349-a3be-c4f3d925b46c), [Uniform Law Commission](https://www.uniformlaws.org/)

#### 13. Follow The Three-Step Clarity Rule

Follow grade school writing rules. Be concise and clear—meet your audience where they are. Then, 1. tell them what you’re going to tell them, 2. tell them, and 3. tell them again what you told them. - [Scott Buchholz](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f9e1452b-3b96-4164-a69f-613fef589b18), [Deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/)

#### 14. Don’t Make Any Assumptions

Communication is a code base—make sure you’re using a language everyone understands. Remember that not everyone has the same knowledge base you do. Craft communication to make it easy to internalize. This might mean starting from points you think are obvious or self-evident. They’re not. Tailor your messaging to tell a cohesive story, and don’t make any assumptions about what someone knows. - [Lewis Wynne-Jones](https://councils.forbes.com/u/f8c4f569-abff-483d-b751-1173945ea4c8), [ThinkData Works](https://www.thinkdataworks.com/)

#### 15. Tie Tech Decisions To Business Value

Don’t just translate tech into plain language; translate it into business value. Technical clarity is important, but executive presence comes from tying every decision back to outcomes the business cares about: revenue, efficiency and customer experience. Whether you’re speaking to engineers or in a boardroom, framing your message through that lens builds alignment, trust and influence. - [Asim Bawany](https://councils.forbes.com/u/d5be0649-4d45-41bb-a350-441593f62f02), [Codup](https://codup.co/)

#### 16. Tell A Clear And Simple Story

True communication requires storytelling—bringing your audience on a journey. You must express it through a business lens, as your listeners may not be tech-savvy. If you cannot make your message sound simple, you don’t understand it yourself. Keep it clear and concise. - [Mike Kiersey](https://councils.forbes.com/u/44115b82-3c1b-446c-8060-57ffdb11f1f4), [Workato](https://www.workato.com/)

#### 17. Consider What Your Audience Cares About

Think about your audience. That sounds obvious, but it’s not. Sometimes, competing priorities muddy the waters, and leaders lose sight of the information their audience actually cares about. Consider what information will matter most to them and tailor messages to directly address their needs or concerns. - [Ricardo Madan](https://councils.forbes.com/u/89f437d4-9658-4456-a500-66b14c8ebf89), [TEKsystems](https://www.teksystems.com/)

#### 18. Adopt Narrative Thinking To Engage Audiences

Tech leaders should master narrative thinking—the ability to turn complex concepts into clear, engaging stories. Tailoring messages with context and outcomes helps you connect with diverse audiences, builds trust and ensures the message is understood and remembered. - [Jayapal Reddy Vummadi](https://councils.forbes.com/u/9e6a4aea-5f51-4975-a364-7fb52c7cb38e), [ZF North America](http://www.zf.com/usa_canada/en/home/home.html)

#### 19. Enable Ongoing Collaboration

Prioritize open communication whenever possible. Use tools like Confluence, Wikis, Slack or Teams channels; email threads; and direct messages to maintain ongoing collaboration. Open communication is more visible to all levels of the organization and contributes to the company’s knowledge graph for AI and searches. - [Dustin Johnson](https://councils.forbes.com/u/d1406a7b-edf9-4c01-8fc6-82b44ac961bb), [Seeq](https://www.seeq.com/)

#### 20. Speak In Terms Of Strategy And Consequences

One tip is to stop explaining technology and start framing consequences. The best tech leaders don’t just describe how something will work—they clarify what happens if we do it and what happens if we don’t. That shift moves communication from technical to strategic and gets everyone to actually listen. - [Ramiro Gonzalez Forcada](https://councils.forbes.com/u/17b9e716-4bee-4768-b6f5-7b3b6d0cd88b), [The Flock](https://www.theflock.com/)
